**Michael Bratman** is an American philosopher at Stanford University whose work on shared intention and shared cooperative activity provides the primary alternative account of coordination grounding that the investigation positions itself against.

## Shared cooperative activity

Bratman's influential account grounds coordination in shared intentions and meshing sub-plans. For agents to engage in shared cooperative activity, they must share an intention to act together, have interlocking sub-plans, and be mutually responsive to each other's intentions. Coordination, on this account, is constituted by the sharing of intentions.

## The investigation's response

The V2 research proposal explicitly addresses Bratman: the cases the investigation cares about involve agents who do not necessarily share values nor intentions but who are structurally entangled in ways that make coordination rational from within each agent's own perspective.

The investigation proposes that if [[Structural Interdependence]] can ground coordination independently of shared intention, this would also illuminate why Bratman's conditions succeed when they do: not because shared intentions are the ground of coordination, but because they co-occur with structural features that are. Shared intentions may be correlated with successful coordination without being constitutive of it.

## Why this matters

If Bratman is right that shared intention is constitutive of coordination, then coordination among agents with genuinely incommensurable values (who by definition cannot share intentions about outcomes) is impossible without first resolving the value disagreement. If the investigation is right that structural features can do the grounding work, then coordination without shared intention becomes formally possible. The stakes of this disagreement are high for the investigation's central claim.
