## Supervisor positioning

### Richard Bradley (critical pressure)

Maps the constraints the thesis escapes. His paper proves that even weak assumptions (Outcome Anonymity, State Dominance, Pareto for Equivalent Outcomes) force homogeneity, ambiguity-neutrality, and strong separability. The thesis preserves State Dominance and Pareto while replacing impartial evaluation with situated structural assessment, escaping the homogeneity and ambiguity-neutrality results.

Bradley doesn't need to agree with the thesis. He needs to understand the territory well enough to test whether the escape actually works. A supervisor who built the room is more valuable for testing whether you've genuinely left it.

**Status after reading Bradley S1-4 (2026-04-07):** the rival models Bradley references (cumulative prospect theory, rank-dependent utility, risk-weighted expected utility) all violate the Sure-Thing Principle, allowing context-dependence between states. Real coordination violates strong separability as a feature (AUKUS Pillar I stability depends on Pillar II). These rival models may serve the thesis's formal needs better than Bradley's own framework, but Bradley's diagnostic mapping of the constraints remains essential for positioning.

### Ruth Chang (foundational concept, not supervisor)

Provides the philosophical concept (parity/incommensurability) the thesis both relies on and extends. Chang defines parity for evaluative comparisons between options: comparable but not rankable on a single scale. The thesis extends parity to structural positions in the coordination space: two configurations are comparable (measurable on structural axes) but neither dominates (each better on different axes). This is a substantive claim about Chang's concept applied to a new domain.

**Open question:** does Chang think parity is extendable to structural domains? Or would she resist the move on grounds that parity is specifically about evaluative comparisons between options, not about structural configurations? The thesis needs to argue for the extension, not assume it. The argument: structural positions ARE comparable (both assessable on objective structural grounds) and can exhibit non-dominance (better on different axes), which satisfies Chang's conditions for parity. Whether she'd accept this needs to be tested, ideally by reading her response to structural interpretations of parity in the existing literature.

**Status:** Chang previously expressed interest in a supervision relationship (Oxford, V1 proposal era). The investigation has moved to LSE targeting Alexander and Bradley. Chang remains a key theorist whose framework the thesis builds on. Potential academic correspondent as the work develops.

### Gerald Gaus (adjacent formal frame, not supervisor)

Gaus (2021) is the nearest existing formal treatment of the Open Society under diversity. His §20 "A Self-Organization Model" presents an explicit cardinal-utility agent-based model in which each agent has an inherent moral utility μ_A(R) in [0,10] for candidate rules, weighted by a social reconciliation function w_A(n)(R), with total utility U_A(R) = μ_A(R) × w_A(n)(R) (p. 149, eq. 1). Agents come in four weighting types differing in how much they value reconciliation with others (Quasi-Kantian, Moderately Conditional, Linear Cooperators, Highly Conditional Cooperators). The equilibrium concept is vanilla Nash applied to a coordination-reconciliation game whose "eligible set" filters out rules some agent ranks below no-rule (p. 145 in §19.3). Gaus runs agent-based simulations (Models I-III in §§20.2-20.4) showing convergence dynamics under random, polarised and differential-reference-group distributions.

Gaus is methodologically individualist about preferences. Utilities explicitly do not support interpersonal comparisons (p. 147, footnote 1), but each agent has a complete cardinal ordering over candidate rules within her own perspective. Value diversity enters as heterogeneity in μ profiles and typed weighting functions. His state space is a discrete set of rules; there is no geometric manifold, no metric, no axes to be non-orthogonal.

The v2 and v3 proposals treated Gaus as a placeholder whose framework the thesis would differentiate from by being "a weaker mathematical object" for "partial commensurability." The Claude-assisted scan sharpens this. The v3 placeholder framing (Gaus formalises fitness landscapes within a complex adaptive systems framework) is technically true but misses the target. Gaus does not use fitness landscapes as the central formal object in §20. He uses utility maximisation with weighted reconciliation dynamics on a set of rules. His complex adaptive systems commitment lives at the system level (emergent macro dynamics from micro maximisers), not at the level of the preference structure.

The thesis's wedge against Gaus has five distinct components:

1. **Completeness.** Gaus's agents have complete cardinal orderings over rules within their own perspective. The thesis addresses cases where agents cannot construct complete orderings even for themselves (intrapersonal incompleteness). This is Bradley's "incomplete betterness relations" at the individual level, extended to the collective. Gaus does not engage this.

2. **Object type.** Gaus's state space is a discrete set of rules; the thesis proposes a geometric configuration manifold with structural and evaluative coordinates. Non-orthogonal axes, variable metric structure and continuous deformation become formally tractable only because there are axes to be non-orthogonal; none of these moves is available in a rule set.

3. **Structural features as first-class variables.** Gaus has no analogue of shared risk topology, entanglement density, constraint topology or procedural commitments as coordinates of the space. His "structure" is the reference-group network and the rule set itself. The thesis proposes structural features as a distinct class of variable that does coordination work independent of agents' preferences altogether.

4. **Variable geometry.** Gaus gestures at reflexivity and many-to-one-to-many relations in §29 (p. 238) but treats these as arguments for epistemic humility about policy, not as formal structure. The position-dependent metric tensor is a genuine novelty available to the thesis.

5. **No Bradley or Chang engagement.** Gaus does not engage Bradley 2022 or Chang's parity concept in the sections read. The thesis's Bradley-door claim and its appeal to Chang's parity are not pre-empted.

A stronger critique the thesis can make against Gaus's own formalism: the completeness assumption underlying μ_A(R) ∈ [0,10] is borderline feasible even for idealised individuals (colliding with Chang, Bradley and the broader decision-under-uncertainty literature) and becomes a fiction for collective agents (nations, treaty parties, corporations) whose "preferences" are an aggregation problem rather than a primitive. This means the partial-ordering move is not just a technical weakening for edge cases but an object more faithful to how real coordination happens at both individual and collective scales. This stronger critique needs to be made carefully but is defensible, and it reframes the thesis from "Gaus for edge cases" to "Gaus's object is too strong to describe the phenomenon, and the weaker object is the more honest mathematical representation." The risk in making this critique is that it cuts against Gaus more sharply than the proposal currently implies; the benefit is that it gives the thesis a genuine formal wedge rather than a niche application.

A reviewer's natural counter-punch: "every formal model is an idealisation. Gaus's completeness assumption yields tractable predictions. What does your weaker object buy that compensates for giving up those predictions?" Honest answer: either characterisation results the stronger object cannot give, or a class of cases the stronger object misclassifies. Both are research claims, not proven results, and the proposal should flag them as such.

The targeted reading does not resolve whether this distancing is philosophical or formal. ## Formal object evolution

### Configuration manifold and cone bundles (surfaced 2026-04-10)

During the Gaus targeted reading, the previously loose description of the "configuration space with structural and evaluative axes" sharpened into a more specific mathematical sketch. The base object is a Riemannian configuration manifold M. Points in M are full configurations of a coordination arrangement. Local coordinates are of two types: structural coordinates (observer-independent; shared risk topology, entanglement density, constraint topology, procedural commitment strength) and evaluative coordinates (agent-specific; security valuation, sovereignty commitment, and so on). Structural and evaluative sit in the same manifold as different coordinate types rather than as separate layers.

M carries a position-dependent metric tensor g_{ij}(x). Off-diagonal components encode the non-orthogonality of axes: moving along one axis can pull you along another, with the effect varying with position. The metric may be smooth (classical Riemannian manifold) or stratified with phase boundaries where eigenvalues change sign or vanish (formalising cooperative-to-adversarial regime transitions as bifurcation points). This is where the variable-geometry insight from the Alexander Ch. 29 reading lives formally.

Over M sits a bundle of dominance cones. At each point x, each agent i has a cone C_i(x) ⊂ T_x M picking out tangent directions that i regards as improvements from their situated position. Unlike a half-space derived from a scalar utility gradient, a dominance cone can be narrower than a half-space, and its complement can include a parity region where i can neither endorse nor reject the move. Each agent's cone assignment x ↦ C_i(x) is a section of the cone bundle. Smooth sections correspond to agents whose evaluative stance deforms continuously; discontinuous sections are agents with sharp thresholds (red lines, commitments that switch on at a boundary).

Structural equilibrium is the position x* from which no agent can reach a cone-endorsed destination via an available move. The "available" half of the condition is feasibility under the metric and coalition structure; the "cone-endorsed" half is the partial-ordering analogue of "improves utility." Nash equilibrium on a finite game is recovered as the degenerate limit: complete orderings collapse cones to half-spaces, discrete strategy sets collapse the manifold to a finite set, and the existence result is Kakutani.

**Status:** this sketch is not proven. It is held as a working direction with explicit gaps around existence of structural equilibria in general, computational tractability, and model fit to real coordination problems. The sketch is held back from the written proposal and reserved for face-to-face discussion with Alexander, for two reasons. First, the author's mathematical refresh is in progress; the formal stack spans territory that is not on a standard engineering physics curriculum. Second, the sketch reads more strongly as gesturing-with-substance when the invitation to discuss comes from the supervision conversation rather than from the written document. The proposal's Methodology section gestures at the direction without committing to the specific machinery.

### Structural equilibrium

Originally framed as "not Nash." Refined to: Nash generalised to partial orderings over structural position-vectors. The formal novelty is the space (configuration space, not strategy space) and the mathematical object (partial ordering, not cardinal utility), not the equilibrium concept (no-unilateral-deviation inherited from Nash). The thesis question: does this weaker object produce results the stronger one can't?

**Further refinement (2026-04-10):** after the Gaus targeted reading and the mathematical sketch above, structural equilibrium has a more specific formal home. It is the no-reachable-dominating-position condition on a Riemannian configuration manifold with a cone bundle structure. Nash on a finite strategy space with cardinal utility is the degenerate limit where cones collapse to half-spaces and the manifold collapses to a discrete set. The generalisation is therefore strictly conservative: anything Nash proves for classical coordination games holds in the limit. The open research question is whether the weaker object yields non-trivial stability results in conditions the classical framework excludes by assumption (intrapersonal incompleteness, non-orthogonal axes, variable metric across regimes).

### Structural axes

Candidate features (risk exposure, entanglement density, constraint burden, procedural commitment strength) are empirically motivated from coordination-without-convergence cases, not axiomatically derived. The set is open. The formal programme tests sufficiency and necessity. The axes are not claimed as exhaustive.

**Refinement (2026-04-10):** in the geometric sketch, structural and evaluative commitments sit in the same manifold as different coordinate types. Structural coordinates (risk topology, entanglement density, constraint topology, procedural commitments) are observer-independent: any agent looking at the arrangement agrees on the measurement. Evaluative coordinates (security valuation, sovereignty commitment, etc.) are agent-specific: different agents occupy different positions on them. The L1/L2 distinction therefore maps onto "which coordinates are observer-independent," not onto a layered architecture. The three moves the thesis makes (partial orderings within an agent, partial orderings across agents, structural features as first-class objects) travel as a package, and the third move is the core of which the first two are parts. The earlier framing of "structural features vs evaluative commitments" as distinct categories is preserved formally by the coordinate-type distinction, rather than by a separate layering.

### Parity in the structural domain

Two configurations exhibit parity when each agent can assess both on structural grounds (objective, measurable) but neither dominates (better on different axes). Subjectivity enters in weighting, not measurement. Parity emerges from the interaction between objective structure and subjective weighting. Grounds Chang's concept without requiring Bradley's impartial evaluator.

**Refinement (2026-04-10):** in the geometric sketch, parity has a formal home as a region of the tangent space at each point. At position x, the parity region for agent i is the subset of T_x M that lies outside both i's dominance cone C_i(x) and its reflection (the cone of rejected directions). Moves into the parity region are those i can neither endorse nor reject. This preserves Chang's key property that parity is a substantive subset of possible comparisons, not a singleton boundary case between "better" and "worse." The formal adequacy of this picture against Chang's own definition has not been tested and is an open question, but the geometric home is clean and the contrast with scalar utility is sharp: a utility gradient gives you a half-space of "up" directions and no parity region, while a dominance cone can have a parity region that is a substantive fraction of the tangent space.

### Objective vs subjective structure (from Tension 6)

Structural features are objective properties of a configuration. How agents perceive and weight them is perspective-dependent. Layer 1 (objective) is where stability is defined. Layer 2 (subjective) is where coordination behaviour comes from. The thesis contribution is Layer 1. The divergence between layers is itself informative.

**Refinement (2026-04-10):** the L1/L2 split maps cleanly onto the metric/cone distinction in the geometric sketch. The metric g_{ij}(x) is the observer-independent structure at L1 (real facts about costs, distances and axis interaction). The cone bundle C_i(x) is the agent-specific evaluation at L2 (situated partial orderings over possible moves). These are logically independent structures at the base level, though they interact in the equilibrium condition. This is a sharper version of the original L1/L2 claim and gives both layers specific formal homes in recognised mathematical traditions: differential geometry for the metric, convex analysis and cone theory for the cones. It also answers a standing objection: if structural fitness is observer-independent, then the metric is the formal object that "observer-independent structure" names.

### Mathematical stack (surfaced 2026-04-10)

The formal object under development sits at the intersection of three mathematical traditions, with supporting layers from two more. None of the three is a core engineering physics subject, so the formalism requires a refresh programme (see reading log, Mathematical Foundations for the Formal Object section).

1. **Differential geometry.** Configuration manifold M with position-dependent metric tensor g_{ij}(x). Standard Riemannian geometry handles the smooth case; stratified manifolds with phase boundaries handle the case where regime transitions are formal singularities. Entry references: Schutz, Lee, Boumal.

2. **Convex analysis and cone theory.** Dominance cones as the formal home of partial orderings. Pointed cones, dual cones, generalised inequalities, polyhedral representations. Entry references: Boyd & Vandenberghe, Ehrgott, Davey & Priestley for the order-theoretic background, Rockafellar for rigour where needed.

3. **Vector-valued game theory.** Structural equilibrium generalising Nash to cone orderings. Shapley 1959 is the origin point; modern formulations in Corley, Sawaragi et al. Cooperative game theory (Peleg & Sudhölter) for the coalition-reachability half of the equilibrium condition.

Supporting layers:

4. **Dynamical systems.** Time evolution, bifurcations at regime transitions. Strogatz for the introduction; Hofbauer & Sigmund for evolutionary dynamics specifically, which also ties the thesis to Alexander's tradition directly.

5. **Empirical operationalisation.** What counts as a structural feature in a real coordination problem. No single textbook; domain-specific via the thesis's own case studies.

**Methodological commitment:** computational exploration will supplement analytical work throughout. Python scientific stack tools (NumPy, SciPy, SymPy, CVXPY, geomstats, pymanopt) cover the computational needs. Gaus's §20 precedent (agent-based simulations as the central empirical move) confirms computational methods are in-tradition for this field, and Alexander's broader field (evolutionary game theory) is heavily computational. This commitment should be stated explicitly in the proposal's Methodology section, with a paragraph noting that definitions and existence theorems will be developed on paper and numerical exploration will be used to build intuition, test conjectures and identify interesting structure before commitment to proofs. A minimal Python implementation of the configuration manifold with polyhedral cones and Euclidean metric is plausible as a pre-MPhil artefact, though the definitions must be tight before code is worth writing.

### Tiered defensibility of structural features (surfaced 2026-04-11)

After the literature exploration on the structure/preferences distinction (2026-04-10) raised the concern that strong observer-independence claims for structural features would not survive contact with Putnam, the working position retreated to a conditional version along Sen's positional-objectivity lines: structural features are observer-independent given that agents share a vocabulary for what counts as a downside or an entanglement. The author then observed (2026-04-11) that the conditional retreat is too uniform: shared risk topology admits a stronger claim at the catastrophic-to-humanity level than the other structural features do, because at that level the categorisation is robustly shared across virtually any agent who values their own and their kind's continued existence. This sharpened the position into a tiered account that gives the strongest claim where it is genuinely defensible without overgeneralising.

**The tier structure**:

**Tier 1: civilisation-ending threats.** Nuclear war between major powers, runaway misaligned AI, climate collapse to ice-age conditions, engineered pandemic with very high fatality rates, asteroid impact above a certain mass. Categorisation at this tier is robustly observer-independent in the sense that matters: virtually any agent who values their own and their kind's continued existence will recognise these as downsides. The value-loading collapses to "you should not want humanity to go extinct," which is about as close to a universal premise as ethical claims get. Putnam's "but who decides what counts as a downside" attack has the least bite at this tier because the categorisation does not depend on which agent's values you adopt. Academic precedent for the Tier 1 claim is well established in the existential risk research literature: Bostrom (2002, 2013), Ord (2020), the (now-closed) Future of Humanity Institute at Oxford and the Centre for the Study of Existential Risk at Cambridge.

**Tier 2: serious-but-not-existential threats.** Regional war that does not escalate, financial system collapse, large-scale forced migration, food system failure in one region, breakdown of a critical alliance. Categorisation here is broadly shared across reasonable agents but some genuine disagreement creeps in. AUKUS sits at this tier: the three parties broadly agree on what counts as catastrophic in the Indo-Pacific even if they would rank positive outcomes differently. The conditional-observer-independence framing (Sen's positional objectivity) is the right form for Tier 2 claims. This is the case study domain for the thesis.

**Tier 3: contested cases.** Immigration policy, cultural change, economic distribution, regulatory burdens. Heavily value-laden. The thesis explicitly does not claim observer-independence at this tier and notes it as a boundary condition.

**Asymmetric application across structural features**:

The tier structure does not apply uniformly to all four candidate structural features. Each has its own defensibility profile.

- **Shared risk topology** is the strongest case for a partial Tier 1 claim. Existential threats provide a robust observer-independent benchmark. Tier 2 cases (regional risks shared by alliance partners) admit the conditional version. Tier 3 cases (contested cultural risks) are out of scope.
- **Constraint topology** has a partial Tier 1 case at the physical-constraint level (fundamental physics, hard biological limits), but legal and institutional constraints are Tier 2 at best (depend on which institutional regime is accepted) and political constraints are Tier 3.
- **Entanglement density** does not have a meaningful Tier 1 case. Whose dependencies count, and how heavily different kinds of dependency are weighted, varies across agents. The conditional retreat is essential throughout.
- **Procedural commitments** are almost entirely value-laden. Which procedures count as binding depends on which institutional traditions are accepted. The conditional retreat is essential throughout.

**Practical implication for the proposal**:

The Methodology section can introduce the four candidate structural features with explicit acknowledgement of their differing defensibility profiles. The strongest version of the observer-independence claim is reserved for shared risk topology at the catastrophic tier; the conditional version (Sen's positional objectivity) carries the case study domain (AUKUS at Tier 2); Tier 3 is acknowledged as out of scope. Citing Bostrom 2013 or Ord 2020 grounds the Tier 1 claim in recognised philosophical territory. This is more honest than either a flat strong claim or a flat weak one because it tracks the actual structure of the phenomenon.

**Two cautions**:

1. The Tier 1 success at the catastrophic tier should not tempt overclaiming at Tier 2. AUKUS does not run on existential risk; it runs on serious-but-not-extinction-level regional security risks. The conditional retreat is the right form for the case study domain even after the Tier 1 strengthening.

2. The thesis should cite the academic existential risk literature without engaging the broader Effective Altruism or longtermism political debates. The academic work is solid; the surrounding movement is contested. The proposal stays in academic territory.

### Predictive models as a missing element (surfaced 2026-04-11)

Surfaced from the user's reading of Gaus §15.1. Gaus's six-element perspective model includes element V, predictive models that map options to social worlds. The thesis's current formal sketch (configuration manifold with metric and cone bundle) has no element V analogue. Agents in the manifold need some way to predict where moves lead, but the metric tensor only tells the agent the cost or distance of a move; it does not model the agent's prediction of what configuration the move arrives at, what consequences follow, how the system responds. Different agents may have different predictive models of the same arrangement, and those predictive models are themselves a source of diversity that is neither metric (structural) nor cone (evaluative) in the current sketch.

The user (2026-04-11) has confirmed that element V is going to be a key part of the thesis but the construction is not yet worked out. Open question for future development: how should predictive models be represented formally in the configuration manifold framework? Candidate moves include:

1. **Per-agent dynamical systems**: each agent carries a vector field on the manifold representing their model of how configurations evolve. Agents can have different vector fields over the same manifold.
2. **Causal model bundles**: a bundle structure where each fibre at point x is the set of plausible next-positions according to that agent's causal model. This is structurally similar to the cone bundle but encodes "what I expect to happen" rather than "what I would prefer".
3. **Learned predictive models with shared updating**: agents update their predictive models based on observed outcomes, possibly converging on shared models in the limit but starting with substantial divergence. Connects to Bayesian belief revision and to evolutionary dynamics on prediction.

None of these is committed yet. The honest current state is that element V is a flagged refinement, not a worked formal object. It will need substantial development during the MPhil year, possibly drawing on dynamical systems theory (Strogatz, already in the reading log) and on the cognitive theory of categorisation (Hayek 1952, Page 2007, North 2005, recently added).

The most important consequence for the proposal: the Methodology section should acknowledge that the formal object under development has at least three component types (structural coordinates with metric, evaluative cones, predictive models), not the current two (structural and evaluative). This is a more honest description of the formal programme and avoids the trap of overclaiming completeness for the current sketch.

The thesis aims to escape the restrictive results Bradley (2022) shows are forced by impartiality. The escape is a working proposal, not a proven result.

**Formal mechanism (how it works):**
2. Structural equilibrium is Nash generalised to partial orderings over position-vectors in a Riemannian configuration manifold with agent-specific dominance cones as fibres.
