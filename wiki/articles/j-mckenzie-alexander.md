**J. McKenzie Alexander** (also published as Jason McKenzie Alexander) is Professor of Philosophy in the Department of Philosophy, Logic and Scientific Method at the London School of Economics and Political Science.[^lse-profile] His research spans [[Evolutionary Game Theory]], the evolution of morality and social norms, computational philosophy, agent-based modelling and the philosophy of society.[^lse-profile]

## Education

Alexander holds a B.S. in Mathematics from Lewis & Clark College (1995), an M.A. in Philosophy from the University of California, Irvine (1998) and a Ph.D. in Philosophy from the University of California, Irvine (2000).[^cv] His doctoral dissertation, *The Evolution of Distributive Justice*, was completed in the Logic and Philosophy of Science programme at UC Irvine, where he worked alongside Brian Skyrms on evolutionary game-theoretic explanations of justice norms.[^cv][^diss] His areas of specialisation are decision and game theory and the philosophy of social science; his areas of competence are moral and political philosophy and logic.[^cv]

## Career

Alexander was a Visiting Assistant Professor at the University of California, San Diego (2000--2001) before joining LSE as Lecturer in Philosophy in 2001.[^cv] He was promoted to Senior Lecturer (2005--2006), Reader (2006--2013) and Professor (2013--present).[^cv] He was a visiting scholar at Carnegie Mellon University in the autumn of 2003.[^home]

At LSE he served as Head of the Department of Philosophy, Logic and Scientific Method (2018--2021) and as Academic Governor on the LSE Council and Court of Governors (2012--2018).[^cv][^lse-profile] He has coordinated the BSc and MSc programmes in Philosophy, Logic and Scientific Method across multiple periods from 2001 to 2021.[^cv]

He served as Associate Editor of *Philosophy of Science* (2009--2013) and has remained on the journal's editorial board since 2013.[^cv]

## Research

Alexander's research programme began with evolutionary game theory applied to the emergence of moral and social norms and has expanded into decision theory, formal epistemology and the philosophy of society.[^lse-profile]

### Evolution of morality on networks

His first book, *The Structural Evolution of Morality* (Cambridge UP, 2007), provided a foundational treatment of how moral behaviour, including fairness, trust, cooperation and retaliation, can be explained through evolutionary game theory operating on social networks.[^sem-2007] The book drew on bounded rationality and computational models of social networks to show that population structure, not just strategic content, shapes which norms emerge and persist. This structural emphasis connects to the broader finding by Nowak and May (1992) that spatial topology alone produces qualitatively different cooperation outcomes even when the underlying game is fixed.[^sem-2007]

### Evolutionary game theory

His *Evolutionary Game Theory* (Cambridge UP, Elements in Decision Theory and Philosophy, 2023) is a concise treatment of the field covering static stability concepts, replicator and other population dynamics, local interaction models and open problems.[^egt-2023] His encyclopedia article "Evolutionary Game Theory" in the *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* (first published 2002, substantially revised 2009 and 2021) has been viewed over 100,000 times and remains the standard introductory survey of the field.[^sep-2021][^lse-profile]

### Signalling, cooperation and social learning

A recurring thread in Alexander's journal articles examines how cooperation and communication emerge in structured populations of boundedly rational agents. "Bargaining with Neighbors: Is Justice Contagious?" (with Brian Skyrms, *The Journal of Philosophy*, 1999) explored spatial evolutionary dynamics in bargaining games.[^skyrms-1999] "Cheap Talk, Reinforcement Learning, and the Emergence of Cooperation" (*Philosophy of Science* 82(5), 2015) showed that cheap talk can sustain cooperation in a social network model when agents discount the past, even without formal institutions.[^cheap-talk] "Learning to Signal in a Dynamic World" (*British Journal for the Philosophy of Science* 65(4), 2014) and "Inventing New Signals" (with Brian Skyrms and Sandy L. Zabell, *Dynamic Games and Applications* 2(1), 2012) extended the analysis to signalling games.[^signal-2014][^inventing]

### Philosophy of science and epistemology

"Preferential Attachment and the Search for Successful Theories" (*Philosophy of Science* 80(5), 2013) modelled theory selection by boundedly rational agents as a variable-arm bandit problem, showing that social learning via preferential attachment outperforms individual reinforcement learning.[^pref-attach] "Epistemic Landscapes, Optimal Search and the Division of Cognitive Labor" (with Johannes Himmelreich and Christopher Thompson, *Philosophy of Science* 82(3), 2015) applied landscape models to the distribution of research effort.[^epistemic]

### Open society and political philosophy

His most recent book, *The Open Society as an Enemy: A Critique of How Free Societies Turned Against Themselves* (LSE Press, 2024, ISBN 978-1-911712-26-8), argues that populist agendas on both left and right have eroded the values underpinning liberal democracies and that a new defence of the open society is required.[^ose-2024] The book is published open-access.[^ose-2024]

## Relevance to the investigation

Alexander is one of two target supervisors for the LSE MPhil/PhD application (alongside [[Richard Bradley]]). His work is directly relevant to sub-question 2 of the research proposal: under what conditions do coordination equilibria grounded in [[Structural Interdependence]] resist defection, capture and decay?

### Complementary not competing

The source-note analysis of Alexander's SEP entry identifies a productive framing for the relationship between his evolutionary game-theoretic programme and the investigation's structural hypothesis.[^source-notes] Alexander's models explain how cooperative norms emerge and stabilise across populations over evolutionary time. The investigation asks a different question: what grounds coordination in the specific case, before the evolutionary dynamics have had time to operate.[^source-notes]

The two accounts are complementary rather than competing. Alexander explains the long run (population-level norm evolution through differential replication); the investigation explains the acute case (a small number of specific actors making situated judgements under uncertainty, with genuinely incommensurable values, right now, once).[^source-notes] A nation deciding whether to cooperate on [[AUKUS]] Pillar II is not replicating differentially across a population. It is a specific set of actors facing a specific structural configuration.

### The payoff-structure boundary

Alexander's entire formal apparatus, from the static ESS concepts of Maynard Smith through replicator dynamics to local interaction models, requires a constructible payoff structure: a shared matrix V that all agents recognise.[^source-notes] When agents hold values that are genuinely incomparable in [[Ruth Chang]]'s sense, the starting conditions for any game-theoretic model are absent. This is not a limitation that can be patched; it is a boundary condition of the framework itself.[^source-notes]

The investigation's key question for Alexander's framework: can his evolutionary stability analysis be extended to agents whose payoffs are genuinely incomparable? If [[Coordination Without Convergence]] can be grounded in structural features (shared risk ordering, constraint hardness, entanglement density), what does evolutionary stability look like in that regime?[^source-notes]

### The Nowak and May bridge

Alexander's work inherits the Nowak and May (1992) result that spatial structure alone produces qualitatively different cooperation outcomes even when the underlying game is fixed.[^source-notes] The investigation's thesis framing pushes this further: "Nowak and May demonstrated that structure matters even when the game is fixed. The investigation asks what happens when structure is all you have."[^source-notes]
