# Research Proposal: Coordination Across Difference: Post-Ideological Frameworks for Existential Governance

**Applicant:** Samuel Norton
**Degree:** DPhil in Philosophy, University of Oxford

## The Problem

Current political systems evolved for industrial scarcity and nation-state competition. Contemporary conditions demand global cooperation on existential interdependence (climate, biosecurity, transformative AI, economic systems, geopolitical relations) at speeds that outpace 19th-century institutional frameworks. Traditional left-right philosophy provides inadequate tools: stakes are existential, timescales compressed, actors multi-stakeholder (states, corporations, civil society, AI systems), and ideological heuristics break down under deep uncertainty (Knightian uncertainty). These systems exhibit characteristic failure modes: markets defer long-term risks for quarterly returns, democratic institutions succumb to capture dynamics, nation-states compete rather than cooperate, technocracy lacks legitimacy. Current approaches assume either ideological convergence (technocratic solutions) or paralytic pluralism (endless negotiation). Neither scales to existential coordination challenges. This is not inevitable decline. The same species that built these now-inadequate frameworks can build better ones - but only if we understand coordination as constitutive of collective agency rather than merely instrumental to shared goals. How do we coordinate collective decision-making when inherited frameworks for practical reasoning, value conflict resolution and institutional design assume conditions that no longer hold?

## Research Questions

**Central Question:** How do we build coordination frameworks that enable collective action across scales - from families and communities through organisations and institutions to nations and planetary governance - without requiring homogeneity? Whether the divisions are ideological (capitalism/socialism, liberal democracy/authoritarianism, nation-state sovereignty/multilateralism), cultural, institutional or simply different stakeholder priorities, can we derive coordination principles analogous to a compass - providing direction whilst allowing each actor to chart their own course? Can coordination theory address both theoretical inadequacy and practical pathologies simultaneously?

These coordination patterns manifest across all scales of collective action, but this research focuses on their most consequential manifestations: institutional and planetary coordination under existential constraints. Specific lines of inquiry include:

### Practical Reason Beyond Ideological Sorting

When facing hard choices about existential risks, how should collectives reason without defaulting to tribal heuristics or the observed failure modes of their inherited frameworks? What philosophical principles enable coordination when actors hold incommensurable values but share existential stakes?

### Institutional Design for Pluralistic Unity

What architecture enables multi-stakeholder coordination that neither requires ideological homogeneity nor succumbs to the failure modes of existing systems such as regulatory capture, short-term profit maximisation or bureaucratic paralysis? Can we derive coordination mechanisms from first principles that are robust to both theoretical inadequacy and practical tendencies towards will to power and corruption?

### Coordination Across Human and Non-Human Actors

As coordination increasingly involves non-human actors (AI systems, automated markets, algorithmic governance), how do we preserve meaningful human agency and democratic legitimacy whilst leveraging computational capabilities? How do we avoid both technocratic override of human judgement and the paralysis that comes from treating every actor as requiring equal voice?

## Why This Matters

We face existential coordination challenges such as climate tipping points, transformative AI, biological risks, economic instability, with institutional architectures whose assumptions predate electrification, let alone computation. These exhibit predictable failure modes: market mechanisms systematically externalise catastrophic tail risks onto future generations - climate adaptation, pandemic preparedness, AI safety investments all exhibit this pattern. Democratic processes cannot iterate at technological speed, nation-states compete rather than coordinate on shared threats. This leaves us oscillating between imposed solutions that lack buy-in and negotiated processes that move slower than the risks they address. Without coordination theory grounded in contemporary conditions, we face either technocratic authoritarianism or gridlock as existential risks materialise.

This research is grounded in a substantive conviction: that human beings are capable of genuine collective agency across difference, and that this capacity is currently squandered by frameworks assuming coordination requires either homogeneity or aligned self-interest. Beyond avoiding catastrophe, the question is whether we can become—collectively—the kind of species that chooses coordination as an expression of what we are, rather than a constraint imposed by circumstance.

## Why Existing Work Doesn't Achieve this Synthesis

Chang's work on hard choices analyses individual practical reason under value incommensurability but doesn't address institutional design for collective hard choices.

Ostrom's institutional design theory assumes bounded communities with repeated interactions, stable resource dynamics, and iterative learning from failure - conditions absent from existential coordination where defection may be irreversible and actors lack shared community bonds. Deliberative democracy theory (Habermas, Rawls, Gutmann & Thompson) addresses legitimacy but assumes conditions that don't hold for existential coordination: unlimited time for deliberation, human-only actors, slow-changing conditions. Game-theoretic mechanism design (Hurwicz, Maskin) optimises for specific objectives but doesn't address when objectives are incommensurable or how to preserve democratic legitimacy. AI governance philosophy (Bostrom, Russell, Dafoe) addresses technical alignment but doesn't adequately synthesise with institutional legitimacy theory - assuming either technocratic solutions or human override without addressing how to coordinate across human and AI actors when objectives are genuinely incommensurable.

Each addresses part of the puzzle. None synthesise across individual hard choices, institutional design, democratic legitimacy and strategic modelling under the conditions existential coordination actually faces: compressed timescales, AI actors, genuine value pluralism and risk of irreversible consequences.

## Methodological Approach

This research is grounded in analytical practical philosophy, addressing questions of collective practical reason, value conflict, and institutional legitimacy through rigorous conceptual analysis.

The central philosophical methodology draws on contemporary work in practical reason and hard choices - particularly frameworks for decision-making under value incommensurability and deep uncertainty. When inherited ideological categories break down, what normative resources remain for collective action? This question requires careful analysis of the structure of practical reasoning itself: whether coordination principles can be derived that don't presuppose substantive value agreement, and what conditions must obtain for such principles to generate legitimate collective decisions. Institutional design questions are approached through political philosophy rather than policy analysis: what normative architecture enables coordination across difference while preserving meaningful agency? This engages Ostrom's work on collective action, Buchanan's constitutional political economy, and contemporary debates on democratic legitimacy under conditions of deep pluralism. Empirical material from international coordination efforts (climate negotiations, AI governance initiatives, defence cooperation) serves not as independent methodology but as philosophical case study, revealing the practical inadequacy of existing frameworks and testing the applicability of proposed alternatives. Similarly, formal methods from game theory and complexity science inform philosophical claims rather than substitute for them: computational modelling can illuminate coordination dynamics, but the normative evaluation of coordination mechanisms remains philosophical work.

## Expected Contribution

This research develops a rigorous framework for post-ideological coordination theory grounded in practical philosophy. Rather than presupposing shared ideological commitments, it derives coordination principles from formal analysis of strategic structure, value incommensurability, and institutional design.

The framework generates specific, testable mechanisms for existential coordination challenges. For instance, global financial governance currently oscillates between harmonised regulation, requiring convergence on economic philosophy that sovereign nations resist, and regulatory arbitrage where capital exploits jurisdictional gaps. The framework would derive coordination protocols from shared systemic risk containment: actors needn't agree on optimal economic policy to coordinate on preventing cascading failures. I theorise that similar mechanisms could be applied to AI governance, climate coordination, biosecurity and geopolitical relations amongst other areas, with clear success criteria that actual institutions can test how philosophical analysis, informed by complexity science and systems theory, can generate defensible coordination claims - addressing both theoretical adequacy and practical implementation without collapsing into either pure abstraction or atheoretical policy analysis.

I have begun developing the Unity Compass framework as a Github repository for early-stage infrastructure regarding this research, with the intention of making it publicly accessible as the work matures. The DPhil would provide the philosophical rigour this work requires to survive academic scrutiny whilst maintaining practical relevance. Ultimately, this research tests a conviction: that humanity can become the kind of species that is capable of coordination across genuine difference - not despite our plurality, but through it. The stakes have the potential to be not merely institutional, but civilisational.
