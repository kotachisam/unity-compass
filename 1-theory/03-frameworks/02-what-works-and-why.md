# What Works and Why

The [previous document](01-coordination-taxonomy.md) mapped the strategic landscape: what kinds of coordination problems exist, what structures they exhibit and where the diagnostic errors occur. This document asks the next question: when coordination across genuine difference actually succeeds, what enabled it?

The [landscape analysis](../02-landscape/02-where-it-works.md) identified six recurring success factors. This document takes those factors and asks what they mean for the kinds of coordination this investigation cares about. The question is not just "what worked?" but "what transfers, what doesn't and what principles can be extracted for the hardest coordination challenges?"

The answer requires honesty. Some of the best-documented coordination successes depended on conditions that won't hold at the hardest scales. Knowing where the evidence is strong and where the extrapolation begins is itself a form of rigour.

## Ostrom's Design Principles: What Transfers and What Doesn't

Elinor Ostrom's work on commons governance remains the most empirically grounded coordination research in existence. Across hundreds of cases spanning fisheries, irrigation systems, forests and grazing lands, she identified eight design principles that characterised successful self-governance of shared resources.[^1]

The principles themselves are worth stating plainly: clearly defined group boundaries and resource limits. Rules adapted to local conditions rather than imposed from above. Collective choice arrangements giving most participants a voice in rule-making. Active monitoring by parties accountable to the community. Graduated sanctions that escalate proportionally. Low-cost mechanisms for dispute resolution. Recognition by external authorities of the community's right to self-organise. And for larger systems, nested governance with coordination across levels.

These are empirical findings, extracted from communities that successfully managed shared resources over decades and sometimes centuries. These are not abstract ideals. They are battle-tested. They work.

The question is where, and in which ways, they stop working.

Ostrom's conditions are demanding. Her cases involve bounded communities where participants know each other, interact repeatedly, can observe each other's behaviour, share a resource whose dynamics are relatively stable and can learn iteratively from failure. These conditions create exactly the environment where Axelrod's shadow of the future operates: defection is visible, sanctions are credible, reputation matters and the game continues long enough for cooperation to emerge.

High-stakes coordination at scale lacks nearly all of these conditions. The "community" ranges from interpersonal to global, rather than bounded. Interactions are often one-shot or infrequent. Behaviour is difficult to monitor across jurisdictions. The resource dynamics (climate systems, AI capability trajectories, pandemic spread) are nonlinear and poorly understood. And the most consequential failures may be irreversible: you cannot iterate your way out of a crossed tipping point.

This by no means implies that Ostrom's work is irrelevant. It makes the transfer question precise. What generalises from her findings and what depends on conditions that high-stakes coordination can't provide?

<summary>

### What Transfers

</summary>

**Graduated sanctions.** The principle that rule violations should be met with proportional responses rather than binary punishment is robust across contexts. It works in Ostrom's fishing villages because it maintains the relationship while correcting the behaviour. The same logic applies in international coordination: the WTO's dispute settlement mechanism, whatever its flaws, is built on proportional response rather than nuclear options. A coordination framework for existential challenges needs analogous graduated mechanisms: responses that correct without destroying the coordination architecture itself.

**Collective choice arrangements.** Ostrom found that communities where participants had a voice in making the rules governed resources better than those where rules were imposed externally. The principle is about legitimacy through participation, and it generalises. The Irish Citizens' Assembly succeeded partly because randomly selected citizens developed the rules through structured deliberation rather than receiving them from politicians.[^2] Climate negotiations that exclude affected nations produce agreements that collapse on implementation. The principle holds: the people affected by coordination rules must have meaningful input into their design.

**Nested governance.** For larger systems, Ostrom found that governance needed to be layered: local rules within regional frameworks within broader institutional contexts, each level handling what it was best placed to handle. This is directly applicable to multi-scale coordination challenges. AI governance, for instance, plausibly requires local experimentation nested within national regulation nested within international norms. The principle is that no single level of governance can handle coordination across all contexts, and that levels must be able to communicate and adjust.

**Monitoring and transparency.** Successful commons governance required that participants could observe whether others were complying. The mechanism varied (it might be a rotating watch system or a shared information resource), but the principle was consistent: cooperation is sustainable only when compliance is visible. The Montreal Protocol's success depended partly on atmospheric monitoring that made compliance (and violation) scientifically observable.[^3] Any coordination framework for existential challenges must build in mechanisms that make compliance legible.

</details>

<details>
<summary>

### What Doesn't Transfer

</summary>

**Boundedness.** Ostrom's communities had clear membership. Who was in the group and who wasn't was known. Global coordination lacks this: the "community" managing climate risk or AI development is, in principle, everyone, and in practice, a shifting coalition of states, corporations, civil society organisations and (increasingly) AI systems themselves. You cannot define group boundaries for a problem that affects the entire species.

**Repeated interaction with stable partners.** The engine of cooperation in Ostrom's cases was knowing you'd deal with the same people again. In international coordination, the "partners" are administrations that change, corporations that restructure and institutions that evolve. The US climate delegation under one administration may reverse the commitments of the previous one. This is the stable identity assumption that [Axelrod's framework](01-coordination-taxonomy.md) also depends on, and it breaks at precisely the scales where coordination matters most.

**Iterative learning from failure.** Ostrom's communities could try something, observe the results, adjust and try again. The resource dynamics were slow enough and forgiving enough to permit experimentation. High-stakes coordination faces a different reality: some failures are irreversible. You cannot experiment with planetary tipping points. You cannot run a controlled trial on AI governance at scale. The feedback loops that enabled Ostrom's communities to self-correct are absent for the problems this investigation cares about most.

**Stable resource dynamics.** Fisheries and forests change, but their dynamics are broadly understood and relatively predictable. Climate systems, AI capability curves and pandemic spread are characterised by nonlinearity, fat tails and deep uncertainty. The models that would enable Ostrom-style adaptive management don't yet exist for these domains, and may never exist in the form her framework requires.

</details>

The transfer analysis produces a clear picture. Ostrom's structural principles (how to design coordination mechanisms) generalise more readily than her enabling conditions (the environment those mechanisms require). The challenge for this investigation is to understand what preserves the structural insights while operating without the enabling conditions Ostrom's cases depended on.

## Cases That Demonstrate Coordination Without Convergence

The [case studies](../02-landscape/case-studies/) in the landscape directory provide detailed examinations. What follows extracts the principles that matter for building something new, organised by what each case reveals about the conditions under which coordination succeeds without requiring value convergence.

<details>
<summary>

### The Montreal Protocol: Why It Worked When Climate Didn't

</summary>

The Montreal Protocol is the strongest available evidence that planetary-scale coordination across genuine difference is possible. 197 nations coordinated to phase out ozone-depleting substances, and the ozone layer is measurably recovering. No comparable climate agreement has achieved equivalent results.

The standard explanation is that ozone was "easier" than climate. Fewer chemicals, identifiable manufacturers, available substitutes. This is true but insufficient. What made the Montreal Protocol work maps directly onto the [taxonomy](01-coordination-taxonomy.md):

The problem structure was an assurance game rather than a pure social dilemma. Nations weren't primarily tempted to free-ride on others' reductions. They were uncertain whether others would follow through. The Protocol's phased implementation with built-in review periods directly addressed this: each phase built confidence for the next. The Multilateral Fund addressed asymmetric costs, removing the chicken dynamic that could have emerged between developed and developing nations. And the science was clear enough to prevent the problem from being reframed as value incommensurability: nobody was arguing that ozone depletion was a valid trade-off against other values.

Climate coordination faces a fundamentally different strategic structure. The temptation to free-ride is stronger (energy is more economically central than refrigerants). The costs are more diffuse and asymmetric. The science, while clear on the direction, involves longer timescales and more complex feedback loops. And the problem does involve genuine value incommensurability: nations hold different views on the relative weight of economic development, environmental preservation and intergenerational justice.

The transferable insight: successful planetary coordination requires correctly diagnosing the strategic structure and designing mechanisms that match it. The Montreal Protocol didn't succeed because ozone was simple. It succeeded because the coordination mechanism was precisely calibrated to the assurance-game structure of the problem. Climate coordination has consistently applied mechanisms designed for social dilemmas (binding targets, naming and shaming) to a problem that increasingly looks like value incommensurability. The diagnosis drives the treatment.

</details>

### Open-Source Governance: Protocol Over Ideology

</summary>

Open-source software development is the most accessible modern proof of concept for coordination without convergence. The Linux kernel has received contributions from over 20,000 developers representing corporations, governments, universities and independent hobbyists. Their motivations are wildly diverse: commercial advantage, ideological commitment, personal learning, reputation building. They coordinate through shared protocol, not shared worldview.

The mechanism is worth examining precisely. Git (the version control system) makes every contribution visible, every decision reversible and every fork possible. Pull requests create a structured process for proposing changes. Maintainers provide quality control and architectural coherence without dictating the content of contributions. Anyone can leave (fork the project) at any time, which paradoxically makes staying more meaningful.

Mapped to the taxonomy: open-source coordination is primarily a pure coordination problem with assurance game elements. Contributors want to build something together (pure coordination) but need confidence that their contributions won't be wasted or hijacked (assurance). The mechanism resolves both: the shared repository provides the focal point, and the transparency of the version control system provides the assurance.

What makes this remarkable is what it doesn't require. No shared ideology. No enforcement mechanism beyond reputational consequences. No binding agreements. No central authority with coercive power. Just transparent protocol, preserved exit and a shared artifact that benefits everyone involved.

The limitation is important: open-source coordinates around a shared deliverable. The contributors disagree on why they're contributing but agree on what they're building. This is not value incommensurability. It's divergent motivation converging on a common product. When open-source projects do face genuine value conflicts (governance disputes, licensing wars, strategic direction disagreements), the coordination often fractures: forks, hostile takeovers, community splits.

The transferable insight: transparent protocol and preserved exit can enable coordination across very different motivations, but only when there's a shared artifact or shared stakes that gives the coordination a focal point. Coordination without convergence needs *something* shared, even if what's shared is not values but consequences.

</details>

### The Irish Citizens' Assembly: Sortition and Structured Deliberation

</summary>

Ireland's citizens' assemblies produced constitutional recommendations on marriage equality and abortion access that elected politicians had avoided for decades. Randomly selected citizens, given time, expert input and structured facilitation, coordinated across genuine difference on issues where ideological convergence was impossible.

The mechanism: 99 randomly selected citizens met over multiple weekends, heard from experts representing diverse perspectives, deliberated in small groups with trained facilitators and voted on recommendations. The government committed in advance to act on those recommendations through referendum.

What made it work was a precise combination of elements. Sortition removed electoral incentives: participants weren't performing for voters or donors. The structured deliberation gave time for positions to evolve through genuine engagement rather than strategic positioning. Expert input from multiple perspectives meant participants encountered the strongest versions of views they disagreed with, rather than the caricatures that political discourse produces. And the small-group format with facilitation created conditions where the dimensions the [formal toolkit leaves out](../01-core/04-philosophical-foundation.md) could actually operate: seeing through someone else's eyes, holding tension, intellectual humility.

Mapped to the taxonomy: abortion and marriage equality in Ireland were coordination under value incommensurability. The participants held genuinely different values about human life, sexuality and the role of religion in public life. These values were not reconcilable on a common scale. The assembly didn't resolve the value disagreement. It created conditions under which citizens could coordinate on a recommendation despite the disagreement, because the process itself was perceived as legitimate.

The transferable insight: value incommensurability can be navigated (though not resolved) when the coordination process itself is trusted. Legitimacy of mechanism can substitute for convergence of values. If the people involved believe the process is fair, they can accept outcomes they disagree with. This is a significant finding, because it suggests that one bottleneck for coordination under incommensurability may be the absence of trusted process rather than the absence of shared values.

The limitation: Ireland is a small, relatively culturally cohesive nation. The government honoured the recommendations. Scaling this to contexts with less social trust, larger populations, or governments without the political will to bind themselves to citizen recommendations is an open question.

</details>

### Reactor Safety Culture: Coordination Under Irreversible Consequence

</summary>

Nuclear reactor operations provide a case study in coordination under conditions that formal models handle worst: deep uncertainty, compressed timescales, irreversible consequences and the ever-present gap between procedure and judgment.

A submarine reactor compartment is a controlled environment in one sense (every parameter is monitored) and deeply uncertain in another (the interactions between systems under abnormal conditions are complex enough that no procedure manual can cover every scenario). The operators coordinate through a safety culture that combines rigid protocol with cultivated judgment: you follow the procedure until the procedure stops making sense, and then you exercise the professional judgment that years of training have developed. The procedure is the formal model. The judgment is what fills the gap.

This is relevant because it demonstrates, at organisational scale, exactly the complementarity between formal systems and practical wisdom that the [philosophical foundation](../01-core/04-philosophical-foundation.md) argues for at larger scales. Formal procedures capture what can be anticipated. Situated expertise handles what can't. Neither alone is adequate. The Fukushima case study demonstrates what happens when one dominates: TEPCO's institutional culture suppressed the judgment that would have corrected the procedure, and the result was catastrophic.[^4]

Mapped to the taxonomy: reactor operations involve assurance games (each operator trusts the others to follow protocol and exercise judgment) nested within a context of irreversible consequence (getting it wrong means people die). The safety culture is the mechanism that provides assurance: shared training, shared standards, shared commitment to speaking up when something feels wrong, regardless of rank.

The transferable insight: coordination under irreversible consequences requires a culture, not just a mechanism. Protocols are necessary but insufficient. What makes the difference is whether the people operating within the system have both the competence and the psychological safety to exercise judgment when the protocol runs out. This has direct implications for AI governance, climate adaptation and any coordination challenge where the consequences of failure are permanent.

</details>

### The IETF: Rough Consensus Without Unanimity

</summary>

The Internet Engineering Task Force coordinates thousands of engineers across the planet on the technical standards that make the internet function. It does this without voting, without binding authority and without requiring agreement. Its operating principle is "rough consensus and running code."[^5]

Rough consensus means that the working group has addressed substantive objections, not that everyone agrees. The distinction matters enormously. The IETF doesn't aim for unanimity. It aims for a state where the remaining objections are understood, have been taken seriously and are judged by the group to be outweighed by the value of proceeding. Dissenters can record their objections formally without blocking progress.

This is a coordination mechanism designed for intelligent, opinionated experts who disagree on technical approaches but share commitment to making the internet work. It resolves the specific pathology of consensus-based systems: the ability of a small minority to veto progress. And it does so without majority-rule voting, which would risk railroading substantive objections.

Mapped to the taxonomy: IETF coordination is primarily pure coordination with assurance elements. The engineers want interoperable standards (pure coordination) and need confidence that the process won't be captured by a single corporate interest (assurance). Rough consensus addresses both: it provides the focal point while protecting against capture.

The transferable insight: "rough consensus" is a mechanism that deserves serious examination for coordination under value incommensurability. The principle that remaining objections can be acknowledged without requiring resolution, and that progress can proceed alongside documented disagreement, is precisely the kind of mechanism that coordination across genuine difference at larger scales might require. The IETF proves that you can take objections seriously and move forward at the same time. That's not compromise. It's a different kind of coordination.

The limitation: IETF participants share a deep commitment to making the internet work. The shared artifact provides the focal point. Extending rough consensus to domains where the actors don't share that underlying commitment, or where the "artifact" is something as diffuse as a habitable planet, is genuinely untested.

</details>

## Restorative Justice: Coordination Between People Who Share Nothing But Consequences

</summary>

Restorative justice brings together victims, offenders and affected community members to address the harm caused by a crime. It is coordination across the most extreme difference imaginable: participants who share no values, no relationship, no mutual goodwill and often no desire to be in the same room. What they share is consequences.

The mechanism varies (circles, conferences, victim-offender mediation), but the structure is consistent. A trained facilitator creates a space where each party speaks from their experience. The victim describes the impact. The offender hears it directly rather than through the abstractions of legal process. The community members provide context and accountability. Together, they develop a plan to address the harm: restitution, service, behavioural commitments, sometimes simply acknowledgment.

The results are striking. Meta-analyses consistently show higher victim satisfaction, lower reoffending rates and stronger sense of justice than conventional criminal proceedings.[^6] This is not because the parties come to agree on values. It is because the process creates conditions under which coordination becomes possible despite the absence of agreement.

Mapped to the taxonomy: restorative justice is coordination under value incommensurability. The victim and the offender hold fundamentally different relationships to the event. Their values regarding justice, responsibility and what "making it right" means are often irreconcilable. The process doesn't resolve this incommensurability. It creates a structure within which the parties can coordinate on a response despite it.

What makes it work maps onto the dimensions the formal toolkit leaves out. Seeing through someone else's eyes: the offender hears the impact described by the person who lived it. Holding tension: the facilitator maintains a space where anger, grief and shame coexist without premature resolution. Intellectual humility: the process assumes that no single party has the complete picture. Relational trust: built not through prior relationship but through the structure and perceived fairness of the process itself.

The transferable insight: coordination across the most extreme difference is possible when the process is structured to make room for the dimensions formal coordination theory excludes. Restorative justice doesn't work because people are good. It works because the mechanism creates conditions under which people can coordinate despite being in the worst circumstances imaginable. If it works here, between a victim and the person who harmed them, the argument that coordination across difference at larger scales is impossible becomes significantly harder to sustain.

The limitation: restorative justice requires voluntary participation. Both parties must choose to engage. It operates within a broader justice system that provides the alternative (conventional prosecution) if either party declines. Scaling this to contexts where participation is compulsory or where the "alternative" is systemic failure rather than a courtroom changes the dynamic in ways that are genuinely unexplored.

</details>

## The Common Architecture

Across these cases, from the most intimate to the most global, a pattern emerges. Successful coordination without convergence consistently involves five elements.

**Something shared that isn't values.** Shared stakes (Montreal Protocol), shared artifact (open-source), shared commitment to process (Irish assemblies), shared professional culture (reactor operations), shared functional commitment (IETF), shared consequences (restorative justice). A couple navigating a health crisis shares the stakes without necessarily sharing views on treatment. A team shipping a product shares the deliverable without sharing career motivations. In every case, the actors hold different values. In every case, something else provides the coordination substrate. Identifying what that substrate is for any given coordination challenge is one of the most important diagnostic questions the taxonomy equips us to ask.

**Correct diagnosis of strategic structure.** The Montreal Protocol matched its mechanism to an assurance game. Open-source matched its mechanism to a pure coordination problem. The Irish assembly matched its mechanism to value incommensurability. A couple who diagnose their conflict as an assurance game (we'd both commit if we trusted the other would) rather than chicken (who apologises first) will reach for different tools and get better results. When mechanism matches structure, coordination succeeds. When it doesn't, even well-designed mechanisms fail, whether the actors are spouses or superpowers.

**Transparency of process.** In every successful case, the mechanism itself was legible. Git makes contributions visible. The assembly's deliberations were public. IETF proceedings are documented. Reactor safety culture demands transparent communication across rank. Restorative justice works in a circle where everyone speaks and everyone hears. Even in a household, the couples that Gottman's research identifies as successful are the ones whose bids for connection are legible rather than coded.[^7] The participants' motivations remain private. The mechanism through which they coordinate is open.

**Preserved exit.** In every case, participation is voluntary and withdrawal is possible. Open-source projects can be forked. Nations can withdraw from treaties. Assembly participants can dissent. IETF engineers can propose competing standards. Restorative justice requires both parties to choose to participate. In relationships, Gottman's research shows that partnerships where both people feel genuinely free to leave are more resilient than those held together by obligation or entrapment. This is not a weakness. It's load-bearing. Coordination that participants can't leave becomes coercion, and coercion is brittle under pressure.

**Graduated trust rather than comprehensive agreement.** No successful case began with a grand bargain. The Montreal Protocol phased its reductions. Open-source contributions start small and build reputation over time. The Irish assembly process was iterative across multiple assemblies. IETF standards evolve through rough consensus across multiple drafts. Restorative justice begins with the willingness to sit in the same room, not with forgiveness. A new relationship builds trust through small bids answered, not through declarations of eternal commitment on the first date. Trust is built through repeated smaller successes, not demanded upfront.

## What Remains Uncertain

This evidence base is real and it matters. But intellectual honesty requires flagging where the evidence stops and the extrapolation begins.

Every case above involved conditions friendlier than those the hardest coordination challenges face. The Montreal Protocol had clear science and available substitutes. Open-source has a concrete shared artifact. The Irish assembly operated within a functioning democracy with a government willing to be bound. Reactor safety culture depends on shared professional training over years. The IETF's participants share deep technical commitment.

High-stakes coordination at its most demanding involves actors without shared professional culture, without a concrete shared artifact, operating across timescales too compressed for iterative trust-building, facing consequences too irreversible for learning from failure, with strategic structures that may shift between archetypes mid-negotiation.

The principles extracted here are the best available foundations. Whether they're sufficient for the hardest coordination challenges is precisely what the rest of this directory is trying to work out honestly.

The evidence says coordination without convergence is possible. It says a great deal about what enables it. It does not yet tell us whether it can be enabled under the most unforgiving conditions. That is the frontier.
