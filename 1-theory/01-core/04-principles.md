# Principles

## Part I: What Was Excluded

The ancient Greeks had a word — *logos* — that meant something far richer than what we now call logic. For Heraclitus, logos was a cosmic ordering principle: the pattern governing how reality itself operates, including the capacity to hold opposites in tension without resolving them into one side or the other. Unity-in-opposition. The harmonious and the discordant, together.[^1]

Over the course of western philosophy's evolution, logos was progressively narrowed. Plato privileged the unchanging over flux. Aristotle formalised the law of non-contradiction as an absolute constraint. Descartes demanded that all knowledge be broken into simple, non-contradictory parts. Kant made logic transcendental — the very precondition for rational thought. By the time formal logic became the dominant framework for practical reason, several dimensions of the original logos had been quietly excluded: the capacity for holding contradiction, for pattern recognition that precedes inference, for wisdom embedded in situation and embodiment rather than abstraction.[^2]

This matters because everything downstream inherits the narrowing.

If your foundational framework for practical reason says that contradiction is impermissible, that valid reasoning must proceed by formal inference from clear premises, that knowledge requires abstraction from the particular — then every discipline built on those foundations will carry the same constraints. Including coordination theory. Including institutional design. Including how we think about what it means for groups of people (or non-human actors) to make decisions together.

Current coordination theory assumes that rational actors weigh options, calculate expected utilities and select optimal strategies. Where values conflict, it assumes either convergence (we negotiate until we agree) or aggregation (we vote and the majority wins). Where uncertainty is deep, it reaches for probabilistic modelling. These are powerful tools. They are also tools shaped by a specific and historically contingent understanding of what counts as reason. An understanding that excludes, by design, dimensions of practical wisdom that every functioning human relationship depends on.

The incompleteness of coordination theory is inherited. The theorists built well on the foundations they were given. The foundations themselves are what need expanding.

## Part II: What Coordination Actually Requires

Watch any family navigate a genuine disagreement — not a trivial one, but the kind where people hold deeply different values about something that matters. A decision about elderly care. A conflict between siblings about inheritance. A parent and adult child who see the world fundamentally differently.

The ones who coordinate successfully don't do it by running cost-benefit analyses. They don't vote. They don't find the Nash equilibrium. They do something that formal coordination theory has no vocabulary for: they hold the tension. They stay in the room with genuine disagreement. They attend to what the other person is actually experiencing, even when they think the other person is wrong. And they find a way forward that doesn't require anyone to abandon who they are.

This is a *capacity*. It can be cultivated or neglected. It can be present in an individual or absent. It can be supported by institutional design or destroyed by it. And it is absolutely essential for coordination across genuine difference at any scale.

What does this capacity consist of? Several things that formal reason has historically excluded from its domain:

**Seeing through someone else's eyes.** The ability to model another person's perspective accurately, including perspectives you disagree with. This is frequently dismissed as sentiment. It is actually a cognitive skill — a form of knowledge acquisition that cannot be replicated by formal inference. You cannot deduce what it is like to hold values fundamentally different from your own. You can only attend to it. Coordination across difference requires this attending as a prerequisite, because without it you are coordinating with your projection of the other person rather than with who they actually are.

**Holding the tension.** Formal logic demands resolution. One side is right, the other wrong, or we find a synthesis that dissolves the tension. But many of the most important coordination challenges involve values that are genuinely incommensurable — they cannot be ranked on a common scale.[^3] Climate policy that serves economic growth and climate policy that serves ecological preservation may both be valid responses to legitimate values. The capacity to hold both without premature resolution, to coordinate *across* the tension rather than by eliminating it, is precisely what Heraclitus' logos described and what formal logic excludes.

**The wisdom that lives in the body.** The nurse who knows her ward is understaffed before any metric confirms it. The submariner who feels the boat behaving differently before any instrument registers the change. The negotiator who reads the room. This knowledge is real, consequential and irreducible to propositional form. Coordination theory that operates only at the level of formal models and explicit preferences systematically ignores the situated wisdom that often makes the difference between coordination that works and coordination that collapses.

**Knowing you don't have the full picture.** Coordination across genuine difference is impossible if any party believes they already have the complete picture. Intellectual humility — the recognition that your framework or information may be incomplete, that the other party may see something you cannot — is a functional prerequisite for the kind of coordination this framework addresses. Humility here is an engineering requirement, not a moral aspiration.

**Trust is built, not assumed.** Formal coordination theory models actors as discrete agents with defined preferences interacting through mechanisms. The reality of human coordination is relational: trust is built over time, commitments carry weight beyond their strategic value, relationships shape what becomes possible. Treating coordination as a purely mechanical interaction between isolated agents misses the substrate on which all durable coordination actually rests.

These are load-bearing capacities that coordination across genuine difference mechanically requires. A bridge designed without accounting for wind load will eventually fail, regardless of how elegantly it handles static weight. Coordination theory designed without accounting for these capacities will fail (and, arguably, is failing) in exactly the same way — and for exactly the same reasons.

## Part III: Design Principles

The Unity Compass is built on principles that take the above seriously. Each principle addresses both the philosophical incompleteness identified in Part I and the practical capacities identified in Part II.

**1. Coordination without convergence.**
The framework must enable collective action between actors who hold genuinely different values, worldviews and goals. It must never require ideological agreement as a precondition for cooperation. Where convergence emerges, it should be a byproduct of coordination, not a prerequisite for it.

**2. Logos-breadth over logic-alone.**
The framework must incorporate dimensions of practical reason that formal logic excludes: situated knowledge, embodied wisdom, tolerance for contradiction, relational intelligence. It draws on the full bandwidth of human reasoning capacity rather than restricting itself to formal inference. This means expanding what rigour includes rather than relegating it to the back-bench.

**3. Pluralism-preserving.**
The framework must protect genuine difference rather than eroding it. Coordination that works by gradually homogenising its participants has succeeded at cooperation but failed at pluralism. The goal is coordination that recognises diversity as the structural asset it is, rather than seeing it as an obstacle to be overcome.

**4. Operationalisable.**
The framework must produce mechanisms that actual institutions can test, implement and evaluate. Principles that cannot be translated into practice — however philosophically elegant — fail the test this project sets for itself. Every theoretical claim must connect to a concrete coordination mechanism with identifiable success criteria.

**5. Scale-independent.**
The same coordination patterns appear at every scale: between individuals, within teams, across organisations, between nations. The framework must be coherent across these scales. A principle that works for family disagreements but breaks at the level of international coordination (or vice versa) is incomplete.

**6. Barrier-aware.**
The framework must be designed with the [barriers to implementation](03-why-not-already.md) explicitly in mind. Academically rigorous enough to survive peer review. Practically grounded enough to function inside broken incentive structures. Accessible enough to escape the rooms where coordination theory has been trapped. If a principle cannot survive contact with the institutional realities documented in this repository, it needs revision.

**7. Honest about incompleteness.**
The framework acknowledges what it does not yet know. It is being built in the open precisely because the kind of coordination it describes — across genuine difference, incorporating multiple perspectives, remaining humble about its own limitations — can only be developed through the practice of what it preaches. Certainty is not the goal. Adequacy under real conditions is.

---

These principles are the compass bearings. Everything in the [frameworks](../03-frameworks/) directory derives from them. Everything in the [case studies](../../2-practice/03-case-studies/) tests them. And everything in the [critiques and responses](../../3-project/critiques-and-responses/) defends or revises them.

The conviction underneath all of it remains what it was at the start: that the human capacities required for coordination across genuine difference — empathy, humility, the ability to hold tension, the wisdom that lives in bodies and relationships rather than abstractions — are the unavoidable engineering specifications required for a species that wants to survive what's coming.

We are all built from the same materials. The same atoms, the same stardust, the same brief tenancy on the same fragile planet. If that shared ground cannot serve as a foundation for coordination, nothing can. And if it can — then the only question is whether we will build on it in time.
