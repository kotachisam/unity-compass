**Lineage grep over Gaus §20 revises the §15-vs-§20 framing.** Systematic cross-reference search over the full Gaus PDF corrected the earlier capture. §20 does not formally build on §15.1's perspective model. The actual formal lineage is §9.3 (Bicchieri k_n) → §10.3 (μ_A(R_i) first introduced as cardinal inherent utility, with explicit forward reference to §20 in footnote 13) → §19.3 (Deliberative Model from *The Order of Public Reason*, ordinal with quasi-transitive Pareto-or-Indifference rule) → §20 (cardinalises §19.3). §15.1 is a conceptual orphan in Gaus's own book. The smoking gun is §20.1 footnote 3 verbatim: "Thus far, this is simply a cardinalization of the original ordinal model. And so it requires completeness of orderings, something I did not assume in the ordinal model." Gaus cardinalises specifically to enable the product form U_A(R) = μ_A(R) × w_A(n)(R) at p. 149. The sharper differentiation move: the thesis returns to Gaus's own §19.3 ordinal model and provides geometric machinery (partial orderings via dominance cones, vector-valued game theory) that preserves the reconciliation dynamic without requiring cardinalization. Captured as a revision subsection under the Gerald Gaus entry in methodological-evolution.md. Flagged as pending user verification after §§9.3, §10.3 and §19.3 are read directly.

**Six-element perspective model captured, element V flagged as a missing thesis component.** Surfaced from the user's own reading of Gaus §15.1-15.2. Gaus's perspective model decomposes an agent into six elements: (I) goals/values/principles, (II) method for modifying standards, (III) categorisations of the world, (IV) options in a context, (V) predictive models mapping options to social worlds, (VI) a ranking function. Element VI explicitly allows "multiple non-aggregated rankings based on different evaluative standards" (p. 104), which is a partial-ordering claim in Gaus's own conceptual framework. His §20 Self-Organization Model collapses this to a single cardinal utility function for tractability. The thesis lives precisely in the gap between §15's permissive conceptual model and §20's restrictive formalisation. Captured as an extension to the Gerald Gaus subsection in methodological-evolution.md. The six-element taxonomy maps onto the thesis's two-layer (structural/evaluative) split with one critical gap: **element V (predictive models) has no analogue in the current thesis sketch and is flagged as a key missing component.** New subsection "Predictive models as a missing element (surfaced 2026-04-11)" added to Formal object evolution with three candidate formal moves (per-agent dynamical systems, causal model bundles, learned predictive models with shared updating). New question added to open-questions.md under the Gaus-derived section. Two new reading log entries: Muldoon 2016 *Social Contract Theory for a Diverse World* (P3, the Gaus citation for the perspective model) and Gaus 2016 *The Tyranny of the Ideal* (P4, optional follow-up for chapter 2 on the perspective model). The user has confirmed element V will be a key part of the thesis but the construction is not yet worked out; substantial development reserved for the MPhil year.

**Hayek, Page and North added to the structure/preferences foundations.** Surfaced from the user's reading of Gaus §§15-16 (in progress). Gaus draws on Hayek's cognitive theory of mind (*The Sensory Order*, 1952) and Scott Page's three-way taxonomy of categorisation differences (*The Difference*, 2007, ch. 3) to establish that categorisation-dependence is endemic to social science and that "we can no longer accept that there is a single universally valid model of the world" (Solow, cited in Gaus). Three new entries added to the Foundations of structure/preferences distinction section in reading-log.md: Hayek 1952 (P3, the cognitive theory foundation), Page 2007 (P3, the three-way taxonomy), North 2005 (P3, the institutional-economics application). The taxonomy maps directly onto the tier structure: Tier 1 catastrophic threats survive case (iii) "disagreement on basic entities" almost everywhere; Tier 2 sits in case (i) "same atomic entities, different groupings" territory where Sen's positional objectivity handles the difference; Tier 3 runs into case (ii) "one perspective omits what other captures" and sometimes case (iii). Gaus himself accepts the categorisation-dependence point and proceeds; this is a friendly precedent for the thesis's conditional retreat rather than a challenge.

**Tiered defensibility of structural features captured.** After the literature exploration on the structure/preferences distinction surfaced the Putnam concern, the user observed that the conditional retreat (Sen's positional objectivity) is too uniform across the four candidate structural features. Shared risk topology admits a stronger claim at the catastrophic-to-humanity level (nuclear war, runaway AI, climate collapse, pandemic, asteroid impact) because at that tier the categorisation is robustly shared across virtually any agent who values their own and their kind's continued existence. Captured as a new "Tiered defensibility of structural features" subsection in methodological-evolution.md under Formal object evolution. The tier structure: Tier 1 catastrophic (robust observer-independence with academic precedent in Bostrom and Ord), Tier 2 serious-but-not-existential (conditional version, AUKUS sits here), Tier 3 contested (out of scope). Asymmetric application across the four features: shared risk topology has a partial Tier 1 case, constraint topology has a partial Tier 1 case for physical constraints, entanglement density and procedural commitments need the conditional retreat throughout. New reading log section "Existential risk and the catastrophic-categorisation tier" added with Bostrom 2013 (P3, *Global Policy* article, the cleanest single citation), Ord 2020 (P3, *The Precipice*), Bostrom 2002 (P4, the founding paper). Open questions "Is structural fitness observer-independent?" updated with the tiered refinement. Uncommitted.
