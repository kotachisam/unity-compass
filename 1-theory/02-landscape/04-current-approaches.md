# Current Approaches

This investigation is not the first attempt to improve how humans coordinate across difference. At every scale, people and institutions are experimenting with alternatives to the coordination mechanisms that are failing them. This document surveys the most significant current approaches, acknowledges what they do well, identifies where they fall short, and makes explicit where this investigation sits in relation to them.

This is not an exhaustive literature review. It is an honest map of the landscape as it stands, organised by scale.

## Interpersonal Scale

**Nonviolent Communication (NVC).** Developed by Marshall Rosenberg, NVC provides a structured framework for navigating conflict between individuals by separating observations from evaluations, identifying underlying needs, and making requests rather than demands.[^1] It is widely used in mediation, education and therapeutic contexts. NVC engages directly with dimensions the [formal toolkit leaves out](../01-core/04-philosophical-foundation.md): perspective-taking, emotional literacy, the ability to stay present with disagreement. Its limitation is that it was designed for face-to-face interaction between willing participants. It has no mechanism for scaling beyond the interpersonal, and it assumes a degree of good faith that is often absent in institutional and political contexts.

**Restorative Justice.** Restorative justice practices bring together victims, offenders and community members to address harm through dialogue rather than punishment. The process requires participants to sit with perspectives radically different from their own and coordinate on outcomes without requiring agreement on values. It works remarkably well at the community scale and has been adopted in criminal justice systems across New Zealand, Canada, Northern Ireland and elsewhere. Its limitation is similar to NVC: it depends on facilitated, face-to-face interaction and voluntary participation. It has not been successfully abstracted into institutional design principles that operate without facilitation.

**Gottman Method.** The Gottman Institute's four decades of research on couples identified specific, measurable behaviours that predict whether relationships survive conflict. The findings map directly onto Axelrod's game theory results: successful couples are "nice" (they lead with positive interaction), "forgiving" (they repair after conflict), "retaliatory" in a healthy sense (they raise issues rather than suppressing them) and "clear" (their communication is legible to their partner).[^2] The research demonstrates that coordination across genuine difference is an empirically observable, trainable skill, not a mysterious quality some relationships have and others don't. Its limitation: the findings are descriptive rather than prescriptive at scale. Knowing what works in couples doesn't automatically generate institutional design principles.

## Team and Organisational Scale

**Sociocracy and Holacracy.** These governance frameworks distribute decision-making authority across self-organising teams using consent-based (rather than consensus-based) processes. Sociocracy, developed in the Netherlands, uses nested circles of authority. Holacracy, popularised by Zappos's adoption, uses roles rather than job titles and distributes authority through a constitution. Both attempt the surgical combination of [top-down and bottom-up](01-top-down-bottom-up.md): clear structural coherence with distributed intelligence. Their limitation is adoption: both require significant cultural commitment and tend to work best in organisations that self-select for this kind of governance. Scaling beyond mid-sized organisations has proven difficult, and both can generate their own forms of bureaucracy.

**Agile and Scrum.** Originally developed for software, Agile methodology embeds iterative coordination structurally: short work cycles, frequent retrospectives, and the explicit expectation that the plan will change as understanding deepens. It is essentially a coordination protocol designed for conditions of uncertainty, precisely the conditions this investigation cares about. Its adoption across industries beyond software (healthcare, education, government) suggests the principles travel. Its limitation: Agile coordinates well around shared deliverables but has little to say about coordination across genuinely different values. It assumes alignment on what's being built, if not on how.

**Theory U (Otto Scharmer).** Scharmer's framework, developed at MIT, proposes that effective coordination requires "presencing": a quality of attention that integrates open mind, open heart and open will. It explicitly incorporates contemplative and embodied dimensions that most organisational theory excludes. Theory U bridges the gap between the rational-analytical and the experiential in ways that parallel this work's logos-breadth principle. Its limitation: the language and methodology can feel inaccessible, bordering on mystical, in contexts that demand pragmatic credibility. It has been adopted in some organisational development contexts but has not penetrated mainstream institutional design.

**Quaker Consensus.** The Religious Society of Friends has practised consent-based collective decision-making for over three centuries. Quaker business method involves silent reflection, speaking from personal experience rather than argument, and seeking "the sense of the meeting" rather than a vote. It is one of the oldest functioning models of coordination across difference that explicitly incorporates contemplative practice. Its limitation: it depends on a shared spiritual framework (seeking the will of God or the Light) that cannot be assumed outside Quaker communities. The mechanism works, but the underlying metaphysical commitment limits transferability.

## Community and Commons Scale

**Ostrom's Polycentric Governance.** Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize-winning work demonstrated that communities can and do successfully manage shared resources without either privatisation or state control, through locally developed rules informed by situated knowledge.[^3] Her eight design principles for commons governance remain the most empirically grounded framework for coordination at the community scale. Post-Ostrom researchers are now applying polycentric approaches to AI governance and climate coordination. Limitation: Ostrom's conditions (bounded communities, repeated interaction, observable behaviour, graduated sanctions) are demanding. Many existential coordination challenges involve actors who lack shared community bonds, interact infrequently and can defect without immediate consequence.

**Indigenous Governance Traditions.** Many Indigenous communities have practised coordination across difference for centuries through consensus-based systems that embed relational obligation, intergenerational responsibility and connection to place. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy's Great Law of Peace, for instance, coordinated six nations with different languages and interests through a sophisticated system of nested councils and consensus-building. These traditions have been systematically excluded from mainstream coordination theory despite engaging directly with the dimensions this investigation identifies as excluded from formal theory. Their limitation is contextual: they emerge from specific cultural and ecological relationships and resist abstraction into universal design principles without losing what makes them work.

## National Scale

**Citizens' Assemblies and Sortition.** Randomly selected citizens deliberating on policy questions represent perhaps the most promising democratic innovation of the last two decades. Ireland's Citizens' Assembly produced recommendations on abortion and marriage equality that led to successful referenda, achieving coordination on deeply divisive issues that elected politicians had avoided for decades.[^4] The model is spreading: France, Germany, the Netherlands, Northern Ireland and others have adopted or are experimenting with citizens' assemblies. The mechanism is essentially a formalised version of the [surgical combination](01-top-down-bottom-up.md): bottom-up citizen intelligence, institutional scaffolding for deliberation, top-down implementation of recommendations. Limitation: tested almost exclusively within single polities. The model assumes a shared civic identity and a government with the legitimacy and willingness to act on recommendations. Neither condition holds for cross-border existential coordination.

**Quadratic Voting and Mechanism Design (RadicalxChange).** Glen Weyl and the RadicalxChange movement propose redesigning the mathematical mechanisms of collective decision-making. Quadratic voting allows participants to express intensity of preference, not just direction. Quadratic funding allocates resources based on breadth of support rather than depth of pockets. These are elegant technical solutions to specific coordination failures.[^5] Limitation: they solve for preference aggregation, better ways to combine what people want. This investigation argues that aggregation itself is insufficient when values are genuinely incommensurable. You can't quadratically vote your way through a conflict where the parties don't share a common metric for what counts as a good outcome.

**Audrey Tang and Digital Democracy (Taiwan).** Taiwan's former Digital Minister pioneered the use of platforms like pol.is to find consensus across polarised populations by identifying areas of unexpected agreement. The approach uses AI-assisted clustering to surface shared ground that adversarial debate obscures. It has been used successfully for ride-sharing regulation and other policy questions in Taiwan. Limitation: it works well for finding existing common ground. It is less clear how it handles cases where genuine common ground doesn't exist and the coordination challenge is to act despite irreducible disagreement.

## International Scale

**Multilateral Treaty Systems.** The existing architecture of international coordination: the UN system, the Paris Agreement, the WTO, the WHO Framework Convention. These institutions facilitate coordination through voluntary commitment backed by normative pressure and transparency mechanisms. Their strengths (universality, legitimacy, institutional continuity) are also their weaknesses (lowest-common-denominator agreements, enforcement gaps, vulnerability to the [false consensus](03-why-it-fails.md) failure mode). The UN's 2024 Pact for the Future represents the most recent attempt at reform within this framework.

**Polycentric AI Governance.** The emerging academic consensus on AI governance argues for multilevel, polycentric arrangements rather than a single global mechanism, directly applying Ostrom's principles to the most urgent current coordination challenge. The OECD, the Bletchley Declaration process and the UN Global Digital Compact all point in this direction. This approach correctly identifies that centralised governance will fail for AI. Its limitation: polycentricity is a structural principle, not a coordination mechanism. Saying "governance should be multilevel" doesn't tell you how actors at different levels with different interests actually coordinate in practice.

## Cross-Scale / Meta-Level

**The Metacrisis Framework (Daniel Schmachtenberger / Consilience Project).** Schmachtenberger's work is probably the closest existing project to this investigation in diagnostic scope. His "metacrisis" framing, that individual crises share common generator functions including multipolar traps, coordination failures and sensemaking breakdowns, maps onto much of the diagnosis in this repository. His concept of a "third attractor" (a path that avoids both catastrophe and authoritarian lockdown) parallels coordination without convergence. Limitation: the metacrisis framework is stronger on diagnosis than prescription. It has generated extensive podcast and essay content but relatively little in the way of testable, operationalisable mechanisms. The EA Forum criticism is telling: the lack of written, interrogable framework makes it difficult to evaluate or build on.[^6]

**Complexity Science Approaches.** Researchers at the Santa Fe Institute and elsewhere apply complexity theory to governance, arguing that traditional linear policy approaches fail in complex adaptive systems. This provides useful theoretical grounding (the Unity Compass draws on complexity science in its methodology) but complexity science describes system behaviour rather than prescribing coordination mechanisms. Knowing that a system is complex doesn't tell you how to coordinate within it.

**Game Theory Extensions.** Researchers continue to extend Axelrod's work into noisy environments, evolutionary dynamics and multi-player scenarios. Psychological game theory (Colman) addresses some limitations of classical rational choice. These extensions are valuable but remain within the formal modelling paradigm. They make the existing toolkit more sophisticated without expanding what the toolkit is permitted to include.

## Where This Investigation Sits

Every approach surveyed above does something genuinely valuable. The interpersonal approaches (NVC, restorative justice, Gottman) attend to dimensions formal coordination theory leaves out but haven't been theorised as coordination mechanisms or connected upward to institutional design. The organisational approaches (sociocracy, Agile, Theory U) demonstrate that alternatives to pure top-down coordination work in practice but struggle to scale or translate across domains. The national-scale innovations (citizens' assemblies, quadratic voting, digital democracy) are the most directly relevant to formal coordination design but remain untested for cross-border, cross-system coordination. The international approaches (multilateral treaties, polycentric governance) correctly identify structural principles but lack the operational mechanisms to make them function. The meta-level thinkers (Schmachtenberger, complexity science) diagnose brilliantly but are light on testable prescription.

This investigation makes two claims that distinguish its position in the landscape:

**The philosophical diagnosis.** Most approaches above operate within the existing rationality framework and build better mechanisms on top of it. This work argues that the framework itself is incomplete: the concept of rationality underpinning those approaches has been narrowed by multiple converging factors, from philosophical inheritance to the disciplinary origins of game theory to the cultural specificity of Western academic thought. The narrowing of logos to logic is the primary point of observation, almost certainly one strand among multiple. Schmachtenberger comes closest to this diagnostic instinct but doesn't trace it to its philosophical or historical roots.

**The specific formal question.** Most approaches surveyed above try to improve coordination mechanisms. This investigation asks a narrower, more foundational question: under what formal conditions can structural features of interdependence (shared risk topology, shared constraints, network entanglement, procedural commitments) ground coordination among agents with incommensurable values? The question is narrower than many approaches above but formalisable in ways that most diagnostic work is not.

Whether this position turns out to be useful is what the [frameworks](../03-frameworks/) directory is meant to explore.
