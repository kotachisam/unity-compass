# Where Coordination Fails

The [previous document](02-where-it-works.md) identified what successful coordination looks like. This one examines the opposite: the recurring patterns of failure that appear when coordination breaks down across genuine difference. Understanding these patterns is essential because the [frameworks](../03-frameworks/) this project develops must be designed to survive them.

Detailed case examinations live in the [case studies](./04-case-studies/) directory. This document extracts the common architecture of failure.

## The Failure Modes

Coordination failures across domains and scales tend to share recognisable signatures.

**The speed mismatch.** Coordination fails when the problem moves faster than the coordinating mechanism can respond. Climate change operates on physical timescales. Democratic institutions operate on electoral timescales. Corporate strategy operates on quarterly timescales. These rhythms are fundamentally misaligned, and the mismatch is structural rather than accidental. The IPCC produces comprehensive assessments every six to seven years. The climate doesn't wait for the next assessment cycle. COVID-19 demanded coordinated global response in weeks. The WHO's institutional processes were designed for months. By the time coordination caught up, the window for early containment had closed. The same dynamic plays out in families. When one partner undergoes a rapid personal transformation — a career crisis, a spiritual awakening, a health diagnosis — the relationship must adapt at the speed of the change. If the other partner's capacity to process and respond moves slower than the transformation itself, the coordination collapses. The mismatch is the same at every scale: the problem moves faster than the mechanism for addressing it.

**The information distortion.** Coordination fails when the intelligence that would enable good decisions is filtered, simplified or suppressed before it reaches the people with the power to act. This is the structural weakness of top-down coordination identified in [the previous analysis](01-top-down-bottom-up.md), but it deserves its own treatment as a failure mode because of how consistently it appears. The Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster is a particularly well-documented example. TEPCO's own modelling in 2006 predicted that an earthquake of magnitude 8.3 could produce a 15-metre tsunami. The information existed. It moved through institutional channels for five years without producing the modification to the site's 5.7-metre sea wall that would have prevented the disaster. The Kurokawa Report concluded that both TEPCO and the government were "trapped in a safety myth" — institutional culture had distorted the information flow to the point where known risks were treated as theoretical rather than operational.[^1]

**The accountability void.** Coordination fails when the structure of responsibility is so distributed that nobody is accountable for the outcome. This is different from shared responsibility, which can work well. The accountability void occurs when every participant can plausibly claim that the failure was someone else's job. The 2008 financial crisis is the canonical example. Rating agencies rated the instruments. Banks originated the loans. Regulators oversaw the system. Investors bought the products. When the system collapsed, every actor could point to another actor who should have caught the problem. The architecture of the system had been designed (or had evolved) in a way that distributed accountability so widely that it effectively disappeared. The pattern is visible in any shared household. When five housemates share a kitchen, the dishes accumulate. Everyone can see the problem. Everyone assumes someone else will handle it. The structure of shared responsibility without clear ownership produces the same outcome at the kitchen sink as it does in global financial regulation: the mess compounds until a crisis forces the issue.

**The capture dynamic.** Coordination fails when the mechanism designed to serve collective interests is gradually captured by a subset of participants and redirected to serve their interests instead. Stigler's theory of regulatory capture[^2] describes the formal mechanism: regulated industries have concentrated incentives to influence regulators, while the diffuse public has weak incentives to monitor them. But capture extends beyond formal regulation. Industry bodies, standards organisations, professional associations, even international coordinating institutions can all be captured by the actors they are supposed to coordinate. The failure mode is subtle because the institution continues to look like it's coordinating. The meetings still happen. The reports still get published. The coordination is simply no longer serving the purpose it was created for.

**The legitimacy deficit.** Coordination fails when participants don't recognise the coordinating mechanism as having the right to coordinate them. This is the fundamental challenge of global governance. The United Nations can facilitate, convene and recommend. It cannot compel. Its legitimacy rests on voluntary participation by sovereign states, any of which can defect when the coordination becomes inconvenient. The same dynamic plays out at smaller scales: employees who don't trust leadership will comply minimally rather than coordinate genuinely. Citizens who don't trust government institutions will work around them rather than through them. Coordination without legitimacy produces compliance at best and subversion at worst.

**The false consensus.** Coordination fails when agreement is manufactured rather than genuine. This is a particularly dangerous failure mode because it looks like success. Everyone agrees. The decision is made. The coordination appears to be working. But underneath the surface, participants have agreed to language they interpret differently, committed to actions they don't intend to follow through on, or suppressed disagreements that will resurface later with compound interest. International climate agreements are vulnerable to this: ambitious targets announced with fanfare, followed by implementation that quietly falls short, followed by renegotiation of targets downward at the next summit. The coordination mechanism produces the appearance of coordination while the underlying problem continues to worsen.

**The identity entrenchment.** Coordination fails when the process of disagreement hardens the positions it was supposed to bridge. Adversarial structures — parliamentary opposition, litigation, competitive negotiation — are designed on the assumption that truth or good policy emerges from the collision of opposing positions. Sometimes it does. But the same structures can entrench identity around those positions, making participants more committed to "winning" than to coordinating. Once a position becomes part of who you are rather than what you think, the capacity to coordinate across it collapses. This is visible in the polarisation of political discourse globally: positions that began as policy disagreements have calcified into identity markers, and the coordination mechanisms (debate, negotiation, democratic deliberation) have become arenas for performance rather than instruments for collective decision-making.
This operates with equal force in interpersonal relationships. A disagreement between partners about finances or parenting can begin as a genuine difference of opinion and gradually harden into a proxy war about who they each are. Once 'I think we should save more' becomes 'I'm the responsible one and you're the reckless one', the capacity to coordinate on the actual question has been replaced by the need to defend an identity. The original problem becomes unsolvable because it's no longer what the argument is about.

## The Compound Failure

These failure modes rarely appear in isolation. More commonly, they interact and reinforce each other.

A speed mismatch creates pressure. Under pressure, information distortion worsens as institutional filters prioritise reassurance over accuracy. The accountability void means nobody is responsible for correcting the distortion. Captured institutions redirect the response toward the interests of their captors. The legitimacy of the coordinating mechanism erodes as participants observe the dysfunction. False consensus is manufactured to preserve the appearance of progress. And positions harden as participants retreat into identity-based entrenchment rather than engage with a process they no longer trust.

This compound failure is what the [barriers documented in the core](../01-core/03-why-not-already.md) look like in practice. The barriers explain *why* institutions get stuck. These failure modes describe *what it looks like* when they are stuck. The [design principles](../01-core/05-design-principles.md) are constraints the framework must satisfy to avoid reproducing them. And the [frameworks](../03-frameworks/) that follow are the proposed mechanisms for actually doing so.
