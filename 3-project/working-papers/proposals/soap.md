# Statement of Academic Purpose

**Applicant:** Samuel Norton
**Programme:** MPhil/PhD in Philosophy, London School of Economics

## Academic Motivations

I came to philosophy through the operational reality of coordination under catastrophic uncertainty. As a nuclear reactor operator on Royal Navy submarines, the conditions of my professional life were close to what Bradley (2022) calls "social ethics under ambiguity": small groups of agents making consequential decisions where standard expected utility theory does not apply, where the cost of misalignment is total, and where the structure of the situation rather than agreement on values does the coordinating work. I left the Service for defence consulting and watched the same dynamic at larger scale, particularly in arrangements like AUKUS where partner nations coordinate on shared security risks under genuinely different strategic doctrines. What kept returning was the gap between the conditions practitioners operate in and the formal apparatus available to theorise them. Game theory required common payoffs that often did not exist. Ethical frameworks required value convergence that was neither realistic nor necessary. The question that crystallised was whether structural features of interdependence could ground coordination formally where value convergence does not.

I want to study at LSE because the Department of Philosophy, Logic and Scientific Method is one of the few places where this question can be pursued with both formal rigour and philosophical substance. Professor Alexander's evolutionary game theory work, particularly *The Structural Evolution of Morality* (2007) and the diagnostic argument of *The Open Society as an Enemy* (2024), maps the territory this thesis occupies from two sides. The gap between his formal and diagnostic work is the gap the thesis proposes to close. Professor Bradley's work on decision theory under ambiguity, especially the partial orderings he flags as "left for another day" in his 2022 *Ethics* paper, supplies the formal apparatus the thesis builds on. The combination is uncommon. The department's broader culture, in which formal modelling is treated as continuous with philosophical argument rather than separated from it, is the environment the work needs.

I have been in correspondence with Professor Ruth Chang, who advised me on academic suitability before Christmas and who has since confirmed, in response to a specific question, that the structural extension of parity I propose is "a paradigmatic case of parity". I intend to write to Professors Alexander and Bradley before the formal submission of this application.

## Suitability

My academic background is in engineering physics (BSc) and nuclear reactor technology (PGDip). My first profession was nuclear reactor operator on Royal Navy submarines, where coordination under catastrophic uncertainty is an operational reality rather than a theoretical abstraction. After leaving the Service I worked in defence consulting on cross-institutional projects, including arrangements adjacent to AUKUS. That work gave me direct exposure to coordination failures across different institutional logics and supplies the primary case-study domain of the proposed research.

My mathematical preparation extends to the formal apparatus the thesis requires. The technical stack the work needs is not foreign to me: evolutionary game theory after Alexander and Skyrms, decision theory under severe uncertainty after Bradley, partial orderings and parity after Sen and Chang, and the differential geometry of a configuration space with position-dependent metric. My engineering training equipped me to engage these directly. I am also a software engineer of some years' standing, which matters because the formal programme will require simulation work alongside analytical results.

I have undertaken substantial preliminary reading toward this proposal. Alexander's *The Open Society as an Enemy* (2024) and Bradley's "Impartial Evaluation under Ambiguity" (2022) have been read in full with detailed source notes, alongside Alexander's *Stanford Encyclopedia* entry on evolutionary game theory. Gaus's *The Open Society and Its Complexities* (2021) is in progress, with §§19.3 and §20 read directly. The cardinalisation move Gaus flags in his own footnote at §20.1 supplies the textual hook for the thesis's differentiation from his Self-Organization Model. Chang's *The Possibility of Parity* (2002) and *Hard Choices* (2017) have been re-read in preparation for the work sample submitted with this application. The reading programme is documented in a working bibliography and is ongoing, with the Alexander and Bradley formal texts (the Cambridge Elements volume on evolutionary game theory and *Decision Theory with a Human Face* respectively) as the next targets.

## Purpose and Objectives

The MPhil year would provide the philosophical training I need to do this work properly. My background equips me to handle the formal apparatus but I have not been trained in the analytic philosophy traditions in which the thesis must situate itself, particularly social choice theory, formal epistemology under severe uncertainty, and the literature on incommensurability and parity. The taught component of the MPhil would supply this. The supervised research component would let me develop the formal object the thesis proposes, partial orderings over position-vectors with a vector-valued equilibrium concept, to the point where its existence conditions and initial characterisation results can be established. The progression to PhD would then deliver the full formal account, the AUKUS demonstration, and the boundary conditions of the framework.

What I hope to contribute to the department, beyond the thesis itself, is a research programme that connects two strands of LSE Philosophy which currently run beside each other rather than together: Professor Alexander's evolutionary modelling of coordination and Professor Bradley's decision theory under severe uncertainty. The thesis draws on both, and the formal object it develops is intended to be a shared tool rather than a private one. If the programme succeeds, the apparatus is not specific to defence coordination. It generalises to climate governance, AI regulation and any domain where collective action has been considered theoretically intractable because no common payoff structure can be constructed. The intellectual stakes extend beyond the philosophy seminar room. The department's PPE-adjacent culture is the right environment in which to develop them.

I am applying full-time and have made the arrangements needed to commit to the programme without external constraints on attention.
