# Research Proposal: Co-ordination Without Convergence: Navigating Partial Commensurability in Collective Interdependence

**Applicant:** Samuel Norton
**Degree:** MPhil/PhD in Philosophy, London School of Economics

## The Problem

Game theory assumes you can build a payoff matrix. What happens when you can't? Evolutionary game theory relaxes the dynamics but inherits the assumption: outcomes must be representable in a common payoff structure. When agents hold genuinely incommensurable values, the assumption fails by design, because the representation itself becomes a category error.

The circumstances of agents with incommensurable values have been deemed a management problem to be pragmatically navigated in organisational theory, collaborative governance and ecological economics, rather than a formal condition to be theorised about. It is considered outside the bounds of modelling due to inability to determine a mutual payoff structure. But it is something that appears constantly in the human experience, at both the individual and collective levels. A dispute between indigenous land rights and economic development where the very concept of what land is differs categorically. A nation that defines security as territorial sovereignty negotiating with one that defines it as economic interdependence.

Alexander (2024) identifies this gap from the diagnostic side, observing that rehabilitating the Open Society "involves global comparisons and trade-offs along dimensions not obviously comparable" (p. 16). The formal apparatus that would make such comparisons tractable is not in his diagnostic work, nor in the literature it engages.

In reality, fully commensurable and fully incommensurable values represent the extremes, the boundaries, and the space between represents the vast majority of existing conditions. On an individual level, this has been formalised, and continues to be explored in decision making under deep uncertainty amongst other fields, but no comparable formal account exists for collective coordination under these conditions. Collective coordination defaults to game-theoretic foundations, which assume aligned payoff structures can be established.

This thesis investigates what grounds coordination when they cannot.

## Research Question

Value commensurability between agents is not binary but domain-specific and graduated: within any given domain, agents may fully share evaluative frameworks, hold genuinely incommensurable values, or occupy the more common middle ground of partial but incomplete commensurability.

**Central Research Question:** Given that value convergence is neither sufficient nor necessary for coordination, can structural features of interdependence in domains of partial commensurability provide formal foundations for collective coordination in domains where it fails?

**Formal hypothesis:** a configuration space with structural features and evaluative commitments as axes, where stability is defined by a structural equilibrium concept (Nash generalised to partial orderings over position-vectors), preserves the structural analogues of State Dominance and Pareto while replacing impartial evaluation with situated structural assessment. The candidate structural features are shared risk topology, shared constraints, network entanglement and procedural commitments. The research tests whether this weaker mathematical object is tractable, whether these features reduce to a common formal account or require distinct treatment, and whether it yields non-trivial stability results. The central technical risk is that a structural equilibrium concept over partial orderings may not admit existence results comparable to Nash's; an early phase of the research would establish existence conditions before pursuing characterisation.

## Gap Analysis

Olson demonstrated that shared interests do not reliably produce collective action. Ostrom and Schelling demonstrated that coordination can emerge without shared values, through institutional design and structural salience respectively. Taken together, these results suggest that value convergence is neither sufficient nor necessary for coordination. Yet the question of what structural features actually ground coordination when convergence is absent remains formally unaddressed, despite this describing the conditions under which most real-world collective coordination actually occurs.

Evolutionary game theory models multi-agent coordination as a dynamic, adaptive process, but it inherits game theory's foundational requirement: a constructible payoff structure. Alexander's framework models how cooperative norms emerge and stabilise in structured populations, but the agents in those models are playing a defined game with numerical payoffs. The Hawk-Dove game with its fixed value V, for instance: the model appears to depend on V being a number that both players agree represents the value of the resource. What if they don't?

Alexander's own corpus brackets this thesis. His evolutionary game theory, where morality is "a social technology for solving the interdependent decision problems which arise from people's social existence" (Alexander 2007, cited in Alexander 2024, p. 52), supplies the formal tradition. His diagnostic in *The Open Society as an Enemy* argues that piecemeal social engineering converges to local optima on a fitness landscape and calls for multifaceted treatment, but supplies no apparatus to navigate that landscape. This thesis develops it.

Gaus (2021) develops the most direct adjacent formal programme on the combination of axes this thesis engages: open-society topic, formal model of coordination among diverse moral perspectives, normative-ideal target. His Self-Organization Model in §20 introduces a comprehensive utility function U_A(R) = μ_A(R) × w_A(n)(R) combining inherent moral evaluation with a reconciliation weighting, and treats every option in the eligible set as a Nash equilibrium. The model is an explicit cardinalisation of an earlier ordinal model — Gaus's own Deliberative Model in §19.3, which uses a quasi-transitive "Pareto or Indifference" rule (b≻c & c~a does not imply b≻a, p. 145 fn 14). Gaus flags the cost of the cardinalisation himself in §20 footnote 3 (p. 147): "this is simply a cardinalization of the original ordinal model... And so it requires completeness of orderings, something I did not assume in the ordinal model." The cardinal move is forced by the multiplicative form: ordinal data does not support scalar products. This thesis returns to the structural territory of §19.3 — quasi-transitive, partial-order-tolerant — and proposes formal machinery (partial orderings over position-vectors via dominance cones, with a vector-valued equilibrium concept) that preserves the reconciliation dynamic Gaus formalises in §20 without requiring cardinalisation. Where Gaus's §20 deliberately accepts completeness for the multiplicative form, this thesis treats the absence of completeness as the load-bearing structural feature of partial commensurability. The two programmes share the target — rational coordination among agents who do not share evaluative frameworks — and diverge on the formal object: Gaus's cardinal Self-Organization Model versus this thesis's vector-valued generalisation of his earlier ordinal Deliberative Model.

Decision theory under severe uncertainty handles individual rationality when standard expected utility breaks down. In "Impartial Evaluation under Ambiguity" (2022), Bradley proves that three weak conditions (Outcome Anonymity, State Dominance and Pareto for Equivalent Outcomes) force restrictive results on impartial social evaluation: homogeneity of individual betterness, ambiguity neutrality and strong separability. In Section VI he flags incomplete betterness relations as unexplored territory "left for another day". This thesis proposes that partial orderings over structural position-vectors are the formal analogue of those incomplete betterness relations, translated from well-being prospects to situated structural assessment. The coordination space aims to preserve the structural analogues of State Dominance and Pareto while replacing impartial evaluation with situated assessment, a move intended to avoid the homogeneity and ambiguity-neutrality results that Bradley proves are forced by impartiality. Whether it succeeds in doing so is a central formal question.

Bratman's influential account of shared cooperative activity grounds coordination in shared intentions and meshing sub-plans. The cases this thesis addresses involve agents who do not necessarily share values nor intentions but who are structurally entangled in ways that make coordination rational from within each agent's own perspective. If structural interdependence can ground coordination independently of shared intention, this would also illuminate why Bratman's conditions succeed when they do: not because shared intentions are the ground, but because they co-occur with structural features that are.

Addressing this gap requires tools from both traditions: evolutionary game theory's account of how coordination structures emerge and decision theory's formal apparatus for reasoning under severe uncertainty to model multi-agent coordination.

## Methodology

This thesis employs analytical philosophy as its primary mode of inquiry, but the central claim, that structural features of interdependence can ground coordination under conditions of partial value commensurability, requires formal demonstration. Game-theoretic and decision-theoretic modelling will be used to test whether coordination equilibria can be characterised when standard payoff structures are unavailable.

The formal programme targets characterisation results: identifying conditions under which the candidate structural features are individually or jointly sufficient to generate stable coordination equilibria, and whether any such features are necessary.

A working assumption of the proposal is that the axes of the configuration space are not orthogonal. Alexander's argument that the four conceptions of the Open Society are causally interconnected implies that structural features and evaluative commitments interact: moving along one axis changes the position-dependence of others. In mathematical terms, the space likely requires a position-dependent metric tensor rather than a constant one, closer to Riemannian geometry than Euclidean. Whether the geometry is continuously variable or shifts discretely between cooperative and adversarial regimes is itself an open question the research will address.

The primary case study domain for this thesis will be multi-lateral defence coordination for testing the formal claims against real-world coordination under partial commensurability. The UK, USA and Australia share catastrophic security risks through arrangements such as AUKUS, yet operate under different strategic doctrines, different democratic constraints on military deployment, and different definitions of what regional security requires. They coordinate effectively in practice despite lacking a common payoff structure, making this domain a natural test case for coordination grounded in structural interdependence rather than value convergence.

The case selection raises methodological questions about confirmation bias. The formal results will therefore be tested for generalisability against at least one coordination domain in which I hold no operational experience, most likely climate governance or AI regulation, to establish whether the structural features identified in the defence case generalise or prove domain-specific.

## Expected Contribution

This thesis will deliver characterisation results for the proposed structural equilibrium concept, identifying conditions under which coordination is stable. It will give mathematical content to the fitness landscape metaphor Alexander (2024) uses to argue for multifaceted social engineering, providing a tool for evaluating alternative institutional configurations along multiple axes simultaneously without requiring commensurable utilities. The thesis will include a demonstration of the formalism using multi-lateral defence coordination.

The formal account is intended to generalise beyond defence to climate governance, AI regulation, international financial coordination and other domains where agents share structural interdependence but not values. If coordination without convergence can be formally grounded, the implications extend to any context where collective action currently stalls on the assumption that it requires either shared values or aligned self-interest.

The thesis will also identify the boundary conditions of this approach: the degree of structural entanglement below which interdependence cannot ground coordination, and the conditions, if any, under which value divergence is sufficiently total that no structural feature provides purchase. Establishing where the framework fails is as important as establishing where it succeeds.
