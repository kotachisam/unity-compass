# Fukushima Daiichi

**Scale:** National / International
**Verdict:** Failure (coordination), with lessons
**Referenced in:** [Where It Fails](../03-where-it-fails.md)

## Summary

The 2011 Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster resulted from a magnitude 9.0 earthquake and subsequent tsunami overwhelming a nuclear power station whose sea wall was inadequate for the threat. The Kurokawa Report concluded that the disaster was "profoundly man-made" — the result of institutional failures in oversight, risk communication and regulatory independence.

## Why It Matters for the Framework

A textbook case of multiple failure modes compounding: information distortion (TEPCO's own 2006 modelling predicted the threat but the information never produced action), the accountability void (regulator and operator each deferring to the other), capture dynamics (TEPCO's relationship with regulators described as a "safety myth"), and speed mismatch (institutional processes taking years to respond to known risks).

## Key Patterns

- Information distortion: known risk data filtered through institutional culture until it became theoretical rather than operational
- Capture: the boundary between regulator and regulated had dissolved
- Path dependence: the 5.7m sea wall was a legacy design decision that institutional inertia prevented from being revised despite new evidence
- Post-crisis coordination: the remediation effort itself became a case study in multi-stakeholder coordination under extreme pressure
