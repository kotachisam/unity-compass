**Decision theory under severe uncertainty** addresses how agents should make rational choices when the conditions required by standard expected utility theory do not hold: when probabilities cannot be assigned, outcomes cannot be fully enumerated, or the decision model itself may be fundamentally wrong.

## The uncertainty spectrum

Frank Knight's foundational distinction (1921): **risk** involves known probability distributions (rolling a die). **Uncertainty** involves unknown probabilities but known possible outcomes. **Deep uncertainty** (sometimes called severe uncertainty or radical uncertainty) involves unknown outcomes: agents cannot even enumerate what might happen, let alone assign probabilities.

The coordination challenges the investigation addresses operate in the deep uncertainty regime.

## [[Richard Bradley]]'s framework

Bradley's *Decision Theory with a Human Face* brings together work on decision under ambiguity, belief revision and social choice. His paper "Social Ethics under Ambiguity" (2022) evaluates social prospects from an impartial standpoint when ambiguity prevents standard expected utility calculation.

The gap the investigation identifies: Bradley's framework evaluates from an external, impartial standpoint. Coordination requires agents to act from within their own partial perspectives. The distance between impartial evaluation and situated coordination is where the thesis operates.

## Relevance to the investigation

Decision theory under severe uncertainty is central to sub-question 3 of the research proposal: what does rational coordination look like for agents who share [[Structural Interdependence]] but not values?

If structural features of interdependence can ground coordination without value convergence, what decision-theoretic framework applies to agents reasoning about whether and how to coordinate under those conditions? Standard expected utility cannot do this work because it requires the probability assignments and outcome rankings that severe uncertainty and [[Value Incommensurability]] deny.

## Related approaches

- **Robust decision-making** (Lempert et al. 2003): strategies that perform adequately across the widest range of possible futures
- **Info-gap decision theory** (Ben-Haim 2006): decisions where the uncertainty is about the model itself
- **Prospect theory** (Kahneman and Tversky 1979): how actual human decision-making deviates from expected utility under risk
