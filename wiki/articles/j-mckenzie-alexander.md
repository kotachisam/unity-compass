**J. McKenzie Alexander** is Professor of Philosophy at the London School of Economics and Political Science, where he also directs the BSc in Philosophy, Logic and Scientific Method. His research focuses on [[Evolutionary Game Theory]], the evolution of morality and social norms, computational philosophy and the philosophy of society.

## Research focus

Alexander's work examines how cooperative norms emerge and stabilise in structured populations through evolutionary dynamics. His *The Structural Evolution of Morality* (2007) was the foundational treatment of how moral behaviour can be explained through evolutionary game theory on social networks. His *Evolutionary Game Theory* (2023) is the most recent comprehensive treatment of the field.

His most recent book, *The Open Society as an Enemy* (2024, LSE Press), examines how the concept of open society has shifted in contemporary discourse.

## Relevance to the investigation

Alexander is one of two target supervisors for the LSE MPhil/PhD application. His work is directly relevant to sub-question 2 of the research proposal: under what conditions do coordination equilibria grounded in [[Structural Interdependence]] resist defection, capture and decay?

The investigation's key question for Alexander's framework: his models require a constructible payoff structure. Can his evolutionary stability analysis be extended to agents whose payoffs are genuinely incomparable? If [[Coordination Without Convergence]] can be grounded in structural features, what does evolutionary stability look like in that regime?
