# Where Coordination Works

Coordination across genuine difference does happen. It happens more often than the state of our institutions might suggest. Studying where it succeeds reveals recurring patterns that inform what a better coordination framework needs to include.

This document identifies those patterns. Detailed examinations of individual cases live in the [case studies](./04-case-studies/) directory.

## The Success Factors

Across domains and scales, successful coordination between actors with different values, incentives or worldviews tends to share a common architecture.

**Shared stakes without shared ideology.** The actors involved don't need to agree on values. They need to agree that the consequences of failure are unacceptable to everyone. The Montreal Protocol succeeded because ozone depletion threatened every nation regardless of political system or economic philosophy. AUKUS coordinates three nations with overlapping but distinct strategic interests because the shared threat environment is more compelling than the disagreements. Open-source communities build together because the shared artifact matters more than any contributor's motivation for participating. At the interpersonal scale, restorative justice circles bring together victims and offenders who share nothing except the consequences of a crime. They coordinate not because they agree on values but because the stakes of failing to address the harm are unacceptable to both. In each case, coordination was enabled by shared *stakes*, not shared *beliefs*.

**Distributed intelligence with institutional scaffolding.** The pattern identified in [top-down and bottom-up](01-top-down-bottom-up.md) appears consistently in successful coordination. The intelligence about what's needed comes from the people closest to the problem. The institutional structure provides coherence, quality control and resource mobilisation. Ostrom's extensive fieldwork on commons governance demonstrated this across hundreds of cases: communities that successfully managed shared resources (fisheries, irrigation systems, forests) did so through locally developed rules informed by situated knowledge, held within institutional structures that provided enforcement and dispute resolution.[^1]

**Iterative trust-building rather than comprehensive agreement.** Successful coordination between adversaries or competitors almost never begins with a grand bargain. It begins small, builds trust through repeated interaction, and scales gradually. The US-Soviet arms reduction process didn't start with a proposal to abolish all nuclear weapons. It started with small, verifiable reductions, each one building confidence for the next. The same pattern appears in trade negotiations, peace processes and organisational change. Trying to coordinate everything at once creates a single prisoner's dilemma with all the associated incentives to defect. Breaking it into repeated smaller interactions changes the game entirely.[^2]
The same pattern operates in relationships. The Gottman Institute's four decades of research on couples demonstrates that durable partnerships are built through repeated small bids for connection, not grand declarations of commitment. Each bid answered builds trust for the next. Each one ignored erodes it. The mechanism is identical whether the actors are spouses or superpowers.

**Mechanisms for self-correction.** Coordination that lasts is coordination that can adapt. Static agreements between parties with different interests will eventually fail as conditions change and the original terms no longer serve everyone. Successful coordination builds in feedback loops: ways for participants to flag when the arrangement is breaking down, renegotiate terms and adjust without starting from scratch. The scientific peer review system (when functioning well) does this. Open-source version control does this. The Antarctic Treaty System has survived since 1959 partly because its consultative meeting structure allows ongoing adaptation without requiring renegotiation of the treaty itself. At organisational scale, Agile software development embeds this principle structurally: short sprints, retrospectives after each one, and the explicit expectation that the plan will change as understanding deepens. The methodology was designed precisely because traditional top-down project management failed when requirements were uncertain.

**Transparency of mechanism, not transparency of motive.** Successful coordination doesn't require participants to reveal their true motivations. It requires that the *mechanism* of coordination is legible and predictable. In Axelrod's game theory tournaments, Tit for Tat won partly because it was *clear*: other strategies could understand what it was doing and predict how it would respond.[^3] Opaque strategies, even sophisticated ones, performed poorly because other players couldn't establish trust with something they couldn't read. The same applies at institutional scale: coordination mechanisms need to be transparent in their operation even when the participants' internal motivations remain private.

**Preservation of exit.** Coordination that participants can't leave is coercion, not cooperation. The most durable coordination arrangements preserve each actor's autonomy and right to withdraw. This seems counterintuitive — wouldn't the ability to leave undermine commitment? The evidence suggests the opposite. When actors choose to stay because the arrangement serves them, rather than because they're trapped, the coordination is more resilient and adaptive. The European Union's Article 50 (the mechanism by which the UK left) was designed precisely to ensure that membership remained voluntary. Whatever one thinks of Brexit as policy, the existence of the exit mechanism is what makes continued membership meaningful rather than merely compulsory.

## What These Patterns Share

Every one of these factors connects back to the [capacities identified in the philosophical foundation](../01-core/04-philosophical-foundation.md). Shared stakes without shared ideology requires the capacity to *see through someone else's eyes* — to understand what's at stake for the other party without needing to share their worldview. Iterative trust-building requires *relational orientation* — trust as something built rather than assumed. Self-correction requires *intellectual humility* — the acknowledgment that current arrangements may be incomplete. Transparency of mechanism maps to *holding the tension* — coordinating across genuine difference without requiring it to be resolved.

The success factors are hiding in plain sight. Observable, repeatable and well-documented. The question is why they appear so rarely in the institutions that matter most. That question is addressed in the [next document](03-where-it-fails.md).
