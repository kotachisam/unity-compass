**AUKUS** is a trilateral security partnership between Australia, the United Kingdom and the United States, announced in September 2021. Pillar I involves Australia acquiring nuclear-powered submarines through an unprecedented technology-sharing arrangement. Pillar II covers advanced capabilities including AI, quantum computing, cyber and undersea technologies.

## Why it matters for the investigation

AUKUS is the primary applied domain for the doctoral research proposal. It represents [[Coordination Without Convergence]] at the highest stakes: three nations with overlapping but distinct strategic interests, different defence procurement cultures, different parliamentary oversight structures and different relationships to China, coordinating on nuclear submarine technology.

The three parties share catastrophic security risks (a [[Structural Interdependence]] of the "shared risk topology" type) but operate under different strategic doctrines, different democratic constraints on military deployment and different definitions of what regional security requires. They coordinate effectively in practice despite lacking a common payoff structure.

## Coordination dynamics

Several coordination patterns identified in the investigation's landscape analysis are visible in AUKUS:

- **Shared stakes without shared ideology**: the shared threat environment is more compelling than the disagreements
- **Iterative trust-building**: phased implementation with multiple decision points
- **Institutional scaffolding**: formal governance structures to manage trilateral coordination
- **Different strategic cultures**: requiring coordination across genuine institutional difference

## Author's note

The author's defence consulting experience included work in the AUKUS context. This provides operational grounding that most philosophical work on coordination theory lacks, while also raising methodological questions about confirmation bias that the research proposal addresses explicitly.

## Open questions

- How does trilateral coordination manage the tension between speed (security urgency) and due process (democratic oversight)?
- What coordination mechanisms handle the differences in national strategic culture?
- How transferable are the coordination lessons from a security context to other domains?
