# The Open Society as an Enemy

Alexander's most recent book. Examines how the concept of open society has shifted in contemporary discourse. Published by LSE Press, fully open access.

## Why it matters

This is the best window into what Alexander is currently thinking about. If the investigation targets him as primary supervisor, this book reveals his intellectual priorities circa 2024. The relationship between open society, coordination and political openness is directly adjacent to the investigation's central question.

## Summary

346-page diagnostic of how the Open Society underwent value-inversion post-Cold War. Four conceptions examined: cosmopolitan (borders/migration), transparent (privacy/surveillance), Enlightenment (free exchange of ideas), communitarian (polarisation/tribalism). Each shown to be perceived as enemy rather than virtue. Final chapter proposes "multifaceted social engineering" as third way between Popper's piecemeal approach and utopian engineering. Uses fitness landscape metaphor from evolutionary biology. No formal apparatus anywhere in the book.

## Tracking question: diagnostic or prescriptive?

Overwhelmingly diagnostic. The prescriptive content in the final chapter amounts to policy wish-lists (eliminate gerrymandering, regulate social media, open borders gradually) and a fitness landscape metaphor that is never formalised. No game-theoretic models, no decision-theoretic framework, no equilibrium concepts, no mathematical objects. Strikingly, no engagement with his own field's formal tools (no replicator dynamics, no spatial structure, no Nowak and May). The book diagnoses exactly the problem the coordination space addresses and offers no formal tool for it.

## Notes for supervision pitch email

### The core framing

Alexander has diagnosed the coordination failure of open societies across 346 pages. The book identifies that the problem is multifaceted (his word), that piecemeal approaches get stuck at local optima, and that "global comparisons and trade-offs along dimensions not obviously comparable" are needed (p. 16). But he provides no formal object for making those comparisons. The proposed research would build one.

Tense: "you've diagnosed X acutely; I believe this gap admits formalisation, and I'm proposing to spend a PhD finding out." NOT "I've built the tool" or "I'm building the tool." The pitch is: I see where the formal object should go, I can articulate what properties it would need to have, and I want to develop it under supervision. Supervisors want to supervise research, not rubber-stamp conclusions.

### Five specific connection points (with page references)

1. **His fitness landscape is the thesis's configuration space.** He uses Wright's fitness landscape (pp. 307-309) as metaphor for why societies get stuck at suboptimal arrangements. But the axes are undefined, fitness is undefined, and navigation is undefined. The coordination space formalises this: structural features as axes, structural fitness as height, structural equilibrium as the stability concept. The thesis proposes to give his metaphor mathematical content.

2. **His Rorschach concepts are the thesis's Layer 2.** His central diagnostic (pp. 10-11): political concepts succeed precisely because they're semantically empty vessels onto which individuals project. This is the subjective perception of structure (Layer 2). The coordination space explains WHY Rorschach concepts coordinate without convergence: they permit structural alignment at Layer 1 while accommodating divergent projection at Layer 2.

3. **His "multifaceted social engineering" is navigation through structural equilibria.** He identifies that solving intertwined problems requires simultaneous multi-dimensional adjustment, not gradient-following (pp. 307-309). Structural equilibrium (Nash generalised to partial orderings over position-vectors) is the stability concept for exactly this kind of multi-dimensional landscape. The thesis proposes a formal basis for evaluating when a configuration is stable and what transitions are available.

4. **His moral pluralism provides the philosophical warrant.** "Morality as social technology for interdependent decision problems" (p. 52, citing Alexander 2007) is the normative foundation the thesis builds on. If morality solves interdependent decision problems within societies, the coordination space is the representation of the problem space across societies with varying degrees of moral alignment. Structural fitness replaces utility precisely because utility presupposes a shared evaluative standard that moral pluralism denies.

5. **His "dimensions not obviously comparable" are the thesis's partial orderings.** The admission (p. 16) that rehabilitating the Open Society "involves global comparisons and trade-offs along dimensions not obviously comparable" is almost a direct statement of the mathematical problem. Bradley (2022) showed that impartial evaluation under ambiguity forces restrictive results. The thesis proposes that partial orderings over structural position-vectors ARE the incomplete betterness relations Bradley left as an open question, making those "not obviously comparable" dimensions formally tractable without forcing commensurability.

### Tone guidance for the email

- Acknowledge the diagnosis as acute, not claim to improve on it
- Position the thesis as building the formal complement to his diagnostic work
- Reference his own 2007 book (structural evolution of morality) as the bridge between the diagnostic book and the formal proposal
- Reference the SEP article (section 6.1) where he identifies the gap in evolutionary game theory that structural fitness addresses
- Frame the relationship as: "your diagnostic work identifies the problem space; your game-theoretic work provides the mathematical tradition; the proposed research connects the two"
- Do NOT claim to have built the tool or to know it works. Claim to believe the gap admits formalisation, to have identified what properties the formal object would need, and to be proposing to spend 3-4 years developing it under supervision. The honesty is the pitch: "I think this is feasibly formalisable and I want to find out"

- Empirical grounding (every chapter marshals data)

## Notes

### On the introduction (pp. 7-11): Rorschach concepts and common meanings

Taylor (1971) distinguishes intersubjective meaning (shared language enabling coordination) from common meaning (shared reference world creating community). The critical line: common meaning is "more than convergence" because "this sharing must be shared." Common meanings can also "subsist with a high degree of cleavage."

Alexander builds Rorschach concepts from this: concepts whose common meaning depends on individual projection. MAGA and Brexit as paradigm cases. A crowd endorses "Make America Great Again" without a single policy commanding a majority (p. 9). The concept coordinates at the level of abstract shared desire ("things should be better") while permitting total divergence on what "better" means.

Thesis connection: Taylor's "sharing being shared" is meta-coordination. Not shared values but shared orientation within the same space. The coordination space formalises this: agents in a common configuration space, aware others occupy it too, without requiring convergent positions. The question the thesis adds: WHEN does a Rorschach concept sustain structural equilibrium, and when does policy specificity force trade-offs that expose the projection as hollow? The transition from coordination to cleavage is characterisable structurally.

Note: Layer 2 (subjective perception) isn't irrelevant to Layer 1 (structural stability). Agents act on perceptions, and those actions reshape structure. A farmer who believes tariffs serve national greatness may support policy that destroys their structural position. The lag between Layer 2 perception and Layer 1 reckoning is part of the dynamics to investigate.

### On the introduction (pp. 16-17): rejecting piecemeal, the pitch paragraph

The most important passage in the book for the supervision pitch: "The rehabilitation requires a philosophical enquiry into the very kind of society we want to create. It requires answering questions such as: what freedoms do we wish to protect, and what freedoms are we willing to give up in order to make other forms of social organisation possible? These questions cannot be solved in a piecemeal fashion, for they involve global comparisons and trade-offs along dimensions not obviously comparable." (p. 16)

"Dimensions not obviously comparable" is almost a specification for partial orderings over position-vectors. The coordination space is the proposed formal tool for making exactly these comparisons without forcing commensurability. The pitch leads here: "you stated this problem on page 16; I'm proposing to build the formal object that addresses it."

Alexander also identifies the externality problem: piecemeal solutions, each individually reasonable, generate negative externalities when combined (p. 17). This is the multifaceted social engineering argument he develops in Ch. 29. The coordination space's multi-axis structure represents this explicitly: a change along one axis has consequences for stability along others.

### On the introduction (pp. 15-16): polarity, co-held dimensions, moralised knowledge

Alexander assumes the Open Society is net good being disguised as enemy (Baudelaire line, p. 15). The thesis's starting position is more honest: any coordination arrangement has polarity. The coordination space doesn't presuppose a given arrangement is good. It characterises structural stability and for whom.

On free movement, Alexander notes moral AND economic considerations matter (p. 15). The broader point: policy discourse almost never holds moral and economic dimensions simultaneously. The coordination space holds multiple axes by design. This is a feature of the formal object, not a footnote.

On ideational harm and moralisation of knowledge (p. 16): when structural facts become unsayable because they conflict with group identity, Layer 2 constrains what's admissible at Layer 1. The feedback loop between perception and structure breaks. Connects to the open question about L1/L2 dynamics and lag structure.

### On the introduction (pp. 14-16): value-inversion and the concept/implementation gap

Alexander says the doubt about the Open Society is "unfortunate" (p. 15). He means: people are rejecting the concept because of the implementation. His book rehabilitates the concept.

Disagreement: a concept of the Open Society that can't specify the structural preconditions for its own realisation is incomplete AS A CONCEPT, not just poorly implemented. Individual freedom, self-determination and freedom of association presuppose material conditions (economic stability, time, security) that the concept doesn't account for. When those conditions erode (wealth concentration, precarity, inflation), the concept becomes formally exercisable but practically hollow. The doubters aren't misperceiving. They're responding rationally to structural failure that the concept has no apparatus to diagnose.

Thesis connection: the coordination space doesn't start from "the Open Society is good." It starts from "what structural conditions make any coordination arrangement sustainable, and for whom?" That's more honest because it doesn't presuppose the answer. Alexander wants to rehabilitate one arrangement. The thesis builds the tool for evaluating any arrangement, including configurations Alexander might not endorse.

### On the introduction (pp. 13-14): existentialist foundation and the consistency principle

Alexander grounds his Open Society in four elements: individual freedom, self-determination, freedom of association, and a consistency principle. The first three are existentialist. The fourth is defined in Ch. 4 (p. 50): members of a community must not thwart the core freedoms that allowed the community to form. Essentially Popper's paradox of tolerance as a formal principle.

Flag: the consistency principle has a bootstrapping problem. Who determines which freedoms were "core" to the community's formation? The community that already holds power. Assess this properly in Ch. 4.

The closing line: "If people freely choose, on an informed basis, to reject a life based on a scientific attitude, who am I to judge?" This is value pluralism taken to its logical conclusion and marks a genuine break from Popper's critical rationalism. It also connects to the thesis: the coordination space doesn't require agents to share epistemic commitments, only structural interdependence.

### On the introduction (pp. 13-14): two breaks from Popper

Alexander rejects Popper on two grounds. First, Popper's Open Society privileges democracy; Alexander's defends value pluralism. Second, Popper's critical rationalism assumes "we may get nearer to the truth." Alexander: truth about the natural world, fine. Truth about the social world, whose truth? In a diverse world with competing value systems, moral codes and conceptions of rational action, convergence toward truth is the wrong model.

Thesis connection: this natural/social distinction maps onto L1/L2. Structural features (risk, entanglement, constraints) are closer to natural-world facts: observer-independent, operative regardless of belief. Values and meaning are social-world territory: partly constituted by what agents believe. The coordination space's claim is that enough of the coordination problem sits at L1 to ground stability even when L2 is substantially divergent. You can't falsify a way of life, but you can assess the structural stability of the arrangement that sustains it.

### On the preface: synthetic philosophy as methodological alignment

Alexander explicitly rejects analytic philosophy's convention of isolating problems. Calls his own work "synthetic philosophy": weaving threads across disciplines, where the whole exceeds the parts. This is the methodological label for the thesis. The coordination space is synthetic: it doesn't extend game theory OR decision theory OR value theory in isolation. It synthesises structural features from all three into a formal object none produces alone.

Supervision signal: Alexander will not penalise cross-disciplinary ambition if it's rigorous. He will penalise narrowness that misses the tapestry.

### On Chapter 4 (pp. 45-53): existentialism, sedimentation, moral pluralism

The philosophical core. Builds political cosmopolitanism from Beauvoir's existentialism: individual freedom, freedom of association, consistency principle. Morality understood as "social technology for solving interdependent decision problems" (p. 52). Sedimentation (from Merleau-Ponty via Beauvoir) explains why values resist revision without denying metaphysical freedom to revise them.

Sedimentation and structural equilibrium have the same shape. Agents are metaphysically free to defect from a coordination arrangement, but accumulated structural interdependence (risk exposure, entanglement, procedural commitments) makes defection costly. Sedimentation is to individual projects what structural stability is to coordination arrangements. Freedom is real but not frictionless. Open question: is this analogy formal (the cost structures are mathematically equivalent) or merely poetic (they share a family resemblance but operate through different mechanisms)?

### On Chapter 7 (pp. 79-80): states as configurations, migration as feedback

Key line (p. 80): "the division of the world into states would result in a proper group competition, with successful states being those where people choose to live and unsuccessful states being those where people flee."

Alexander is describing the coordination space as a selection mechanism informally. Whether he sees the connection to his own formal tradition but chose not to formalise it here is an open question. States as configurations, migration as feedback signal, structural fitness revealed by which configurations attract and retain agents. Evolutionary dynamics applied to political arrangements, using his own field's logic, but informally.

The single-axis critique is also here implicitly: democratic capitalism optimises on one axis (economic growth) while ignoring others (community, meaning, equality, sustainability). That's gradient-following on a single dimension. It converges to a local optimum that looks good on GDP and terrible on everything else. The coordination space holds multiple axes by design.

### On Chapter 29 (pp. 303-305): Popper's objections, Alexander's refutation, the timescale problem

Popper's three objections to Utopian social engineering: it requires authoritarian power to overcome resistance, it requires sustained commitment across generations of leaders who may disagree, and it requires factual knowledge we don't possess.

Alexander's refutation (pp. 304-305): Popper's third objection is a modal claim ("we cannot possess such knowledge") that collapses into a contingent one when interrogated. Popper says the knowledge can't be had because we lack practical experience, and all knowledge is based on experience. But what if we sought the experience? The impossibility claim isn't counterfactually robust. Popper has shown that utopian social engineering depends on three contingent conditions, not that it's impossible.

The timescale problem: Popper's first two objections become acute under modern democratic incentive structures. Politicians on 4-year cycles can't credibly commit to projects that pay off in 40. Voters on similar cycles can't credibly demand them. The institutional architecture of democratic capitalism is gradient-following on short time horizons. The 4-year cycle was designed (by the framers, with Washington's two-term norm later codified) to prevent tyranny. It was a solution to one structural problem that has sedimented into a constraint on solving another (long-horizon collective action). Solutions become constraints.

Thesis implication: characterising alternative configurations formally gives people something concrete that outlasts individual politicians. The formal object becomes a reference point that survives the timescale problem. Not a solution, but a necessary precondition for one.

### On Chapter 29 (pp. 302-303): the diagnosis summary that maps onto the coordination space

"A defence of the Open Society means doing things differently on a crowded planet home to eight billion people, living unsustainably. What that entails requires collective discussion and thinking about the kind of world we want to create and live in and how to get there from here. And that, I argue, requires thinking about social engineering from a multifaceted, holistic perspective."

Translation: characterise the structural configuration we currently occupy, identify which alternative configurations are reachable from here, evaluate their stability properties along multiple axes simultaneously. That is the coordination space, stated informally. Alexander then tees up Popper as the obstacle to formalising it. The formal vacuum opens immediately after.

### On Chapter 29 "The finest hour" opening (pp. 320-321): empirical case for interdependence, no formalisation

Alexander weaves the four conceptions together through specific causal loops: social media addiction drives anonymous communication which reinforces modern tribes via stereotypes, which creates demand for safe spaces, which produces echo chambers, which facilitate populist rejection of cosmopolitanism. This is the empirical case for why the problem is multifaceted, not an axiomatic assertion. The loops justify rejecting piecemeal approaches with concrete mechanisms rather than appeals to holism.

But the loops are described qualitatively only. No rates, no thresholds, no conditions under which feedback strengthens or weakens. Implication for the thesis: the four conceptions are not orthogonal axes, they have correlation structure. The coordination space's axes almost certainly aren't independent either. Whether the dependencies are characterisable formally is an open question the thesis must address.

### On Chapter 29 (pp. 314-315): the regulatory analogy as a structural intervention argument

Alexander extends an accepted regulatory principle: if we restrict advertising that targets basic physical needs in harmful ways (tobacco, junk food), the same principle should apply when companies target higher-order cognitive and emotional needs. The asymmetry is indefensible once named.

Three reasons this matters for the thesis:

1. **Consistency argument.** It uses an existing social consensus (we regulate harmful advertising) and extends it by identifying a structural similarity the current discourse misses. Not "we should be more enlightened" but "we should apply the rules we already have consistently."

2. **Structural intervention, not epistemic.** Alexander isn't asking people to reason better about their cognitive needs. He's asking the institutional architecture to prevent certain manipulations from being profitable. This is exactly Option 3 from the open questions: structure operates regardless of perception, and institutional design that makes harm visible and costly is more effective than philosophical argument.

3. **Layer 2 manipulation addressable at Layer 1.** The tobacco precedent shows how structural intervention can address manipulation that individuals can't reliably detect themselves. Social media is currently in the "manufactured doubt" phase that tobacco companies used for decades.

The open question Alexander doesn't answer: what structural features formally distinguish a practice warranting regulation from one that doesn't? He makes the analogy and moves on. The coordination space could provide the formal criterion: practices that systematically exploit asymmetries between Layer 2 perception and Layer 1 wellbeing, with structural feedback loops that make the harm invisible or delayed, warrant structural intervention. That's a tractable formalisation question, not a values argument.

Practitioner adjacent: Tristan Harris (Center for Humane Technology, former Google design ethicist) diagnoses the same pattern from the product design side. Not academic, but the diagnosis converges with Alexander's from a different vantage point.

### On Alexander's "multifaceted social engineering" as a unique term (PhilPapers search, 2026-04-10)

Exact-phrase search for "multifaceted social engineering" on PhilPapers returns zero results. The broader "multifaceted social engineering" (non-exact) returns 970 hits, but none use the term as a specific framework; most are either the cybersecurity sense of social engineering (phishing, manipulation) or broad matches on both words appearing independently. Alexander coined the term himself.

Implications for the thesis:
1. No competing literature to engage with. The thesis isn't entering a crowded field; it's picking up a term with no existing usage and giving it formal content.
2. The closest adjacent philosophical lineage is the Popperian "piecemeal social engineering" tradition (Rhees 1947, Støvring 2014 on the conservative critique, Argamakova 2017/2022 on the historical concept). None of these formalise.
3. The formal vacuum is empirically confirmed. If anyone had formalised Alexander's idea, it would have shown up in the search. It didn't.
4. For the supervision pitch: the thesis can frame itself as the first attempt to formalise a term Alexander uses informally. That's a cleaner positioning than "extending existing work on multifaceted social engineering."

### On Chapter 29 (p. 301): the concession that critical rationalism fails

"Underlying these attacks, in many cases, are aspects of human psychology that are deeply hardwired and that short-circuit appeals to reason and evidence... Fear is a powerful determinant of human behaviour, even when it isn't rational."

This is a significant admission from a member of the Department of Philosophy, Logic and Scientific Method. Alexander is conceding that critical rationalism — the foundation Popper built the Open Society on — fails in the domains where it most needs to work. People don't update beliefs based on evidence on the issues that matter most. They're moved by fear, in-group loyalty, status anxiety, the desire for strong leaders.

Thesis warrant: this is the philosophical justification for prioritising structural over epistemic interventions. If you can't reason people out of positions they didn't reason themselves into, you have to change the structure within which they're positioned. Institutional design that makes structural reality legible faster is more effective than philosophical argument. The thesis's prescriptive output (option 3 from open questions) follows from Alexander's own concession that option 1 doesn't work.

### On Chapter 29: the formal vacuum

The concluding chapter is where the gap is widest. Alexander:
- Rejects Popper's piecemeal approach as insufficient (false dichotomy with utopianism)
- Proposes "multifaceted social engineering" as third way
- Uses fitness landscape as metaphor but never defines axes, fitness function, or navigation method
- Admits the policy recommendations are starting points, not solutions
- Explicitly states (p. 324) "practical policy recommendations will require people far more knowledgeable and capable than I am"

This is the opening. He has diagnosed the problem, identified the shape of the solution (multifaceted, landscape-based, not piecemeal), and explicitly acknowledged he lacks the formal tools. The thesis proposes to provide them.

### On the absence of his own formal tools

The most striking feature of the book: Alexander never deploys evolutionary game theory, spatial models, replicator dynamics, or any of the apparatus from his academic career. This 346-page diagnostic could have been written by a political philosopher with no formal training. Why?

Possible readings:
1. He considers the formal tools inadequate to the scale of the problem (which would validate the thesis's claim that something new is needed)
2. He's writing for a general audience and chose accessibility over formalism
3. He sees a gap between his formal work and his normative commitments that he hasn't bridged

Reading 1 is the strongest for the supervision pitch. The thesis proposes the bridge between his diagnostic ambitions (this book) and his formal capabilities (the EGT work).

### On Rorschach concepts as coordination devices

Alexander's most original contribution. A Rorschach concept is a concept whose common meaning is "heavily dependent on the subjective understanding of the individual" (p. 10). It coordinates without convergence: a crowd endorses "Make America Great Again" without any single unambiguous policy commanding a majority (p. 9).

This is a direct empirical illustration of the thesis's core claim. Coordination doesn't require convergence, just shared navigation principles. The Rorschach concept IS the shared navigation principle. The coordination space explains why it works: the concept aligns agents at the structural level (Layer 1: we're all pointing the same direction on the compass) while permitting complete divergence at the evaluative level (Layer 2: we each project different content onto what "great" means).

The thesis adds what Alexander doesn't: a formal account of WHEN Rorschach concepts produce stable coordination (structural equilibrium) and when they collapse into incoherence (the concept can't sustain alignment when structural realities force trade-offs).
