**Parity** is a fourth evaluative relation introduced by [[Ruth Chang]] in "The Possibility of Parity" (*Ethics* 112(4), 2002, pp. 659-688; [doi:10.1086/339673](https://doi.org/10.1086/339673)). Two items are on a par when they are comparable in a meaningful sense but none of the standard three relations (better, worse, equal) holds between them. Parity challenges the trichotomy assumption that has historically structured value theory: that any two comparable items must stand in exactly one of better, worse or equal.

## Parity is not indifference

The cleanest way to distinguish parity from equality is the small-improvements test. If an agent is indifferent between options A and B, a small positive change to A should tip the balance: the agent now prefers the sweetened A. If A and B are on a par, a small positive change may leave the parity relation intact. Chang's illustrative pairings (a chessmaster against a philosopher, Mozart against Michelangelo) are of items that can be compared without being equal and without one becoming strictly better when a small sweetener is added to either side. Parity is therefore a substantive relation, not a knife-edge between better and worse.

## Parity is not incommensurability

[[Value Incommensurability]] is the broader phenomenon of values that cannot be ranked on a common scale. Raz, Anderson and Wiggins offer different treatments of it. Parity is a specific, more formally tractable version: values that are comparable without being rankable. The investigation treats parity as the most formally useful of the incommensurability concepts precisely because it preserves comparability, which is what makes it amenable to partial-ordering representations.

## The voluntarist move

Chang's later work on hard choices (Chang 2017, "Hard Choices", *Journal of the American Philosophical Association* 3(1), 1-21; [doi:10.1017/apa.2017.7](https://doi.org/10.1017/apa.2017.7)) develops a voluntarist account of how agents resolve choices under parity. Rather than discovering a pre-existing ranking, the agent constitutes herself through commitment: becoming the kind of person who values X over Y. The investigation's central question extends this move to the collective level: can groups of agents who hold values on a par constitute themselves as coordination-capable through structural commitment, without first resolving the underlying value disagreement?

## Parity in the structural domain

The investigation's current working direction extends parity from evaluative comparisons between options to the structural domain of coordination arrangements. In the working sketch of a coordination manifold with a cone bundle, parity has a formal home as a region of the tangent space at each point. At position x on the configuration manifold, the parity region for agent i is the subset of the tangent space lying outside both i's dominance cone (the directions i endorses as improvements) and the reflection of that cone (the directions i rejects). Moves into the parity region are those i can neither endorse nor reject.

This preserves Chang's key property that parity is a substantive subset of possible comparisons rather than a singleton boundary case. A scalar utility gradient gives you a half-space of "up" directions and no parity region at all. A dominance cone can carve a parity region that is a substantive fraction of the tangent space, which is the relevant contrast.

Whether this geometric picture is adequate to Chang's own definition of parity is an open question. The sketch is a working direction, not a proven formal object. The investigation proposes it, does not yet establish it.

## Parity and Bradley's incomplete betterness

Bradley (2022), "Social Ethics under Ambiguity", flags incomplete betterness relations as unexplored territory in Section VI, noting that "the rational response to lack of evidence or to disagreement among experts is partial suspension of judgment." The investigation proposes that parity (a relation that is neither better, worse, nor equal) and incomplete betterness (a formal preference relation that is not complete) are closely related, and that partial orderings over position-vectors are a natural formal home for both. This connection is a working research claim, not a proven identity. Parity is philosophically motivated; incomplete betterness is formally characterised; aligning them is part of the investigation's programme rather than one of its premises.
