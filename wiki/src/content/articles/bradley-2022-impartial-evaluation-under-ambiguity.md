# Bradley 2022 (Impartial Evaluation under Ambiguity)

## Bibliographic information

Richard Bradley, "Impartial Evaluation under Ambiguity", *Ethics* 132(3), 2022. DOI: [10.1086/718081](https://doi.org/10.1086/718081). An author-hosted PDF is available at Bradley's LSE personal page. The paper was selected as one of the ten best philosophy articles of 2022 by the Philosopher's Annual.

## Core argument

Bradley frames the paper around Harsanyi's concept of an impartial but benevolent rational evaluator who determines what is morally best by ranking social prospects, understood as distributions of well-being over individuals and states. The evaluator is not assumed omniscient and the results are meant to hold under any form of uncertainty: objective probability, subjective probability and ambiguity alike.

Bradley identifies three weak conditions that any reasonable evaluation of this kind should satisfy. Outcome Anonymity requires impartiality, so that the evaluator is indifferent between reshufflings of well-being across individuals. State Dominance requires that if X's outcome is at least as good as Y's in every state, then X is at least as good overall. Pareto for Equivalent Outcomes requires that social betterness supervene positively on individual betterness.

## Impossibility results

Bradley proves that these three conditions jointly force three restrictive consequences. Section III shows that all individual betterness relations must be identical, a homogeneity result that rules out agents reasonably assigning different probabilities under uncertainty. Section IV shows that the evaluator cannot exhibit any sensitivity to ambiguity, an ambiguity-neutrality result that collapses the space Ellsberg-type agents inhabit. Section V shows that the evaluator must satisfy strong separability across states and comply with the Sure-Thing Principle.

The force of the result is that Bradley's three conditions are weaker than any mainstream rationality theory. They are weaker than Bayesian expected utility, weaker than Gilboa-Schmeidler maxmin, weaker than Klibanoff-Marinacci-Mukerji smooth ambiguity, weaker than Quiggin rank-dependent utility, weaker than Tversky-Kahneman prospect theory and weaker than Buchak risk-weighted expected utility. Even these minimal assumptions rule out attitudes that real agents exhibit and that several of these theories are designed to accommodate.

## The trilemma

Section VI frames the result as a trilemma. A theorist who wants to preserve any of the behaviours Bradley's result rules out must reject one of the three conditions: reject ambiguity sensitivity, reject State Dominance or reject Pareto for Equivalent Outcomes. Bradley identifies rejecting Pareto for Equivalent Outcomes as the most promising of the three routes but does not commit firmly to it.

## The door left open

In Section VI Bradley flags incomplete betterness relations as unexplored territory "left for another day". He frames the rational response to lack of evidence or to disagreement among experts as "partial suspension of judgment" rather than forced ranking. This passage is the load-bearing one for the investigation. The thesis proposes that partial orderings over structural position-vectors are incomplete betterness relations translated from well-being prospects to situated structural assessment. Bradley identified the door. The investigation proposes to walk through it.

Section VI cites Levi 1990 ("Pareto Unanimity and Consensus", *Journal of Philosophy* 87) and Danan et al. 2016 ("Robust Social Decisions", *American Economic Review* 106) as prior work on incomplete social betterness. Both have been added to the investigation's reading log.

## How the investigation uses the paper

The coordination space preserves the structural analogues of State Dominance and Pareto while replacing impartial evaluation with situated structural assessment. State Dominance translates into the claim that a configuration better on every structural axis in every state is more stable. Pareto translates into the claim that coordination stability supervenes on individual structural positions. Outcome Anonymity drops out because stability is assessed from each agent's situated position rather than from an impartial standpoint.

The working claim is that this move aims to escape the homogeneity and ambiguity-neutrality results precisely because those results are forced by impartiality. Whether the escape actually goes through, and whether the structural version of the trilemma dissolves or reappears in different form, is a research claim rather than a proven result. The paper's value for the supervision pitch is that it maps the walls of the room the thesis proposes to leave. That makes Bradley a useful critical reader of the formal programme even in the scenario where he is not the primary supervisor.

## Where the paper limits the investigation

Bradley's framework requires well-being to be interpersonally comparable and numerically measurable. Section 2 makes this explicit as assumption 2, with Adler's *Measuring Social Welfare* (2019) cited in a footnote for the measurement methodology. The coordination space does not assume this. The investigation proposes that structural position can be ordered by dominance over position-vectors (candidate axes include risk exposure, entanglement density, constraint burden, procedural commitment strength) without requiring numerical cardinal comparability between agents. This is part of the formal gap between Bradley's framework and the coordination space, and part of what makes the structural translation non-trivial rather than cosmetic.
