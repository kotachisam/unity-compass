## Mathematical Foundations for the Formal Object

Surfaced from Alexander Ch. 29 reading (2026-04-10) and the variable-geometry insight about non-orthogonal axes. The coordination space's mathematical home is likely differential geometry with a position-dependent metric (Riemannian geometry), possibly extended with dynamical systems theory for the strategic-mode bifurcation question. None of these are core to writing the proposal, but they are the correct references for doing the formal work properly. Engineering physics background sufficient for direct engagement.

| Priority | Author | Work | Status | Notes |
|----------|--------|------|--------|-------|
| **P3** | Schutz, B. F. | *Geometrical Methods of Mathematical Physics* (Cambridge UP, 1980) | not started | Standard physicist's bridge text into differential geometry. Builds from scratch with physics intuition. Most direct entry point given engineering physics background. The single most important reference if only one is read. |
| **P3** | Lee, J. M. | *Introduction to Riemannian Manifolds* (2nd ed., Springer, 2018) | not started | Contemporary standard for Riemannian geometry. Pedagogical, exercise-rich, covers metric tensors, geodesics, curvature properly. Read after Schutz. |
| **P3** | Frankel, T. | *The Geometry of Physics: An Introduction* (3rd ed., Cambridge UP, 2011) | not started | Comprehensive treatment for physicists. Differential forms, exterior calculus, Riemannian geometry, fibre bundles. Cherry-pick chapters. Useful if the strategic-mode-as-parameter question requires fibre bundle formalism. |
| **P3** | Strogatz, S. | *Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos* (2nd ed., Westview Press, 2014) | not started | Standard introduction to dynamical systems. Bifurcations, attractors, chaos. Relevant if the coordination space has variable-mode dynamics that bifurcate between cooperative and adversarial regimes. |
| **P4** | Amari, S. | *Information Geometry and Its Applications* (Springer, 2016) | not started | Applies differential geometry to spaces of probability distributions. Relevant if the coordination space models agents as probability distributions over positions rather than fixed points. More technical; the right reference if the thesis goes that direction. |

## Social Engineering as Philosophical Concept

Surfaced from PhilPapers search (2026-04-10). Key finding: the exact phrase "multifaceted social engineering" returns zero results on PhilPapers. Alexander (2024) coined the term himself. Adjacent literature on "social engineering" as a Popperian concept is thin but traceable.

| Priority | Author | Work | Status | Notes |
|----------|--------|------|--------|-------|
| **P3** | Rhees, R. | "Social Engineering" *Mind* 56(224), 1947 | not started | Earliest philosophical response to Popper's concept. Two years after *The Open Society and Its Enemies*. Likely an early critique that may anticipate Alexander's moves. |
| **P3** | Argamakova, A. A. | "History of Social Engineering Theories" *Russian Journal of Philosophical Sciences* 64(7), 2022 | not started | Traces the concept from the 19th century through the Soviet/American contexts. Useful for situating Alexander's usage historically. |
| **P3** | Argamakova, A. A. | "The Practical Tasks of Social Engineering and the Formation of the Social and Human Sciences" *Russian Studies in Philosophy* 55(1), 2017 | not started | Earlier Argamakova piece on the role of social engineering in the formation of social and human sciences. |
| **P3** | Støvring, K. | "The Conservative Critique of the Enlightenment: The Limits of Social Engineering" *The European Legacy* 19(3), 2014 | not started | Traces the Burke-Oakeshott-Scruton-Hayek lineage of conservative critique. Useful for understanding what Alexander is responding to when he rejects Popper's piecemeal approach. |
| **P4** | Ziółkowski, J. | "Polarization as a Social Engineering Mechanism" (in *Constituents of Political Theory*, Peter Lang, 2024) | not started | Flips the framing: polarisation as deliberate engineering, not natural phenomenon. Worth a closer look given how much of Alexander's book is about polarisation. |
