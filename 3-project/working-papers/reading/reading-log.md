## Mathematical Foundations for the Formal Object

Surfaced from Alexander Ch. 29 reading (2026-04-10) and extended during and after the Gaus §20 reading (2026-04-10). The coordination space's mathematical home spans three core traditions plus two supporting layers.

**Core traditions**: (i) differential geometry with a position-dependent metric (Riemannian geometry) for the configuration manifold and the non-orthogonal axes question; (ii) convex analysis and cone theory for the partial-ordering / dominance-cone move that replaces scalar utility; (iii) vector-valued game theory for the structural equilibrium concept generalising Nash to cone orderings.

**Supporting layers**: (iv) dynamical systems theory for the strategic-mode bifurcation question and evolutionary dynamics on the manifold; (v) cooperative game theory for the coalition-reachability half of the structural equilibrium condition.

None of these is a core subject on a standard engineering physics curriculum, so the formalism requires a refresh programme. The computational side of the work (Python scientific stack: NumPy, SciPy, SymPy, CVXPY, geomstats, pymanopt) is substantial enough to supplement pen-and-paper proof throughout, following the Gaus §20 and broader EGT precedent where agent-based and numerical methods are in-tradition for this field. None of these references is core to writing the proposal; they are the correct references for doing the formal work properly once the proposal has been accepted. Entry order is roughly: Boyd & Vandenberghe → Ehrgott → Hofbauer & Sigmund → Schutz → Lee Smooth Manifolds → Boumal for the computational side, with Davey & Priestley as order-theory scaffolding any time the partial-ordering machinery feels wobbly.

## Foundations of the structure / preferences distinction

Surfaced from the literature exploration on 2026-04-10 after the Gaus targeted reading and the geometric sketch raised the concern that "structural features independent of preferences" might not survive contact with the philosophy of science and fact/value literature. These references are the foundational texts on whether there can be a principled distinction between structural facts about a social arrangement and evaluative commitments about it. The literature splits into five broad camps: (i) strong distinction defensible (Searle, Ostrom); (ii) fact/value entanglement (Putnam, Williams); (iii) structural realism as philosophy-of-science middle ground (Worrall, Ladyman); (iv) thick ethical concepts as the sharpest pointed critique (Williams, Väyrynen); (v) technical precedents in economics and decision theory for preference-robust or preference-independent formalism (Ostrom IAD, Bergemann-Morris, Aumann, Dubra-Maccheroni-Ok, Danan et al.).

The honest defensible retreat position for the thesis is conditional observer-independence along Sen's positional-objectivity lines: given that agents share vocabulary for what counts as a downside or an entanglement (from whatever first-order values they each hold), the topology and correlation structure of those features is a fact about the arrangement that does not depend on how any particular agent weights it. This is weaker than "value-neutral structural facts" and stronger than "preferences in a trench coat." None of the references below is proposal-blocking; they become essential if the thesis needs to defend the metric-vs-cones split against a Putnam-informed reviewer or locate itself within the Ostrom / positional-objectivity tradition.

|----------|--------|------|--------|-------|
| **P2** | Ostrom, E. | *Understanding Institutional Diversity* (Princeton UP, 2005) | not started | 6010 citations. The strongest existing technical precedent for treating structural features of institutions as first-class variables that do coordination work independent of individual preferences. Introduces the Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework. Distinct from the 1990 *Governing the Commons* (already listed as read secondary in Foundational Coordination Theory). The closest existing thing to a vindication of the thesis's structural-features-as-first-class-variables move. Arguably the single paper most necessary to engage with before the proposal's defence. The honest response is to adopt a conditional version of the structural-features claim rather than a value-neutral one. |
| **P2** | Searle, J. | *The Construction of Social Reality* (Free Press, 1995 / Penguin, 1997) | not started | 3214 citations. The foundational vocabulary for observer-independent vs observer-relative facts. Important caveat: Searle's own taxonomy distinguishes brute facts (observer-independent, e.g. physical mass) from institutional facts (observer-relative, dependent on collective acceptance, e.g. money, marriage). The thesis's "structural features" are more institutional than brute on Searle's framework, which means Searle gives you the vocabulary but not a licence to claim strong observer-independence. Read chapters 1-2 for the distinction. |
| **P3** | Putnam, H. | *The Collapse of the Fact/Value Dichotomy and Other Essays* (Harvard UP, 2002) | not started | 1150 citations. The general philosophical attack on the fact/value distinction. Read chapter 1 for the core argument. Relevant beyond economics for anticipating any objection to the metric-vs-cones split. The ancestor paper for the Putnam & Walsh 2011 economics application. |
| **P3** | Walsh, V. | "Sen after Putnam" *Review of Political Economy* 15(3), 2003 | not started | 108 citations. Bridges Putnam's critique to Sen's capability approach and indicates how a defensible retreat position might look. Directly relevant to positioning the thesis between Putnam's strong fact/value entanglement claim and the thesis's conditional-observer-independence claim. |
| **P3** | Sen, A. | "Positional Objectivity" *Journal of Philosophy* 90(3), 1993 | not started | The concept the thesis needs for the defensible middle ground: facts that are objective relative to a position rather than relative to preferences. Probably the single most theoretically useful paper for articulating the conditional-observer-independence claim. Semantic Scholar bulk search missed it; get via Google Scholar or JSTOR. |
| **P3** | Ladyman, J. & Ross, D. | *Every Thing Must Go: Metaphysics Naturalized* (Oxford UP, 2007) | not started | 1156 citations. Contemporary structural realism in philosophy of science. Key claim: science gives knowledge of structural/relational features without requiring knowledge of intrinsic properties. Philosophical cover for the thesis's claim that relational facts about a coordination arrangement can be known without committing to the metaphysics of preferences. |
| **P3** | Väyrynen, P. | "Thick Ethical Concepts" *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*, 2016 | not started | 81 citations. The modern overview of thick concepts (Williams lineage) where descriptive and evaluative content entangle inseparably. Relevant for anticipating the sharpest version of the objection: if "risk" and "entanglement" are thick concepts, the metric-vs-cones split collapses. Free online at plato.stanford.edu. Williams's own *Ethics and the Limits of Philosophy* (1985) is the origin point for thick concepts but not strictly necessary if reading Väyrynen first. |

## Existential risk and the catastrophic-categorisation tier

Surfaced from the tiered-defensibility refinement on 2026-04-11 (see methodological-evolution.md, "Tiered defensibility of structural features"). The thesis's Tier 1 claim is that shared risk topology admits a robust observer-independent reading at the catastrophic-to-humanity level because the categorisation is shared across virtually any agent who values their own and their kind's continued existence. The academic literature on existential risk grounds this claim in recognised philosophical territory. None of the entries below is proposal-blocking, but Bostrom 2013 and Ord 2020 are the cleanest citations for the Tier 1 claim and are worth reading at skim level before the proposal goes out.

| Priority | Author | Work | Status | Notes |
|----------|--------|------|--------|-------|
| **P3** | Bostrom, N. | "Existential Risk Prevention as Global Priority" *Global Policy* 4(1), 2013 | not started | The mature statement of the existential risk position. Argues that civilisation-ending threats constitute a category of harm whose recognition does not depend on contested first-order value commitments. The cleanest single citation for the thesis's Tier 1 claim that shared risk topology admits a robust observer-independent reading at the catastrophic level. |
| **P3** | Ord, T. | *The Precipice: Existential Risk and the Future of Humanity* (Bloomsbury, 2020) | not started | Book-length defence of the existential risk research programme. Toby Ord at Oxford. The most readable entry point to the academic literature. Provides cover for citing the catastrophic-categorisation tier claim without engaging the broader Effective Altruism or longtermism political debates. |
| **P4** | Bostrom, N. | "Existential Risks: Analyzing Human Extinction Scenarios and Related Hazards" *Journal of Evolution and Technology* 9, 2002 | not started | The founding paper. Shorter and earlier than the 2013 statement. Useful for tracing the historical arc of the position; the 2013 paper is the better single citation for the proposal. |

## Social Engineering as Philosophical Concept

Surfaced from PhilPapers search (2026-04-10). Key finding: the exact phrase "multifaceted social engineering" returns zero results on PhilPapers. Alexander (2024) coined the term himself. Adjacent literature on "social engineering" as a Popperian concept is thin but traceable.

| Priority | Author | Work | Status | Notes |
|----------|--------|------|--------|-------|
| **P3** | Rhees, R. | "Social Engineering" *Mind* 56(224), 1947 | not started | Earliest philosophical response to Popper's concept. Two years after *The Open Society and Its Enemies*. Likely an early critique that may anticipate Alexander's moves. |
| **P3** | Argamakova, A. A. | "History of Social Engineering Theories" *Russian Journal of Philosophical Sciences* 64(7), 2022 | not started | Traces the concept from the 19th century through the Soviet/American contexts. Useful for situating Alexander's usage historically. |
| **P3** | Argamakova, A. A. | "The Practical Tasks of Social Engineering and the Formation of the Social and Human Sciences" *Russian Studies in Philosophy* 55(1), 2017 | not started | Earlier Argamakova piece on the role of social engineering in the formation of social and human sciences. |
| **P3** | Støvring, K. | "The Conservative Critique of the Enlightenment: The Limits of Social Engineering" *The European Legacy* 19(3), 2014 | not started | Traces the Burke-Oakeshott-Scruton-Hayek lineage of conservative critique. Useful for understanding what Alexander is responding to when he rejects Popper's piecemeal approach. |
| **P4** | Ziółkowski, J. | "Polarization as a Social Engineering Mechanism" (in *Constituents of Political Theory*, Peter Lang, 2024) | not started | Flips the framing: polarisation as deliberate engineering, not natural phenomenon. Worth a closer look given how much of Alexander's book is about polarisation. |
