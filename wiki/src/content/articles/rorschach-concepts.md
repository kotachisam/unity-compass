**Rorschach concepts** are political concepts whose common meaning is, in J. McKenzie Alexander's phrase, "heavily dependent on the subjective understanding of the individual".[^ose-2024] Alexander coined the term in *The Open Society as an Enemy* (LSE Press, 2024) and uses it as the central diagnostic device for explaining how contemporary political movements can mobilise mass support behind slogans that carry no determinate policy content. A Rorschach concept coordinates agents without requiring them to converge on shared meaning: a crowd endorses the concept while each member projects different content onto it.

## Definition and origin

Alexander introduces the term in the introduction to *The Open Society as an Enemy* (pp. 10-11).[^ose-2024] The defining feature is asymmetry between apparent agreement and underlying interpretation. Participants register shared endorsement at the level of the label while holding divergent and often incompatible views of what the label refers to. The analogy is to the Rorschach inkblot: a single stimulus that supports many projections, none of which is stipulated by the stimulus itself.

The concept is built on a philosophical lineage running through Charles Taylor's "Interpretation and the Sciences of Man" (*Review of Metaphysics* 25(1), 1971). Taylor distinguishes intersubjective meaning, which is the shared language that enables coordination between agents, from common meaning, which is the shared reference world that constitutes a community. Taylor's central observation is that common meaning is "more than convergence" because "this sharing must be shared", and that common meanings can "subsist with a high degree of cleavage".[^taylor-1971] Alexander inherits Taylor's distinction and gives it a sharper and narrower formulation. Where Taylor describes a general feature of social meaning, Alexander identifies a specific class of political concepts in which the cleavage is the point: the concept functions precisely because it admits multiple projections.

## Paradigm cases

Alexander's two running examples are "Make America Great Again" and "Brexit" (pp. 9-11).[^ose-2024] He observes that a crowd can endorse "Make America Great Again" without any single unambiguous policy content commanding a majority of that crowd. Supporters of the slogan align on the abstract direction while diverging on what restoration of greatness requires in specific policy terms: tariffs, immigration reform, cultural restoration, economic protectionism, foreign policy reorientation. Similarly, "Brexit" functioned as a coordination label for voters whose underlying commitments ranged across sovereignty, trade, immigration, regulatory independence and constitutional identity, with no shared specification of what leaving the European Union would entail beyond the act of leaving.

In both cases the slogan aligns agents at the level of abstract shared direction while permitting divergent projection at the level of specific content. The coordination is real in the sense that it mobilises collective action. It is conditional in the sense that it survives only as long as the underlying divergence is not forced into explicit specification.

## Role in the investigation

Rorschach concepts are a direct empirical illustration of the thesis's central claim that coordination does not require convergence, only shared navigation principles. The Rorschach concept is the shared navigation principle: it aligns agents structurally while permitting divergence evaluatively. The investigation's proposed coordination space explains why such concepts can work by distinguishing structural alignment (agents occupy the same configuration space and act in relation to the same structural features) from evaluative convergence (agents share the same ranking over outcomes).

The investigation adds what Alexander's treatment does not provide: a formal account of the conditions under which a Rorschach concept produces stable coordination rather than merely the appearance of it. Alexander describes Rorschach concepts as a diagnostic observation; the investigation proposes to characterise them as a specific case within a formal framework in which structural equilibrium can be defined.

## Open questions

When does a Rorschach concept produce genuine coordination and when does it collapse into incoherence under the pressure of policy specificity? The distinction matters because Rorschach alignment and structural equilibrium can look identical from the outside while behaving very differently under stress. A concept that sustains alignment only as long as trade-offs are deferred is not the same object as a configuration that is stable against the forcing of those trade-offs. Characterising the transition from Rorschach alignment to structural cleavage is an open question in the investigation's running register.[^open-questions]

Related open questions include the relationship between Layer 1 structural features and Layer 2 subjective projection, the lag between perception and structural reckoning, and the conditions under which a Rorschach concept transitions from coordination device to source of conflict as specification is forced.
