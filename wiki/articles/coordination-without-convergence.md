**Coordination without convergence** is the central concept of the Unity Compass investigation. It refers to the possibility that collective coordination among agents can be grounded in structural features of their interdependence rather than in shared values, aligned incentives or ideological agreement.

## The claim

The claim has two parts. First, that value convergence is *not sufficient* for coordination: [[Mancur Olson]] demonstrated that shared interests do not reliably produce collective action, because strategic structures (free-riding, coordination failures) can block cooperation even when all parties would benefit from it. Second, that value convergence is *not necessary*: [[Elinor Ostrom]] demonstrated that coordination can emerge through institutional design among actors who do not share values, and [[Thomas Schelling]] demonstrated that coordination can emerge through structural salience without shared values or even communication.

Taken together, these results suggest that convergence is neither sufficient nor necessary for coordination. The question the investigation poses is: if not convergence, what?

## The hypothesis

The working hypothesis is that **structural features of interdependence** can ground coordination where value convergence cannot. Four candidate features are currently being explored: shared risk topology, shared constraints, network entanglement and procedural commitments. See [[Structural Interdependence]] for detail on each.

## Relationship to existing theory

Most coordination theory either assumes convergence (shared payoff structures in [[Evolutionary Game Theory]], common utility functions in mechanism design) or tries to produce it (deliberation in Habermasian democracy, negotiation in bargaining theory). The investigation argues there is a third path: coordination grounded in structural features of the interdependence itself, without requiring or producing agreement on values.

## Applied domain

The primary case study for testing this hypothesis is [[AUKUS]] trilateral defence coordination, where three nations with genuinely different strategic doctrines coordinate on catastrophic security risks without sharing a common payoff structure.
