**Thomas Schelling** (1921-2016) was an American economist and Nobel laureate whose *The Strategy of Conflict* (1960) introduced the concept of focal points to coordination theory.

## Focal points

Schelling demonstrated that people are remarkably good at finding coordination solutions without explicit communication, gravitating toward options that feel "obvious" even when the obviousness is culturally contingent. Two strangers told to meet somewhere in New York City will disproportionately choose Grand Central Station at noon, not because it is objectively optimal but because it is salient.

This finding is significant for the investigation because it shows that coordination does not require negotiation, enforcement or even communication. Structural salience alone can generate coordination equilibria.

## Role in the investigation's central argument

Schelling provides one of the two key demonstrations that value convergence is *not necessary* for coordination. [[Elinor Ostrom]] demonstrated this through institutional design; Schelling demonstrated it through structural salience. Together with [[Mancur Olson]]'s demonstration that convergence is *not sufficient* (shared interests don't reliably produce collective action), these three results establish the foundation for the investigation's central claim: [[Coordination Without Convergence]].

## Coordination taxonomy connection

In the investigation's coordination taxonomy, Schelling's focal-point concept maps directly to the pure coordination archetype: situations where all parties want to coordinate and the only problem is uncertainty about what the others will do. The mechanism that solves pure coordination is information, shared signals, common knowledge and salient conventions. Schelling showed this works even without explicit agreement.
