The **Fukushima Daiichi** nuclear disaster (March 2011) resulted from a magnitude 9.0 earthquake and subsequent tsunami overwhelming a nuclear power station whose sea wall was inadequate for the threat. The Kurokawa Report concluded that the disaster was "profoundly man-made": the result of institutional failures in oversight, risk communication and regulatory independence.

## Compounding failure modes

The investigation uses Fukushima as its most detailed example of how coordination failure modes interact and reinforce each other:

- **Information distortion**: TEPCO's own 2006 modelling predicted that an earthquake of magnitude 8.3 could produce a 15-metre tsunami. The information existed. It moved through institutional channels for five years without producing modification to the site's 5.7-metre sea wall.
- **Regulatory capture**: the boundary between regulator and regulated had dissolved. The Kurokawa Report described both TEPCO and the government as "trapped in a safety myth."
- **Path dependence**: the 5.7m sea wall was a legacy design decision that institutional inertia prevented from being revised despite new evidence.

## The gap between procedure and judgment

The investigation treats Fukushima as a demonstration of what happens when institutional culture suppresses situated judgment. TEPCO's institutional culture prioritised procedural compliance over the exercise of professional judgment. The formal systems worked exactly as designed. The judgment that would have corrected them was systematically disabled by the very institution the systems were meant to serve.

This connects to the investigation's broader argument that coordination under high-stakes conditions requires both formal procedure and situated judgment, and that institutional designs which suppress either one are fragile at exactly the moments when resilience matters most.
