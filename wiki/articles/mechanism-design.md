**Mechanism design** is the branch of economics and [[Game Theory]] that constructs the rules of strategic interaction to achieve desired outcomes, accounting for participants' strategic behaviour. Sometimes called "reverse game theory": instead of analysing a given game, the designer constructs the game to produce a target result.

## Core assumptions

The mechanism design framework assumes:

1. **Objectives can be specified in advance.** The designer knows what "success" looks like. This breaks when objectives are contested ([[Value Incommensurability]]), when they shift as conditions change, or when specifying the objective requires the kind of collective deliberation the mechanism is supposed to enable.

2. **The designer stands outside the system.** The mechanism designer observes actors, understands their incentives and constructs rules. In real coordination, the designer is also a participant, shaped by the same cognitive biases and institutional pressures as everyone else.

3. **Self-interest as the universal motivator.** Rational choice theory assumes actors maximise their own utility. This predicts a great deal of behaviour accurately but is systematically wrong in ways that matter. People cooperate when the model says they shouldn't, sacrifice for strangers and honour commitments beyond their strategic value. Designing institutions exclusively for self-interested actors can erode the pro-social motivations that already exist.

4. **Incentive compatibility as the gold standard.** The goal is designing rules so self-interested behaviour produces the desired collective outcome. This is elegant when it works (spectrum auctions, kidney exchange programmes). It fails when the desired collective outcome is itself what the actors disagree about.

## Where it works

Bilateral trade negotiations, auction design, matching markets (kidneys, school places, medical residencies), spectrum allocation. Domains where objectives are clear, actors are strategic and the design space is well-understood.

## Where it fails for the investigation

Mechanism design inherits and sometimes amplifies [[Game Theory]]'s foundational assumptions. Where objectives are contested, actors are complex and the system is reflexive, it produces mechanisms that look rigorous while quietly substituting a tractable problem for the actual one. The investigation's central question begins in the territory where mechanism design's first assumption (specifiable objectives) breaks down.
