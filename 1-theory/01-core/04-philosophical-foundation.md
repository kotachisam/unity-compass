# Philosophical Foundation

## Where Coordination Theory Runs Out

Coordination theory is remarkably good at certain problems. When actors share objectives, when preferences can be clearly stated, when outcomes can be modelled probabilistically — the existing toolkit works. Game theory, mechanism design, institutional economics: these are powerful, proven and essential.

Where coordination theory consistently breaks down is at precisely the point this framework cares about most: coordination between actors who hold genuinely different values and cannot resolve that difference through negotiation, aggregation or compromise. The standard toolkit assumes that value conflicts are either resolvable (we negotiate until we converge) or aggregable (we vote and accept the outcome). When neither works — when the values are genuinely incommensurable and the stakes are too high for anyone to simply concede — the toolkit runs out of moves.[^1]

Why? Several factors converge.

**The parent disciplines were already constrained.** Coordination theory emerged primarily from economics and game theory — fields committed to formal modelling with quantifiable preferences before they ever touched coordination problems. Rational choice theory assumes actors have well-defined, consistent preferences and act to maximise expected utility.[^2] These are useful simplifications for certain problems. They are also simplifications that exclude, by design, much of how humans actually navigate disagreement.

**The foundational context was adversarial.** The game theory that underpins most coordination theory was developed at RAND in the 1950s for nuclear deterrence — an explicitly adversarial framing between strategic competitors. That origin baked in assumptions about actors as self-interested agents in competition. Coordination across genuine difference, where actors may hold values that are neither aligned nor opposed but simply different, was never the problem the toolkit was designed to solve.

**The tractability trap.** Genuine value incommensurability is brutally hard to formalise. Academic incentive structures reward publishable results, and problems that resist clean modelling get sidelined in favour of problems that yield tractable solutions. The result is a body of theory that is sophisticated about the problems it can handle and largely silent about the ones it can't.

**Cultural narrowness.** Coordination theory is overwhelmingly a Western, Anglo-American academic product. Entire traditions of thinking about coordination across difference — Indigenous governance practices, Confucian relational ethics, the South African concept of Ubuntu, Islamic *shura* (consultation) — were simply absent from the conversation when the foundational frameworks were built. The discipline didn't reject these perspectives. It never encountered them.

**The deepest thread: a narrowed concept of reason itself.** Underneath all of these proximate causes sits a philosophical inheritance that shapes the very concept of what counts as rational. The ancient Greeks had a word — *logos* — that meant something far richer than what we now call logic. For Heraclitus, it was a cosmic ordering principle that included the capacity to hold opposites in tension without forcing resolution. Unity-in-opposition.[^3] Over the course of western philosophy, logos was progressively narrowed: Plato privileged permanence over flux, Aristotle formalised non-contradiction as an absolute constraint, and by the Enlightenment, logic had become synonymous with formal inference from clear premises. The capacity for holding tension, for pattern recognition that precedes deduction, for wisdom embedded in situation rather than abstraction — all of it was quietly excluded from what "reason" meant.[^4]

This matters because everything downstream inherits the narrowing. If your foundational framework for practical reason says that contradiction is impermissible and that knowledge requires abstraction from the particular, then every discipline built on those foundations will carry the same constraints. The parent disciplines were constrained. The adversarial framing was adopted. The tractable problems were prioritised. Other traditions were overlooked. Each of these is a factor in its own right. But each also operates within a philosophical inheritance that had already narrowed what "rational coordination" could mean.

The incompleteness of coordination theory is multiply caused. The theorists built well on the foundations they were given, within the disciplines available to them, in the cultural context they inhabited. Those foundations, disciplines and contexts are what need expanding.

## What Coordination Actually Requires

Watch any family navigate a genuine disagreement — not a trivial one, but the kind where people hold deeply different values about something that matters. A decision about elderly care. A conflict between siblings about inheritance. A parent and adult child who see the world fundamentally differently.

The ones who coordinate successfully don't do it by running cost-benefit analyses. They don't vote. They don't find the Nash equilibrium. They do something that formal coordination theory has no vocabulary for: they hold the tension. They stay in the room with genuine disagreement. They attend to what the other person is actually experiencing, even when they think the other person is wrong. And they find a way forward that doesn't require anyone to abandon who they are.

This is a *capacity*. It can be cultivated or neglected. It can be present in an individual or absent. It can be supported by institutional design or destroyed by it. And it is absolutely essential for coordination across genuine difference at any scale.

What does this capacity consist of? Several things that formal reason has historically excluded from its domain:

**Seeing through someone else's eyes.** The ability to model another person's perspective accurately, including perspectives you disagree with. This is frequently dismissed as sentiment. It is actually a cognitive skill — a form of knowledge acquisition that cannot be replicated by formal inference. You cannot deduce what it is like to hold values fundamentally different from your own. You can only attend to it. Coordination across difference requires this attending as a prerequisite, because without it you are coordinating with your projection of the other person rather than with who they actually are.

**Holding the tension.** Formal logic demands resolution. One side is right, the other wrong, or we find a synthesis that dissolves the tension. But many of the most important coordination challenges involve values that are genuinely incommensurable — they cannot be ranked on a common scale.[^5] Climate policy that serves economic growth and climate policy that serves ecological preservation may both be valid responses to legitimate values. The capacity to hold both without premature resolution, to coordinate *across* the tension rather than by eliminating it, is precisely what Heraclitus' logos described and what formal logic excludes.

**The wisdom that lives in the body.** The nurse who knows her ward is understaffed before any metric confirms it. The submariner who feels the boat behaving differently before any instrument registers the change. The negotiator who reads the room. This knowledge is real, consequential and irreducible to propositional form. Coordination theory that operates only at the level of formal models and explicit preferences systematically ignores the situated wisdom that often makes the difference between coordination that works and coordination that collapses.

**Knowing you don't have the full picture.** Coordination across genuine difference is impossible if any party believes they already have the complete picture. Intellectual humility — the recognition that your framework or information may be incomplete, that the other party may see something you cannot — is a functional prerequisite for the kind of coordination this framework addresses. Humility here is an engineering requirement, not a moral aspiration.

**Trust is built, not assumed.** Formal coordination theory models actors as discrete agents with defined preferences interacting through mechanisms. The reality of human coordination is relational: trust is built over time, commitments carry weight beyond their strategic value, relationships shape what becomes possible. Treating coordination as a purely mechanical interaction between isolated agents misses the substrate on which all durable coordination actually rests.

These are load-bearing capacities that coordination across genuine difference mechanically requires. A bridge designed without accounting for wind load will eventually fail, regardless of how elegantly it handles static weight. Coordination theory designed without accounting for these capacities will fail (and, arguably, is failing) in exactly the same way — and for exactly the same reasons.

[^1]: Colman, Andrew M. 'Cooperation, Psychological Game Theory, and Limitations of Rationality in Social Interaction.' *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, Vol. 26, No. 2 (2003). Colman demonstrates that instrumental rationality "fails to explain intuitively obvious features of human interaction" and "yields predictions starkly at variance with experimental findings."

[^2]: For a comprehensive critique of rational choice assumptions in interactive contexts, see Bicchieri, Cristina. *The Grammar of Society: The Nature and Dynamics of Social Norms* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2006).

[^3]: Robinson, T.M. *Heraclitus: Fragments* (Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 1987). See Fragment 10: "Thou shouldst unite things whole and things not whole, that which tends to unite and that which tends to separate, the harmonious and the discordant."

[^4]: For the full argument tracing the progressive narrowing of logos to formal logic, see Norton, Samuel K. ['On Logos'](../../3-project/working-papers/on-logos.pdf) (2024/2025).

[^5]: Chang, Ruth. 'The Possibility of Parity.' *Ethics*, Vol. 112, No. 4 (2002). Chang argues that items may be "on a par" — comparable but not rankable — challenging the assumption that all values can be ordered on a single scale.
