# Coordination Taxonomy

Before proposing what to build differently, we need a shared vocabulary for what we're actually facing. The problems that arise when people need to act together are not all the same. They have different structures, different dynamics and different failure signatures. A mechanism that resolves one type brilliantly can make another type worse.

Most collective action failures are failures of diagnosis, rather than failures of will. The wrong tool applied to the wrong problem, by people who never paused to ask what kind of problem they were looking at.

## Why This Matters

Consider two scenarios. In the first, everyone in a room wants the same outcome but nobody knows what the others will do. In the second, everyone in a room wants a different outcome and each person benefits from undermining the others. These are fundamentally different coordination problems. They require fundamentally different mechanisms. But from the outside, both look like "people failing to cooperate."

If you treat a problem where people want to cooperate but can't find each other (a coordination game) as though people are selfishly defecting (a social dilemma), you will design enforcement mechanisms for a situation that needed communication. If you treat a problem where incentives genuinely conflict as though everyone just needs to talk more, you will waste time on dialogue that can't resolve a structural misalignment.

Getting the diagnosis right is not a preliminary step. It is the first and most consequential decision in any coordination effort. Everything that follows in this directory depends on it.

## The Strategic Archetypes

Five recurring structures appear across coordination problems at every scale. Each has its own logic, its own equilibrium dynamics and its own characteristic failure mode when misdiagnosed.

### Pure Coordination

</summary>

The simplest case. All parties want to coordinate and would benefit from doing so. The problem is not conflicting interests but uncertainty about what the others will do.

Two people walking toward each other on a narrow pavement. Both want to avoid collision. Neither cares which side they pass on, only that they pass on different sides. The problem is solved instantly if either can signal, and permanently if a convention exists (keep left, keep right). No enforcement needed, no incentive alignment, no negotiation. Just a focal point.

At institutional scale: standardisation problems. Which side of the road to drive on. Which technical protocol to adopt. Which currency to denominate trade in. Everyone benefits from coordination. Nobody much cares which standard wins, only that one does. The mechanism that solves pure coordination is information: shared signals, common knowledge, salient conventions. Thomas Schelling's work on focal points showed that people are remarkably good at finding these without explicit communication, gravitating toward solutions that feel "obvious" even when the obviousness is culturally contingent.[^1]

The characteristic misdiagnosis: treating pure coordination as though it were a conflict. Introducing negotiation, bargaining or enforcement into a situation that just needed a signal. International date formats, metric vs imperial, plug socket standards: decades of waste generated by treating convention problems as though someone had to win.

</details>

### Social Dilemmas

</summary>

The archetype that dominates coordination theory. Each party would benefit from mutual cooperation, but each also has an individual incentive to defect, and if enough parties defect, everyone ends up worse off.

The prisoner's dilemma is the canonical formal example. Two people, each choosing independently whether to cooperate or defect. If both cooperate, both do well. If one defects while the other cooperates, the defector does best and the cooperator does worst. If both defect, both do badly. The individually rational choice (defect) produces the collectively irrational outcome (mutual defection).[^2]

In a household: the dishes. Both partners benefit from a clean kitchen. Neither wants to be the one who always does the washing up, especially if the other won't reciprocate. Each evening's decision to leave the dishes "just this once" is individually rational and collectively corrosive.

At planetary scale: carbon emissions. Every nation benefits from a stable climate. Every nation also benefits from cheap energy in the short term. The incentive to free-ride on others' reductions is structural, not moral. The nations that do reduce bear costs while nations that don't capture benefits. This isn't selfishness. It is the logic of the game.

The mechanisms that address social dilemmas are well-studied: repeated interaction (the shadow of the future makes defection costly), monitoring and sanctioning (Ostrom's graduated sanctions), binding agreements with enforcement, and changing the payoff structure through taxes, subsidies or regulation so that cooperation becomes individually rational.

The characteristic misdiagnosis: assuming the problem is attitudinal rather than structural. Exhorting people to cooperate when the incentive structure rewards defection. Climate rhetoric that frames the problem as moral failure rather than strategic structure falls into this trap repeatedly.

</details>

### Assurance Games

</summary>

Subtler than social dilemmas and frequently misdiagnosed as them. In an assurance game, everyone prefers mutual cooperation to mutual defection, just as in the prisoner's dilemma. The difference: nobody has an incentive to defect *if they believe the others will cooperate*. The problem is not temptation to free-ride. It is uncertainty about whether others will hold up their end.

A couple considering having children. Both want to, but only if they're confident the other is genuinely committed. Neither wants to be the one who reorganised their entire life for a partnership the other was ambivalent about. The coordination problem is not conflicting interests. It is mutual vulnerability. Each needs assurance that the other will follow through.

At international scale: nuclear disarmament. Most nations would prefer a world without nuclear weapons to the current one. No nation is willing to disarm unilaterally. The problem is not that nations prefer having nuclear weapons. It is that disarming without assurance that others will follow is catastrophic. The equilibrium where everyone cooperates is better for everyone, but it requires trust that the equilibrium will hold. Absent that trust, the inferior equilibrium (everyone stays armed) persists.

The mechanisms that resolve assurance games are different from those addressing social dilemmas. Enforcement is less important than credible commitment: confidence-building measures, phased approaches with verification, track records of follow-through, and institutions that make commitment visible and reversibility costly. The IETF's rough consensus model works partly because contributions are public and reputational stakes are real, creating assurance without enforcement.[^3]

The characteristic misdiagnosis: misdiagnosing assurance problems as social dilemmas and introducing heavy enforcement where what was needed was trust-building. If the problem is "I'd cooperate if I knew you would," then punishing defection is addressing a symptom while ignoring the cause. The cure is credible signals, not bigger sticks.

</details>

<summary>

### Chicken

</summary>

The game of nerve. Two parties on a collision course. Both prefer swerving to crashing, but both prefer the other to swerve first. The worst outcome is mutual stubbornness (both crash). The second worst is being the one who yields. The best is holding firm while the other yields.

Siblings fighting over inheritance. Both would rather reach an agreement than destroy the family through litigation. Neither wants to be the one who concedes. Each escalates in the hope that the other will back down first. The dynamic produces brinksmanship: each side demonstrating commitment to holding firm, hoping the other's nerve breaks. If neither does, the outcome is catastrophic for both.

At geopolitical scale: the Cuban Missile Crisis is the textbook case. Both the US and USSR preferred avoiding nuclear war to fighting one. Neither wanted to be seen as the one who blinked. The game's logic produces escalation toward the outcome both parties least want, because yielding first carries a cost that rational actors try to avoid.

The mechanisms for chicken involve face-saving (allowing parties to yield without appearing to lose), pre-commitment devices (removing your own option to swerve, paradoxically), side payments (compensating the party that yields) and most importantly, decoupling the immediate decision from the identity stakes. When "who swerves" becomes "who you are" rather than "what you do this time," chicken becomes unresolvable because yielding is experienced as self-destruction rather than strategic adjustment.

The characteristic misdiagnosis: allowing the game to become an identity contest. Once "I never back down" becomes part of a party's self-concept, the strategic structure of the game is overridden by the psychological need to maintain self-image. This is the identity entrenchment failure mode documented in [Where Coordination Fails](../02-landscape/03-why-it-fails.md), and it operates with equal force in marriages and geopolitics.

</details>

<summary>

### Coordination Under Value Incommensurability

</summary>

This is the category existing theory handles worst, and the one this investigation cares about most.

In all four archetypes above, the payoffs are assumed to be commensurable. Actors may disagree about strategy, but they can in principle rank outcomes on a shared scale. Social dilemmas assume everyone agrees what "better off" means. Assurance games assume everyone wants the same cooperative outcome. Even chicken assumes both players rank "crash" as worst.

But many of the coordination problems that matter most lack this shared ranking. Climate policy that prioritises economic growth and climate policy that prioritises ecological preservation are not just different strategies for the same goal. They are expressions of genuinely different values about what matters. A nation that values sovereignty and a nation that values global governance do not merely disagree about means. They disagree about what "coordination success" would look like.

Ruth Chang's work on hard choices provides the philosophical framework for this.[^4] When values are genuinely incommensurable, they cannot be ranked on a common scale. The options are not better, worse or equal. They are "on a par": comparable but not rankable. At the individual level, Chang argues that agents resolve such choices through volitional commitment: constituting themselves as the kind of person who values X over Y, rather than discovering a pre-existing ranking. The choice is an act of self-creation, not calculation.

The central question for this investigation is whether collectives can exercise analogous agency. Can a group of actors who hold genuinely incommensurable values constitute themselves as coordination-capable through commitment to coordination itself, without first resolving the value disagreement? Not by pretending the disagreement doesn't exist, and not by splitting the difference. But by committing to shared orientation (we agree catastrophe is unacceptable) without requiring shared destination (we disagree on what the ideal outcome looks like).

This is what the compass metaphor describes operationally: shared direction, different routes. And it is the archetype where the [dimensions the formal toolkit leaves out](../01-core/04-philosophical-foundation.md) become most visible. Formal game theory provides no tools for coordination under genuine incommensurability, because it assumes commensurability at the foundations. Whether those excluded dimensions actually do the coordination work here, or whether structural features of interdependence (shared risk, shared constraints, network entanglement, procedural commitments) can ground coordination without needing them, is a central question this investigation is trying to work out. The taxonomy's contribution, for now, is to name the problem clearly and distinguish it from the others, because collapsing it into social dilemmas or assurance games (as most policy frameworks do) guarantees misdiagnosis and therefore mistreatment.

</details>

## Compound Structures and Real-World Messiness

These archetypes are analytical tools, not descriptions of reality. Any real coordination challenge involves multiple structures operating simultaneously, shifting between them as conditions change, and compounding with the failure modes documented in [Where Coordination Fails](../02-landscape/03-why-it-fails.md). These include, but are not limited to: speed mismatches, information distortion, accountability voids, capture dynamics, legitimacy deficits, false consensus and identity entrenchment.

A climate negotiation is not "a social dilemma." It is a social dilemma layered with assurance game dynamics, punctuated by chicken between major emitters, complicated by value incommensurability between development priorities and ecological preservation, and shot through with capture dynamics, speed mismatches and legitimacy deficits at every level. A marriage in crisis is not "an assurance game." It is an assurance game entangled with chicken dynamics over who initiates difficult conversations, identity entrenchment around who each partner believes themselves to be, and information distortion as each partner's narrative of the relationship diverges further from the other's.

The taxonomy's value is not in producing clean labels. It is in providing a diagnostic vocabulary that can identify which structures are dominant at any given moment, which mechanisms are therefore appropriate, and when the structure has shifted enough that the mechanism needs to change. The failure modes describe what goes wrong in practice. The archetypes describe why it goes wrong structurally. Both lenses are needed.

## Axelrod's Tournaments: What Formal Modelling Reveals and Where It Stops

Robert Axelrod's iterated prisoner's dilemma tournaments in the early 1980s remain one of the most influential results in coordination theory.[^5] He invited game theorists to submit strategies for a repeated prisoner's dilemma, then ran them against each other in a computational tournament. The results were striking.

The winning strategy, tit-for-tat, was also the simplest: cooperate on the first move, then copy whatever the other player did last time. It was nice (never defected first), retaliatory (punished defection immediately), forgiving (returned to cooperation after the other player did) and clear (its pattern was easy for other strategies to recognise and adapt to).

Axelrod drew several conclusions that matter for coordination theory broadly. Cooperation can emerge without central authority. Simple, transparent strategies outperform complex, exploitative ones in repeated interactions. Reciprocity is the engine of cooperation. The shadow of the future (knowing you'll interact again) transforms the strategic landscape.

These are real insights, and they generalise beyond the formal setting. The reason your local grocer doesn't cheat you isn't moral sainthood. It's that you'll be back next week. Repeated interaction with a known partner creates cooperation from self-interest. Axelrod showed why, and how, and under what conditions.

But the conditions matter.

Axelrod's results depend on four assumptions that break under precisely the conditions existential coordination faces:

**Indefinite iteration.** Tit-for-tat works because both players know the game continues. If both know which round is last, the logic unravels: defect in the final round (no retaliation possible), therefore defect in the penultimate round (the final round's outcome is fixed), and so on back to the first move. Climate coordination, pandemic response and AI governance all involve interactions that may not repeat, or where "the last round" is a systemic tipping point after which the game structure changes irrevocably.

**Stable identity.** Axelrod's strategies track what "the other player" did. But in institutional coordination, "the other player" is a rotating cast of officials, administrations, governments and corporate leadership teams. The US that signed the Paris Agreement is not the US that withdrew from it. Reputation and reciprocity require continuity of identity, and institutional coordination often lacks it.

**Binary choices.** Cooperate or defect. Real coordination involves continuous spectrums of commitment, partial compliance, creative alternatives and shifting goalposts. The binary frame is useful for formal analysis but wildly reductive as a description of actual coordination.

**Commensurable payoffs.** Axelrod's players agree on the value of outcomes. They just face a dilemma about strategy. When parties disagree about what "cooperate" even means, because their values produce different rankings of the available outcomes, the iterated prisoner's dilemma framework doesn't apply. It's not that tit-for-tat fails. It's that you're not in the game tit-for-tat was designed for.

This is not a criticism of Axelrod. It is a demonstration of the formalism boundary that [Document 03](03-where-models-fail.md) examines in depth. Axelrod's tournaments showed what formal modelling can reveal when its assumptions hold. The coordination challenges this framework addresses are precisely the ones where they don't.

## Diagnosis Before Treatment

The practical takeaway from this taxonomy is a question that should precede every coordination effort: what kind of problem are we actually facing?

Is this a pure coordination problem (we all want to align but can't find each other)? If so, the solution is communication and convention, not enforcement.

Is this a social dilemma (individual incentives conflict with collective interest)? If so, the solution is restructuring incentives, not exhortation.

Is this an assurance game (everyone would cooperate if confident others will too)? If so, the solution is credible commitment and trust-building, not punishment.

Is this chicken (both parties are escalating toward an outcome neither wants)? If so, the solution is face-saving and decoupling identity from position.

Or is this coordination under value incommensurability (we need to act together but can't agree on what "better" means)? If so, none of the above mechanisms are adequate on their own, and the coordination challenge is of a fundamentally different kind.

The same problem can exhibit different structures at different stages. A climate negotiation might begin as an assurance game (everyone would cooperate with sufficient confidence), escalate into chicken (who bears costs first), and reveal itself as value incommensurability (the parties don't just disagree on strategy but on what matters). Recognising these transitions in real time is itself a coordination capacity that existing frameworks do little to cultivate.

The [empirical evidence](02-what-works-and-why.md) examines what has actually worked across these types. The [formalism boundary](03-where-models-fail.md) examines where the models break. The [requirements](04-what-coordination-requires.md) derive what any adequate mechanism must satisfy. And the [open questions](05-open-questions.md) are honest about what we don't yet know.
