**Path dependence** describes the structural tendency of institutions to resist change even when the people inside them recognise the need for it. The investigation identifies it as one of five compound barriers to coordination reform, and the one most directly connected to the philosophical inheritance that narrowed what "rational coordination" could mean.

## The mechanism

Every institution develops internal logic: processes, hierarchies, career tracks, funding mechanisms, evaluation criteria. Over time, these structures become self-reinforcing. The people who succeed within them are the people best adapted to them. The skills that get rewarded are the skills the institution already values. Reform proposals are evaluated by the very frameworks the reform would replace.

Political scientist [[Paul Pierson]] called this "increasing returns": the longer an institution operates under a given set of assumptions, the more costly it becomes to switch to different ones, and the more the institution selects for people who see the current assumptions as natural rather than chosen.

## Why good ideas stall

The idea may be better. The transition cost (political, financial, cultural, psychological) is enormous. And the people with the authority to approve the transition are, by definition, the people who succeeded under the current system.

## Role in the investigation

Path dependence appears in the investigation's account of why coordination theory has not been reformed despite decades of good thinking by good people. It is one of five structural barriers (alongside academic gatekeeping, broken incentives, public-private misalignment and the Overton window) that reinforce each other to produce what the investigation calls "a system of stuckness."

The [[Fukushima Daiichi]] case study demonstrates path dependence operationally: the 5.7m sea wall was a legacy design decision that institutional inertia prevented from being revised despite new evidence predicting inadequacy.
