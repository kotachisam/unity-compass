**Heraclitus** of Ephesus (c. 535-475 BCE) was a pre-Socratic Greek philosopher whose concept of [[Logos]] grounds the investigation's critique that Western reason was progressively narrowed from a capacity for holding opposites in tension to formal logic alone.

## Key idea: unity-in-opposition

Fragment 10: "Thou shouldst unite things whole and things not whole, that which tends to unite and that which tends to separate, the harmonious and the discordant."

For Heraclitus, logos was not logic in the modern sense. It was a cosmic ordering principle that included the capacity to hold contraries together without forcing resolution. Opposition was not a problem to be solved but a structure to be navigated. The universe was constituted by the tension between opposites, and wisdom consisted in perceiving the unity within that tension.

## Role in the investigation

Heraclitus provides the philosophical anchor for the investigation's claim that coordination theory inherited a narrowed concept of practical reason. The progressive narrowing from Heraclitean logos through Plato (who privileged permanence over flux), Aristotle (who formalised non-contradiction as absolute), and the Enlightenment (which made logic synonymous with formal inference) is the primary point of observation connecting the investigation's multiple diagnoses of why coordination theory is incomplete.

The investigation's design principle "logos-breadth over logic-alone" is a direct reference to recovering what the narrowing excluded: situated knowledge, embodied wisdom, tolerance for contradiction, relational intelligence.
