# Alexander 2024 (The Open Society as an Enemy)

## Bibliographic information

J. McKenzie Alexander, *The Open Society as an Enemy: A Critique of How Free Societies Turned Against Themselves*. London: LSE Press, 2024. 346 pages. ISBN 978-1-911712-26-8. Open access, doi:10.31389/lsepress.ose.

## Argument

Alexander argues that populist agendas on both the left and the right have eroded the values underpinning liberal democracies and that a new defence of the Open Society is required. The book works through four conceptions of the Open Society and tracks a post-Cold War value-inversion in each: cosmopolitan (borders and migration), transparent (privacy and surveillance), Enlightenment (free exchange of ideas), and communitarian (polarisation and tribalism). In every case the Open Society has come to be perceived as the enemy rather than the virtue.

## Diagnostic apparatus

The introduction (pp. 10-11) develops *Rorschach concepts* as the book's central diagnostic category: political concepts whose common meaning is "heavily dependent on the subjective understanding of the individual". MAGA and Brexit are the paradigm cases. Such concepts coordinate behaviour without requiring shared content, because individuals project incompatible meanings onto the same surface.

Chapter 4 grounds the normative frame in an existentialist reading of political cosmopolitanism built on four elements: individual freedom, self-determination, freedom of association, and a *consistency principle* (p. 50) under which members of a community must not thwart the core freedoms that allowed the community to form. Morality is framed, citing Alexander's own 2007 book, as "a social technology for solving the interdependent decision problems which arise from people's social existence" (p. 52).

Alexander distances himself from complex adaptive systems approaches to the Open Society in his introduction (approx. p. 13), noting that although societies are complex systems they are not always best understood via complex systems theory.

## The prescriptive move

Chapter 29 proposes *multifaceted social engineering* as a third way between Popper's piecemeal approach, which Alexander argues converges to local optima, and utopian engineering. The proposal imports Sewall Wright's 1932 fitness landscape metaphor to characterise the problem. The axes of the landscape, the fitness function and the navigation method are all left unspecified. Alexander concedes on p. 324 that "practical policy recommendations will require people far more knowledgeable and capable than I am".

## The formal vacuum

Across 346 pages the book never deploys Alexander's own formal toolkit: no evolutionary game theory, no replicator dynamics, no spatial models, no Nowak and May style analyses. This is striking given the shape of Alexander's academic career. The diagnostic book about coordination failures was written without recourse to the formal apparatus of the field in which Alexander works.

## Load-bearing passages for the investigation

Two passages carry the weight in the supervision pitch. The first is p. 16: "the rehabilitation requires a philosophical enquiry into the very kind of society we want to create. It requires answering questions such as: what freedoms do we wish to protect, and what freedoms are we willing to give up in order to make other forms of social organisation possible? These questions cannot be solved in a piecemeal fashion, for they involve global comparisons and trade-offs along dimensions not obviously comparable." This is read as an almost-direct statement of the mathematical problem the coordination space is being developed to address.

The second is the p. 52 warrant from Chapter 4. If morality is understood as a social technology for solving interdependent decision problems within societies, then the coordination space is the representation of the same problem space across societies whose moral alignment varies.

## Relation to the investigation

The initial framing of the thesis was that it complements Alexander's evolutionary game theory programme. After this reading a sharper framing emerged: the thesis proposes to bridge Alexander's two disconnected bodies of work, his formal EGT programme and this diagnostic book, rather than being merely complementary to one of them.

The book extends the investigation in three ways. Rorschach concepts supply a direct empirical illustration of coordination without convergence. Multifaceted social engineering names the prescriptive target that the formal programme is being built to serve. The formal vacuum is the opening the thesis walks through.

The book also complicates the investigation in one way. Alexander's p. 13 distancing from complex adaptive systems framings is rhetorical more than substantive, but any differential-geometric formalisation the thesis develops will need to justify why it is defensible in a project whose target supervisor has flagged scepticism about complex-systems framings of the Open Society.
