# Research Proposal: Co-ordination Without Convergence: Navigating Partial Commensurability in Collective Interdependence

**Applicant:** Samuel Norton
**Degree:** MPhil/PhD in Philosophy, London School of Economics

## The Problem

Game theory assumes you can build a payoff matrix. What happens when you can't? In bounded environments, when a payoff structure is established, the options of strategies and decision possibilities for the actors involved are given by the game. This can be expanded up to n actors and remain formulaically calculable. This expands to evolutionary game theory to relax the boundary conditions and allow for graduated adaptations in strategies over time. But the foundational assumption persists across both frameworks: that outcomes can be represented in a common payoff structure. When agents hold genuinely incommensurable values, this assumption fails by design, because the representation itself becomes a category error.

The circumstances of agents with incommensurable values have been deemed a management problem to be pragmatically navigated in organisational theory, collaborative governance and ecological economics, rather than a formal condition to be theorised about. It is considered outside the bounds of modelling due to inability to determine a mutual payoff structure. But it is something that appears constantly in the human experience, at both the individual and collective levels. A couple where one values security and the other values adventure, and neither is "more" of anything the other recognises. A dispute between indigenous land rights and economic development where the very concept of what land is differs categorically. A nation that defines security as territorial sovereignty negotiating with one that defines it as economic interdependence.

In reality, fully commensurable and fully incommensurable values represent the extremes, the boundaries, and the space between represents the vast majority of existing conditions. On an individual level, this has been formalised, and continues to be explored in decision making under deep uncertainty amongst other fields, but no comparable formal account exists for collective coordination under these conditions. Collective coordination defaults to game-theoretic foundations, which assume aligned payoff structures can be established.

This thesis investigates what grounds coordination when they cannot.

## Research Question

Value commensurability between agents is not binary but domain-specific and graduated: within any given domain, agents may fully share evaluative frameworks, hold genuinely incommensurable values, or occupy the more common middle ground of partial but incomplete commensurability.

**Central Research Question:** Given that value convergence is neither sufficient nor necessary for coordination, can structural features of interdependence in domains of partial commensurability provide formal foundations for collective coordination in domains where it fails?

## Gap Analysis

Olson demonstrated that shared interests do not reliably produce collective action. Ostrom and Schelling demonstrated that coordination can emerge without shared values, through institutional design and structural salience respectively. Taken together, these results suggest that value convergence is neither sufficient nor necessary for coordination. Yet the question of what structural features actually ground coordination when convergence is absent remains formally unaddressed, despite this describing the conditions under which most real-world collective coordination actually occurs.

Evolutionary game theory models multi-agent coordination as a dynamic, adaptive process, but it inherits game theory's foundational requirement: a constructible payoff structure. Alexander's framework models how cooperative norms emerge and stabilise in structured populations, but the agents in those models are playing a defined game with numerical payoffs. The Hawk-Dove game with its fixed value V, for instance - the model appears to depend on V being a number that both players agree represents the value of the resource. What if they don't?

Decision theory under severe uncertainty handles individual rationality when standard expected utility breaks down. In "Impartial Evaluation under Ambiguity", Bradley's framework evaluates social prospects from an impartial standpoint. But coordination requires agents to act from within their own partial perspectives, not from an external evaluative position. The gap between impartial evaluation and situated coordination is where this thesis operates. Bradley's ongoing collaboration with Spiekermann as part of the Open Society Foundation on cohesion under severe uncertainty suggests this territory is of active interest at LSE.

Bratman's influential account of shared cooperative activity grounds coordination in shared intentions and meshing sub-plans. The cases this thesis addresses involve agents who do not necessarily share values nor intentions but who are structurally entangled in ways that make coordination rational from within each agent's own perspective. If structural interdependence can ground coordination independently of shared intention, this would also illuminate why Bratman's conditions succeed when they do: not because shared intentions are the ground, but because they co-occur with structural features that are.

Addressing this gap requires tools from both traditions: evolutionary game theory's account of how coordination structures emerge and decision theory's formal apparatus for reasoning under severe uncertainty to model multi-agent coordination.

## Methodology

This thesis employs analytical philosophy as its primary mode of inquiry, but the central claim, that structural features of interdependence can ground coordination under conditions of partial value commensurability, requires formal demonstration. Game-theoretic and decision-theoretic modelling will be used to test whether coordination equilibria can be characterised when standard payoff structures are unavailable. My background in engineering physics and software provides the formal modelling capability this demands.

The formal programme targets characterisation results: identifying conditions under which structural features of interdependence are individually or jointly sufficient to generate stable coordination equilibria, and whether any such features are necessary. This includes establishing whether the candidate structural features (shared risk topology, shared constraints, network entanglement, procedural commitments) reduce to a common formal account or require distinct treatment.

The primary case study domain for this thesis will be multi-lateral defence coordination for testing the formal claims against real-world coordination under partial commensurability. The UK, USA and Australia share catastrophic security risks through arrangements such as AUKUS, yet operate under different strategic doctrines, different democratic constraints on military deployment, and different definitions of what regional security requires. They coordinate effectively in practice despite lacking a common payoff structure, making this domain a natural test case for coordination grounded in structural interdependence rather than value convergence.

Consider agents whose preference orderings over positive outcomes are genuinely incomparable. Standard coordination games cannot be defined for these agents because no common payoff matrix can be constructed. However, these agents may nonetheless share structural features of their interdependence that make coordination rational from within each agent's own partial perspective. The central formal question is whether such features can generate stable coordination equilibria in the absence of a shared payoff structure.

My operational familiarity with this domain provides analytical access that a purely theoretical approach would lack, but also raises methodological questions about confirmation bias. The formal results will therefore be tested for generalisability against at least one coordination domain where I hold no operational experience, most likely climate governance or AI regulation, to establish whether the structural features identified in the defence case generalise or prove domain-specific.

## Expected Contribution

This thesis will deliver a formal account of how structural features of interdependence (shared risk topology, shared constraints, network entanglement, procedural commitments and potentially others) can ground coordination among agents whose values are partially or fully incommensurable. It will establish specific formal conditions under which such coordination is stable, and present a demonstration of the formalism using multi-lateral defence coordination.

The formal account is intended to generalise beyond defence to climate governance, AI regulation, international financial coordination and other domains where agents share structural interdependence but not values. If coordination without convergence can be formally grounded, the implications extend to any context where collective action currently stalls on the assumption that it requires either shared values or aligned self-interest.

The thesis will also identify the boundary conditions of this approach: the degree of structural entanglement below which interdependence cannot ground coordination, and the conditions, if any, under which incommensurability is sufficiently total that no structural feature provides purchase. Establishing where the framework fails is as important as establishing where it succeeds.
