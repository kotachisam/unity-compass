**Restorative justice** brings together victims, offenders and affected community members to address the harm caused by a crime through dialogue rather than punishment. It is coordination across the most extreme difference imaginable: participants who share no values, no relationship, no mutual goodwill and often no desire to be in the same room. What they share is consequences.

## The mechanism

The format varies (circles, conferences, victim-offender mediation) but the structure is consistent. A trained facilitator creates a space where each party speaks from their experience. The victim describes the impact. The offender hears it directly rather than through the abstractions of legal process. Community members provide context and accountability. Together, they develop a plan to address the harm.

## Results

Meta-analyses consistently show higher victim satisfaction, lower reoffending rates and stronger sense of justice than conventional criminal proceedings. This is not because the parties come to agree on values. It is because the process creates conditions under which coordination becomes possible despite the absence of agreement.

## Role in the investigation

Mapped to the coordination taxonomy, restorative justice is coordination under [[Value Incommensurability]]. The victim and the offender hold fundamentally different relationships to the event. Their values regarding justice, responsibility and what "making it right" means are often irreconcilable. The process does not resolve this incommensurability. It creates a structure within which the parties can coordinate on a response despite it.

The investigation identifies restorative justice as evidence that coordination across the most extreme difference is possible when the process is structured to make room for the dimensions [[Game Theory|formal coordination theory]] excludes: perspective-taking, holding tension, intellectual humility and relational trust built through the structure of the process itself.

## Transferable insight

If coordination works here, between a victim and the person who harmed them, the argument that coordination across difference at larger scales is impossible becomes significantly harder to sustain. The key finding: legitimacy of process can substitute for convergence of values.

## Limitations

Restorative justice requires voluntary participation and operates within a broader justice system that provides the alternative if either party declines. Scaling to contexts where participation is compulsory or where the alternative is systemic failure changes the dynamic in ways that are genuinely unexplored.
