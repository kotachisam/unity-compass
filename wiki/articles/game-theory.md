**Game theory** is the mathematical study of strategic interaction between rational agents. It is the dominant formal framework for analysing coordination and the parent discipline of much of the theory the investigation engages with, including [[Evolutionary Game Theory]], [[Mechanism Design]] and [[Social Choice Theory]].

## Origins

The game theory that underpins most coordination theory was developed at RAND in the 1950s for nuclear deterrence, an explicitly adversarial framing between strategic competitors. That origin baked in assumptions about actors as self-interested agents in competition. Coordination across genuine difference, where actors may hold values that are neither aligned nor opposed but simply different, was never the problem the toolkit was designed to solve.

## The investigation's coordination taxonomy

The investigation's coordination taxonomy uses game-theoretic vocabulary to map five strategic archetypes: pure coordination, social dilemmas, assurance games, chicken and [[Value Incommensurability|coordination under value incommensurability]]. The first four are well-served by game-theoretic analysis. The fifth is where the framework breaks down.

## Four assumptions that break

The investigation identifies four foundational assumptions in game theory that fail under the conditions it cares about most:

1. **Actors are rational and know the others are too.** Common knowledge of rationality structures every equilibrium analysis. It breaks when actors are boundedly rational, operate under cognitive biases, or are non-human agents with opaque objective functions.

2. **Preferences are stable, complete and known.** The model assumes actors can rank all possible outcomes. Preferences shift under deliberation (the Irish Citizens' Assembly demonstrated this empirically). Preferences under deep uncertainty may be fundamentally incoherent.

3. **The game structure is common knowledge.** In real coordination, the game structure is itself contested. Climate negotiations involve actors who disagree about whether they're in a social dilemma, an assurance game or a value incommensurability problem.

4. **Payoffs are commensurable.** Outcomes can be ranked on a shared scale. This is the assumption that [[Value Incommensurability]] violates directly. When agents hold values that cannot be placed on a common scale, the payoff matrix that game theory requires cannot be constructed.

## Relationship to the investigation

Game theory is not the enemy. The investigation argues that the toolkit is powerful where its assumptions hold, and that the error is using it without knowing where it stops. The investigation's central question begins precisely where game theory's fourth assumption (commensurable payoffs) breaks down.
