**Mancur Olson** (1932-1998) was an American political economist whose *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965) demonstrated that shared interests do not reliably produce collective coordination.

## The collective action problem

Olson showed that rational, self-interested individuals will not act to achieve their common group interests unless the group is small enough for individual contributions to be noticeable, or unless selective incentives (benefits available only to participants) operate alongside the shared interest. In large groups, the incentive to free-ride on others' contributions dominates.

This was a direct challenge to the assumption that groups with shared interests will naturally organise to pursue them.

## Role in the investigation

Olson provides the "not sufficient" half of the investigation's central philosophical distinction. The research proposal argues that value convergence is *neither sufficient nor necessary* for coordination:

- **Not sufficient** (Olson): shared interests don't reliably produce collective action, because strategic structures like free-riding can block cooperation even when all parties would benefit
- **Not necessary** ([[Elinor Ostrom]], [[Thomas Schelling]]): coordination can emerge through institutional design or structural salience without shared values

Olson's result is critical because it prevents the investigation from being dismissed as trivially true. If convergence were sufficient for coordination, then the cases where it fails would be anomalies rather than structural features. Olson shows that even with full convergence, coordination can fail. This means the investigation's question about what grounds coordination is a live question even in the easiest cases.
