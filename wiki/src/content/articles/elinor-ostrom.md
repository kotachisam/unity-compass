**Elinor Ostrom** (1933-2012) was an American political economist who received the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 2009 for her work demonstrating that communities can and do successfully manage shared resources without either privatisation or state control.

## Key contribution

Ostrom's [*Governing the Commons*](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511807763) (1990) analysed hundreds of cases spanning fisheries, irrigation systems, forests and grazing lands. She identified eight design principles that characterised successful self-governance of shared resources: clearly defined boundaries, rules adapted to local conditions, collective choice arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution mechanisms, recognition of the right to self-organise, and nested governance for larger systems.

These findings are empirical, not theoretical. They were extracted from communities that successfully managed shared resources over decades and sometimes centuries.

## Relevance to the investigation

Ostrom's work is foundational to [[Coordination Without Convergence]] in two ways. First, her cases demonstrate that coordination can emerge through institutional design among actors who do not share values, providing evidence for the "not necessary" side of the claim that convergence is neither sufficient nor necessary. Second, her design principles provide the most empirically tested framework for how coordination mechanisms should be structured.

The investigation's transfer analysis identifies what generalises from Ostrom's findings (graduated sanctions, collective choice, nested governance, monitoring) and what depends on conditions that the hardest coordination challenges cannot provide (bounded communities, repeated interaction with stable partners, iterative learning from failure, stable resource dynamics).

## Limitations for the investigation

Ostrom's conditions are demanding. Her cases involve bounded communities where participants know each other, interact repeatedly, can observe each other's behaviour, share a resource whose dynamics are relatively stable, and can learn iteratively from failure. High-stakes coordination at scale lacks nearly all of these conditions.
