# Core

This is the intellectual foundation of the Unity Compass. Four documents that make a single argument, best read in order.

## [Manifesto](01-manifesto.md)

The stake in the ground. We have accepted the way we cooperate as if it were set in stone. It is not. Current models assume that coordination requires either a shared worldview or an immediate interest. Evidence from families, communities, open-source development and countless other domains shows otherwise. Coordination across genuine difference is possible. What we lack is the theory, the language and the institutional architecture to do it at the scale our challenges now demand.

## [Why Now](02-why-now.md)

The case for urgency. AI has introduced non-human actors into coordination for the first time in history. Several environmental and systemic tipping points have been crossed. The pace of change is outrunning the institutions meant to manage it, and the gap between the speed of the world and the speed of institutional response is widening visibly. The tools we are trusting with civilisational stakes were designed for a different era.

## [Why Not Already](03-why-not-already.md)

The diagnosis of stuckness. If the need is this urgent, why hasn't it been done? Five structural barriers that reinforce each other: the ideas are locked in academic institutions that most practitioners never access, incentive structures in both public and private sectors make rational actors behave in ways that prevent coordination, the intersection where coordination matters most has the weakest frameworks, institutions resist change even when the people inside them can see the need for it, and the most necessary conversations remain outside the bounds of what's considered politically acceptable.

## [Principles](04-principles.md)

The foundation for what gets built. Starting from the observation that coordination theory inherited a narrowed understanding of practical reason, this document identifies the capacities that coordination across genuine difference actually requires: the ability to see through someone else's eyes, to hold tension without premature resolution, to recognise knowledge that lives in experience rather than abstraction, to operate with intellectual humility, and to build trust relationally rather than assuming it mechanistically. Seven design principles follow, each shaped by these capacities and built to survive contact with the institutional barriers diagnosed in the previous document.

---

Together, these four documents argue that coordination theory is incomplete, the incompleteness is structural rather than accidental, the stakes are civilisational, and the principles for building something better can be derived from understanding what was excluded and why.

Everything else in this repository builds on this core.
