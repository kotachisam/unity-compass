**Robert Axelrod** is an American political scientist at the University of Michigan whose work on the evolution of cooperation is foundational to the investigation's engagement with [[Evolutionary Game Theory]].

## The tournaments

In the early 1980s, Axelrod invited game theorists to submit strategies for a repeated prisoner's dilemma, then ran them against each other in a computational tournament. The winning strategy, tit-for-tat, was also the simplest: cooperate on the first move, then copy whatever the other player did last time.

Tit-for-tat was nice (never defected first), retaliatory (punished defection immediately), forgiving (returned to cooperation after the other player did) and clear (its pattern was easy for other strategies to recognise and adapt to). These four qualities became a widely cited framework for understanding what makes cooperation stable.

## Key insights for coordination theory

Axelrod drew several conclusions that the investigation takes seriously:

- Cooperation can emerge without central authority
- Simple, transparent strategies outperform complex, exploitative ones in repeated interactions
- Reciprocity is the engine of cooperation
- The shadow of the future (knowing you'll interact again) transforms the strategic landscape

## Where the investigation identifies limitations

The coordination taxonomy identifies four assumptions in Axelrod's framework that break under the conditions the investigation cares about most:

1. **Indefinite iteration.** Tit-for-tat requires both players to know the game continues. High-stakes coordination often involves interactions that may not repeat, or where the "last round" is a systemic tipping point.

2. **Stable identity.** Axelrod's strategies track what "the other player" did. In institutional coordination, the other player is a rotating cast of officials, administrations and governments. The US that signed the Paris Agreement is not the US that withdrew from it.

3. **Binary choices.** Cooperate or defect. Real coordination involves continuous spectrums of commitment and creative alternatives.

4. **Commensurable payoffs.** Axelrod's players agree on the value of outcomes. When parties hold genuinely incommensurable values, the iterated prisoner's dilemma framework does not apply. This is the gap [[Structural Interdependence]] is meant to address.
