## Introduction

Chang argues that parity is a fourth value relation outside of the bounds of the usual three relations, greater than, less than and equal to. Two options are on a par when they are comparable but bidirectionally-related - qualitatively different, with neither dominating overall. This account operates at the level of individual rational choice. 

Coordination among agents with structurally incommensurable evaluations raises a question Chang's apparatus does not address: what makes a coordination problem tractable when no common payoff structure can be constructed? Rather than extending parity by analogy, this essay treats parity as a tool with which to investigate a different problem. Standard game-theoretic foundations require a common payoff structure to define equilibrium. In its absence, the central machinery — Nash, evolutionary stability, mechanism design — cannot even be stated. This essay argues that the place to look for *why* is in the structure of the choice space itself rather than the agents within it.

## 1 - Chang Reconstruction

Ruth Chang (2002, 2017) presents a clear distinction between easy choices, when one option clearly dominates the other, and hard choices, where such a division is not so simply made. She then identifies the three sources most commonly proposed for the difficulty of such choices - our ignorance, the incomparability of the choices themselves and the incommensurability of their value sets - and argues why none of the three is defensible.

First is our ignorance. Hard choices are hard because we can't see the future, we don't have enough knowledge about each of the options in front of us and if we did, the decision would be easy. This position has genuine purchase. Plenty of apparently hard choices collapse into easy ones once a relevant fact comes to light - a job offer made trivial by news of the company's instability, a dilemma resolved by a single disclosed condition. If ignorance dissolves some hardness, the ignorance theorist asks, why not all of it? Chang's reply turns on cases where the ignorance theorist's wish is granted. Suppose you face a choice between two careers: one as a lawyer, and the other as a musician, and stipulate that you know in advance everything relevant about each life - the eventual income, the personal and professional relationships, the satisfactions at every stage. The choice remains hard. Stipulating away the agent's ignorance leaves the hardness untouched. Whatever is doing the work, it is not informational.

Second is the incommensurability of the choices themselves. Chang defines incommensurability cleanly - when "there is no common unit by which they can be measured with respect to V". The premise for her refutation is simple: measurement is a stronger requirement than comparison, and rational agents can qualitatively compare without quantifiably measuring, giving the example that you can confidently say a lifetime achievement award is better than a slice of apple pie without being able to provide metrics that evidence that choice.

Finally, the incomparability of the choices themselves. Chang highlights the distinction between non-comparability and incomparability, using an absurd example of the number four and a plate of fried eggs to illustrate the former concept, where A and B cannot be logically or rationally compared due to the lack of covering value that makes a comparison possible. Incomparability describes an option A and B that can be compared, but no better, worse or equal value relation holds between them. Chang establishes two key premises prior to refuting this explanation: firstly that there is incomparability at all, citing herself and Broome (1997) to say that establishing incomparability is a challenge in itself, and secondly that hard choices can be made rationally, as an exercise of rational agency. 

Based on these premises, Chang gives three main arguments to refute the incomparability explanation. The argument I will focus on in this essay is how she describes the appeal of keeping isomorphism between evaluative relations and practical response with respect to hard choices. She separates this appeal into four parts, arguing that for each of the trichotomous evaluative relations, there's a corresponding rational response that neatly maps onto it. A better option? Choose the better. A worse option? Pass on the worse. Equal warrants picking, arbitrarily but rationally, because A and B are equally good. In the case of incomparability, by contrast, there's no rational response that neatly maps onto the evaluative relation, so it pairs not with a rational response but with what Chang calls plumping, an arational selection that falls outside the scope of practical reason. Hard choices, however, are made within the scope of practical reason, so they cannot be cases of incomparability.

So if ignorance, incommensurability and incomparability cannot explain the hardness of hard choices, what can? Chang proposes parity, a fourth value relation that sits alongside greater than, less than or equal to, where A and B are in the same neighbourhood of V overall, yet are very different qualitatively in respect to the V they deliver. This leaves the reader with no clarity as to what distinguishes parity from equality, however. To this end, Chang presents the Small Improvement Argument, or SIA, originally introduced by Ronald de Sousa, as her case for differentiation - that if A and B were equal, and you then improve A by a small amount and call it A+, then A+ should be better than B, since A+ > A and A = B. But A+ is *not* better than B. Therefore A and B can't have been equal in the first place. One such example may be to consider two careers, say between a profession as a philosopher and a profession as a Naval nuclear engineer. A philosopher's life may give one of intellectual freedom, pushing the bounds of thought and developing novel theorems, whereas a naval engineer gets adventure, deep technical mastery, discipline and high-pressure work. They are neither better nor worse than each other, simply different in their natures. A small bump in salary to the naval engineer wouldn't tip the balance against the philosophy career.

If A and B are not better, worse or equal and they are also not incomparable, because we can rationally engage with the comparison, then there must be a fourth relation that captures their evaluative standing. Parity is the relationship that survives small improvements precisely because the qualitative differences between A and B mean small, unidimensional changes to one side don't tip the balance against the other. This is the structural feature that distinguishes it from equality, as in equality, any improvement to one side or other would tip the relation into betterness.

Chang's argument carries implications beyond the question of value relations alone. She holds that standard decision and rational choice theory have rested on a mistaken assumption: that the choice attitudes available to a rational agent are exhausted by strict preference, reverse preference and indifference. If parity is real, that conceptual space is larger than the trichotomy admits. The formal apparatus built on trichotomous assumptions, including the partial orderings sometimes deployed by decision theorists, will on her account fall short of capturing what rational agents are actually doing. Mainstream rational choice theory therefore needs reworking.

This observation both anticipates and challenges the move this essay proposes. The challenge is direct: if standard partial orderings underdescribe the choice attitudes a rational agent can take at the individual level, why expect them to fare better when extended from individual choice to collective coordination? The response, which the next section develops, is that the apparatus required is not bare partial orderings but partial orderings enriched with structural position information. What Chang shows underdescribes individual rational choice can, when augmented with the right structural objects, capture coordination among agents whose evaluative commitments are themselves partially ordered.

## 2 - The Structural Argument
