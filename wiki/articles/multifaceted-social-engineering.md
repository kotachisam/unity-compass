# Multifaceted Social Engineering

Multifaceted social engineering is a term coined by J. McKenzie Alexander in *The Open Society as an Enemy* (2024) for a holistic approach to social engineering that addresses multiple dimensions of the Open Society simultaneously rather than one at a time. Alexander presents it in Chapter 29 as a third way between Popper's piecemeal social engineering and the utopian social engineering that Popper rejected. A PhilPapers exact-phrase search returns zero results for the term, confirming that it has no prior usage in the philosophical literature.

## Origin and context

Alexander introduces the term in the concluding chapter of a 346-page diagnostic of value-inversion in four conceptions of the Open Society: cosmopolitan, transparent, Enlightenment and communitarian. Having argued that each conception is under pressure and that the pressures are interrelated, Alexander concludes that "a defence of the Open Society means doing things differently on a crowded planet home to eight billion people" and that this "requires thinking about social engineering from a multifaceted, holistic perspective" (p. 303). The term appears as the name for that perspective. It is not developed in earlier Alexander work and does not carry over from any identified prior source.

## Argument against piecemeal social engineering

Alexander's case against the piecemeal approach is staged through Wright's fitness landscape metaphor from evolutionary biology. On this picture the piecemeal social engineer "would move in the direction that has the steepest upward slope" and "most of the time, such adjustments end up converging to a local optimum which is far below the global optimum" (p. 307). The claim is that gradient-following along one dimension at a time is structurally incapable of reaching configurations that require coordinated movement across several dimensions at once. The argument is reinforced empirically in the "finest hour" section, where Alexander traces specific causal loops between the four conceptions: social media addiction drives anonymous communication, which reinforces modern tribes via stereotypes, which creates demand for safe spaces, which produces echo chambers, which facilitate populist rejection of cosmopolitanism. These loops are described qualitatively, without rates, thresholds or conditions for when feedback strengthens or weakens. The loops justify rejecting piecemeal approaches with concrete mechanisms rather than by appeal to holism.

## Against the impossibility of holistic approaches

Popper raised three objections to utopian social engineering: that it requires authoritarian power to overcome resistance, that it requires sustained commitment across generations of leaders who may disagree, and that it requires factual knowledge we do not possess. Alexander accepts the first two as serious contingent obstacles. He rejects the third as a modal claim that collapses into a contingent one under interrogation (pp. 304-305). Popper holds that the knowledge cannot be had because practical experience is lacking and all knowledge is grounded in experience. Alexander replies that the impossibility claim is not counterfactually robust: the experience could be sought. Popper has therefore shown, on Alexander's reading, that utopian social engineering depends on three contingent conditions, not that it is impossible in principle. This clears conceptual space for a holistic alternative without collapsing into the utopianism Popper targeted.

## The formal vacuum

Alexander's presentation of multifaceted social engineering is entirely informal. The fitness landscape is invoked as metaphor; the axes, the fitness function and the navigation method are not specified. No evolutionary game theory, replicator dynamics or spatial models from Alexander's own academic field appear in the book. The policy material in Chapter 29 is described as starting points rather than solutions, and Alexander concedes explicitly that "practical policy recommendations will require people far more knowledgeable and capable than I am" (p. 324). The concept names a shape the solution should take without providing the formal apparatus to give that shape content.

## Role in the investigation

The investigation treats multifaceted social engineering as the prescriptive target that the formal programme aims to serve. The working proposal is that the term gains formal content as navigation through a coordination space: structural features and evaluative commitments as axes in the same configuration manifold, structural equilibrium as the stability concept under which no agent can reach a dominating position from their situated perspective, and Alexander's piecemeal-versus-multifaceted distinction as single-axis versus multi-axis moves that respect the metric's non-orthogonal axis interaction. The absence of prior usage of the term means the investigation is not entering a crowded field. It is picking up a term with no existing formal content and proposing to supply it. Whether the proposal succeeds is an open question, not a result.

## Relation to Wright's fitness landscape

Alexander imports the landscape metaphor from Wright (1932) without engaging Wright's own shifting balance theory, in which genetic drift allows populations to cross neutral ridges between adaptive peaks. The investigation proposes that Wright's drift across neutral ridges has a possible analogue in coalition traversal across parity regions on the coordination manifold, where multiple configurations are structurally equivalent from the relevant agents' perspectives. Whether this is a formal analogue or a loose metaphor is itself an open question within the programme.
