# Research Proposal: Coordination Across Difference: Post-Ideological Frameworks for Existential Governance

**Applicant**: Samuel Norton
**Degree**: DPhil in Philosophy, University of Oxford

## The Problem

Current political systems evolved for industrial scarcity and nation-state competition. Contemporary conditions demand global cooperation on existential interdependence (climate, biosecurity, transformative AI, economic systems, geopolitical relations) at speeds that outpace 19th-century institutional frameworks. Traditional left-right philosophy provides inadequate tools: stakes are existential, timescales compressed, actors multi-stakeholder (states, corporations, civil society, AI systems), and ideological heuristics break down under deep uncertainty (Knightian uncertainty). These systems exhibit characteristic failure modes: markets defer long-term risks for quarterly returns, democratic institutions succumb to capture dynamics, nation-states compete rather than cooperate, technocracy lacks legitimacy. Current approaches assume either ideological convergence (technocratic solutions) or paralytic pluralism (endless negotiation). Neither scales to existential coordination challenges. How do we coordinate collective decision-making when inherited frameworks for practical reasoning, value conflict resolution and institutional design assume conditions that no longer hold?

## Research Questions

**Central Question**: How do we build coordination frameworks that transcend divisive ideological categorisations (capitalism/socialism, liberal democracy/authoritarianism, nation-state sovereignty/multilateralism) without requiring ideological convergence? Can we derive coordination principles analogous to a compass - providing direction while allowing each actor to chart their own course? Can coordination theory address both theoretical inadequacy and practical pathologies simultaneously?

This question opens multiple lines of inquiry, including but not limited to:

1. **Practical Reason Beyond Ideological Sorting**: When facing hard choices about existential risks, how should collectives reason without defaulting to tribal heuristics or the observed failure modes of their inherited frameworks? What philosophical principles enable coordination when actors hold incommensurable values but share existential stakes?

2. **Institutional Design for Pluralistic Unity**: What architecture enables multi-stakeholder coordination that neither requires ideological homogeneity nor succumbs to the failure modes of existing systems such as regulatory capture, short-term profit maximisation or bureaucratic paralysis? Can we derive coordination mechanisms from first principles that are robust to both theoretical inadequacy and practical tendencies towards will to power and corruption?

3. **Coordination Across Human and Non-Human Actors**: As coordination increasingly involves non-human actors (AI systems, automated markets, algorithmic governance), how do we preserve meaningful human agency and democratic legitimacy while leveraging computational capabilities? How do we avoid both technocratic override of human judgment and the paralysis that comes from treating every actor as requiring equal voice?

## Methodological Approach

Interdisciplinary philosophical analysis combining:

- **Practical philosophy**: Drawing on contemporary work in practical philosophy on hard choices and practical reason (particularly value incommensurability and decision-making under uncertainty)
- **Systems theory & complexity science**: Operationalising coordination patterns across scales (individual → organisational → political)
- **Applied game theory**: Modelling multi-stakeholder coordination under incomplete information.
- **Institutional design philosophy**: Drawing on Ostrom, Buchanan, and contemporary political philosophy
- **Empirical grounding**: Case studies from AUKUS defence coordination, climate negotiations, international AI governance initiatives and economic policy coordination (e.g. pandemic response, financial crisis management).

## Why This Matters

We face existential coordination challenges - climate tipping points, biological risks, transformative AI and economic instability among others - with governance structures designed for the steam engine era. These structures exhibit predictable failure modes under contemporary conditions: market actors defer long-term existential risk management (climate adaptation, pandemic preparedness, AI safety) for quarterly returns, democratic processes cannot iterate faster than the pace of technological and environmental change, and nation-states compete on capabilities rather than coordinate on shared threats. Without new coordination theory grounded in contemporary conditions rather than 19th-century assumptions, we face either technocratic authoritarianism or paralytic gridlock.

## Expected Contribution

This research develops a rigorous framework for post-ideological coordination theory grounded in practical philosophy, providing tools for collective action that don't presuppose shared ideological commitments. It yields specific, operationalisable mechanisms for geopolitical relations, economic modelling, AI governance, climate coordination and biosecurity among other areas - testable by actual institutions across all scales with clear success criteria. Methodologically, it demonstrates how complexity science and systems theory can generate philosophically defensible coordination claims without collapsing into pure abstraction or theoretical policy analysis.

The Unity Compass framework (https://github.com/kotachisam/unity-compass) represents early-stage infrastructure for this research, demonstrating scope and commitment to public accessibility. The DPhil would provide the philosophical rigour this work requires to survive academic scrutiny while maintaining practical relevance.
