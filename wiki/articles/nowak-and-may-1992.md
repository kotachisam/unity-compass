**"Evolutionary games and spatial chaos"** is a 1992 paper by Martin A. Nowak and Robert M. May, published in *Nature* 359, 826-829. It demonstrated that placing agents on a spatial lattice transforms the outcome of the prisoner's dilemma from a single predictable equilibrium into three qualitatively distinct regimes, including deterministic chaos, without altering the game itself.[^1]

## The result

Classical evolutionary game theory predicts a single outcome for the prisoner's dilemma in a well-mixed population: universal defection. Defect strictly dominates Cooperate, so any population of cooperators is invaded and replaced.

Nowak and May retained that strict dominance but changed one thing: they placed agents on a two-dimensional lattice where each individual interacted only with its immediate neighbours. Using only two pure strategies (always-cooperate, always-defect), they found that the spatial structure alone produced three qualitatively different outcomes depending on the ratio of the temptation payoff to the reward for mutual cooperation:[^1]

1. **All-defect.** The replicator dynamics prediction holds. Defectors sweep the population.
2. **Stable coexistence.** Cooperators and defectors persist indefinitely in fixed or oscillating spatial configurations.
3. **Deterministic chaos.** Ever-changing spatial patterns that Nowak and May described as "dynamic fractals": kaleidoscopic, self-similar structures that never settle into a fixed state.

The game did not change. Defect still strictly dominated. The spatial structure alone determined which outcome obtained. The transition between regimes was governed by payoff parameter values, but the existence of multiple regimes was a consequence of spatial topology, not strategic innovation.

## The authors

**Martin A. Nowak** (born 1965) is an Austrian-born mathematical biologist. He studied biochemistry and mathematics at the University of Vienna before completing his doctorate with Karl Sigmund, one of the founders of evolutionary game theory. After holding positions at the University of Oxford, where he worked with May, he moved to the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton and then to Harvard University, where he became Professor of Mathematics and Biology and directed the Program for Evolutionary Dynamics. His research spans evolutionary dynamics, cooperation theory and mathematical oncology. Major works include *Evolutionary Dynamics: Exploring the Equations of Life* (Harvard UP, 2006) and *SuperCooperators* (Free Press, 2011).[^2]

**Robert McCredie May, Baron May of Oxford** (1936-2020) was an Australian-British theoretical physicist and ecologist. Born in Sydney, he earned his doctorate in theoretical physics from the University of Sydney before pivoting to mathematical ecology. He held the Royal Society Research Professorship at the University of Oxford and later served as Chief Scientific Adviser to the UK Government (1995-2000) and President of the Royal Society (2000-2005). May's earlier work on chaos in ecological populations, particularly his landmark 1976 paper "Simple mathematical models with very complicated dynamics" in *Nature*, established him as a pioneer of chaos theory in biology. He was appointed to the Order of Merit in 2002 and received a life peerage as Baron May of Oxford. His collaboration with Nowak brought the spatial dynamics tradition he had pioneered in ecology into evolutionary game theory.[^3][^4]

## Significance for evolutionary game theory

Before Nowak and May, the dominant approach to evolutionary game theory operated on the assumption of well-mixed populations: any agent could interact with any other, and fitness was calculated against the population average. The replicator dynamics of Taylor and Jonker (1978) formalised this approach. Under well-mixing, the prisoner's dilemma has a single stable equilibrium at all-defect.[^5]

Nowak and May's result broke that assumption open. By showing that spatial structure alone could sustain cooperation in a game where cooperation should be eliminated, they established that the topology of interaction is not a background condition but an active variable. The paper launched the field of spatial evolutionary game theory and prompted extensive investigation into how network structure affects the evolution of cooperation.[^1]

Subsequent work extended the basic result. Nowak and May themselves explored stochastic variants. Szabó and Fath (2007) surveyed two decades of evolutionary games on graphs. Santos and Pacheco (2005) showed that scale-free networks, where some nodes have many more connections than others, promote cooperation even more strongly than regular lattices. The core insight, that structure does independent work, has proved robust across network types, game structures and update rules.[^6][^7]

J. McKenzie Alexander's treatment in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy highlights Nowak and May as a pivotal result, noting that in spatial models "outcomes depend on specific payoff values rather than just strategic structure" and that cooperation persists "even when Defect strictly dominates."[^5]

## Role in the investigation

The investigation treats Nowak and May (1992) as the strongest existing evidence for its central claim: that structural features of interdependence can do coordination work independent of the strategic content of the interaction.

### Structure determining outcomes

The paper's core finding maps directly onto the investigation's thesis. In the standard prisoner's dilemma, the outcome is determined by the payoff structure. In the spatial prisoner's dilemma, the outcome is determined by the interaction structure. The game is identical; the structure changes; the result changes. This is precisely the relationship the investigation posits between structural interdependence and coordination: that structural position can ground coordination even when the strategic analysis (the "game") provides no basis for it.[^5]

The investigation frames the implication as: "Nowak and May demonstrated that structure matters even when the game is fixed. The investigation asks what happens when structure is all you have."[^5]

### The three-body-problem parallel

The result is structurally analogous to the three-body problem in physics. Two-body gravitational systems are analytically solvable. Three bodies produce chaos. In Nowak and May: a well-mixed population is solvable (all-defect). Spatial structure produces chaos. Simple deterministic rules, spatial interaction and chaotic outcomes that cannot be predicted from the rules alone. The mathematical territory the thesis enters may require similar tools: dynamical systems theory, spatial and network topology, sensitivity to initial conditions.[^5]

### The boundary of the result

Nowak and May's spatial prisoner's dilemma still requires a constructible payoff structure. The agents play a defined game with numerical payoffs. The spatial structure transforms the outcome of that game, but the game must be specifiable in the first place. The investigation operates in the regime where even the payoff structure is absent: where the parties hold genuinely incommensurable values and the game cannot be constructed. The question becomes whether structural features (shared risk ordering, constraint hardness, entanglement density) can do the work that both payoff structure and spatial structure do in Nowak and May's model.[^5]
