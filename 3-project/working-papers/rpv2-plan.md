# PhD Research Proposal Skeleton - LSE MPhil/PhD

## How to Use This Document

**Do not write the proposal from this scaffold.**

1. Print it or read it once
2. Close it
3. Write a messy, unstructured, first-person account of what you actually believe about coordination, why it matters to you, and what you think is wrong with how everyone else thinks about it. Write it like you'd explain it to Cox over a beer. Write it angry, write it excited, write it uneven.
4. Then open this scaffold and use the checklist below to verify your draft hits what it needs to hit
5. Restructure your raw thinking into this skeleton's shape, but let the prose stay yours

The scaffold is the bones. Your voice is the muscle. Don't let the bones write the muscle for you.

## Voice and Authenticity

The old proposal was predominantly AI-drafted and reads like it. Specific tells to eliminate:

- **Balanced sentence templates**: the old version used "X addresses Y but assumes Z" for every thinker. Real writing has stronger opinions about some things than others. Have uneven conviction.
- **Defensive comprehensiveness**: mentioning everything so no reviewer can say "you missed X." Reviewers don't think that way. They think "does this person know what they're actually doing?" Depth over coverage.
- **Rhetorical escalation**: every section building to a grand claim. "Not merely institutional, but civilisational." Academics find this grating. Let the ideas carry weight without amplification.
- **Even weighting**: giving every concept, thinker, and domain equal space. Real thinking is lumpy - you know some areas better than others, some ideas excite you more. Let that show.
- **Hedged confidence**: "I theorise that...", "this has the potential to be..." Either commit to a claim and defend it, or don't make it.

The test: if a paragraph could have been written about any coordination theory proposal by anyone, it's too generic. Every sentence should reflect a specific choice that reveals how *you* think about this problem.

**Old proposal pitfalls to avoid**:

## 3. Central Research Question (short, sharp)

**If value convergence is sufficient but not necessary for coordination, what other structural features of interdependence can ground stable collective action among agents with incommensurable values, and under what formal conditions?**

The load-bearing philosophical distinction: convergence is *sufficient* but not *necessary* for coordination. This is the claim that distinguishes the thesis from adjacent work. Most coordination theory either assumes convergence (shared values, aligned incentives) or tries to produce it (mechanism design, deliberation). This thesis argues there's a third path: coordination grounded in structural features of the interdependence itself, without requiring or producing agreement on values.

Sub-questions (max 3):

1. **Taxonomy**: What types of structural interdependence (shared risk, shared constraints, network effects, procedural commitments) can substitute for value convergence in grounding coordination? Are these formally reducible to a common account, or does each require distinct mechanisms?
2. **Stability**: Under what conditions do coordination equilibria grounded in structural interdependence resist defection, capture, and decay? (Alexander territory - evolutionary stability of norms without shared values)
3. **Rationality**: What does rational coordination require of agents who share structural interdependence but not values? What decision-theoretic framework applies under severe uncertainty and value incommensurability? (Bradley territory - connects to his work with Spiekermann on cohesion under severe uncertainty)

**Old proposal pitfalls to avoid**:

- The old central question spanned "families and communities through organisations and institutions to nations and planetary governance" - literally every scale of human organisation. No PhD answers that.
- The compass metaphor appeared in the research question itself. Metaphors don't belong in research questions. Formal claims do. A reviewer reads a metaphor in a research question and thinks: "this person has a metaphor, not a theory."
- "Can coordination theory address both theoretical inadequacy and practical pathologies simultaneously?" was two PhDs in one sentence.
- The three old sub-questions each contained at least three distinct research areas (nine total across three questions). The new sub-questions each map to one supervisor's expertise and one analytical task.

- **Game theory (Hurwicz, Maskin)**: mechanism design, but assumes commensurable payoff structures

- **Alexander's own work**: evolutionary emergence of norms, but [read his papers - identify what he hasn't addressed that you would. His computational philosophy angle may offer tools you can extend. Be specific about which models and what they don't yet capture about coordination under value incommensurability]
- **Bradley's own work**: decision under uncertainty, but [read his papers - identify the extension point. **Old proposal pitfalls to avoid**:

- Each thinker got a one-sentence dismissal. That's not a gap analysis, it's a drive-by. Engage with why their approaches are strong before explaining where they fall short.
- Chang got one sentence despite being the proposed supervisor. This signalled surface-level engagement with the person the proposal claimed to build on. Don't repeat this with Alexander and Bradley - if they're your target supervisors, demonstrate the deepest engagement with their work.
- The old gap was defined as requiring synthesis across FOUR areas under FOUR conditions. That's a wish list, not a research gap. Committees are rightly suspicious of "I'll do the synthesis nobody else has done" because usually the reason nobody has done it is that the fields are incommensurable, not that everyone lacked the vision.
- The "none synthesise across..." framing implies all prior work was incomplete because it didn't attempt your specific combination. That's arrogant. Better framing: "each framework illuminates X, but the question of Y remains formally unaddressed because it requires tools from both evolutionary game theory and decision theory under incommensurability."

## Appendix: Formal Sketch of the Core Claim

Include a short note (can be a paragraph or a boxed aside) in or after the methodology section that gestures at the formal shape of the argument. Not a full model - a sketch that shows you can think formally, not just talk about formalism. Something along these lines, rewritten in your voice:

> Consider n agents with preference orderings that are genuinely incomparable (in Chang's sense - not merely unranked but positively on a par). Standard coordination games require a common payoff matrix. The claim is that a shared structure of interdependence - definable as a partial ordering over catastrophic outcomes that all agents can endorse without agreeing on the ranking of positive outcomes - provides a sufficient basis for coordination equilibria. The MPhil year would formalise this intuition using tools from Alexander's evolutionary stability analysis and Bradley's framework for decision under severe uncertainty.

That's three sentences and it tells the committee you're not just saying "I'll use formal methods" - you can actually gesture at what the formalism looks like. This separates you from every applicant who promises rigour without demonstrating capacity for it.
