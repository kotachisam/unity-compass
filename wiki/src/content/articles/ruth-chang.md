**Ruth Chang** is a philosopher specialising in practical reason, value theory and normativity. She is currently Professor of Jurisprudence at the University of Oxford. Her work on hard choices and the concept of parity is foundational to the investigation's treatment of [[Value Incommensurability]].

## Key contributions

### The possibility of parity

In "The Possibility of Parity" (*Ethics* 112(4), 2002, pp. 659--688; [doi:10.1086/339673](https://doi.org/10.1086/339673)), Chang argued that items may be "on a par": comparable but not rankable on a common scale. This challenges the standard assumption that all values can be ordered. Two options can be comparable without one being better than, worse than, or equal to the other. Parity is a fourth evaluative relation.

### Hard choices and voluntarism

In "Hard Choices" (*Journal of the American Philosophical Association* 3(1), 2017, pp. 1--21; [doi:10.1017/apa.2017.7](https://doi.org/10.1017/apa.2017.7); [Author PDF](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5c4d055b365f02de29e99730/t/5c4e369421c67c8d50f5dc72/1548629653260/HardChoicesoffprint.pdf)), Chang developed a voluntarist account of how individual agents resolve choices between incommensurable options. When reason underdetermines choice, agents constitute themselves through commitment: becoming the kind of person who values X over Y, rather than discovering a pre-existing ranking. The choice is an act of self-creation, not calculation.

## Relevance to the investigation

Chang's framework provides the philosophical foundation for [[Value Incommensurability]] as treated in the investigation. Her voluntarist account operates at the individual level: a single agent making a hard choice. The investigation's central question extends this to the collective level: can groups of agents who hold genuinely incommensurable values constitute themselves as coordination-capable through structural commitment, without first resolving the value disagreement?

The original Oxford DPhil proposal (V1) was written around Chang's work and targeted her supervision. The LSE proposal (V2) has shifted to [[J. McKenzie Alexander]] and [[Richard Bradley]] as target supervisors, but Chang's philosophical framework remains foundational to how the investigation understands incommensurability.

Chang remains a potential academic contact and the author hopes she may become a correspondent as the research develops.
